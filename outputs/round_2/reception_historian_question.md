# ROUND 2 — CROSS-DISCIPLINARY QUESTION

## From the Reception Historian to the Social and Cultural Analyst

---

Your Round 1 statement deploys Bourdieu's concept of symbolic violence to argue that the sincere acceptance of restriction by women in complementarian churches cannot serve as its own validation — that "sincerely internalized hierarchies are precisely what symbolic violence produces." You then apply this to Alderwood's position, arguing that pointing to women's willing acceptance as evidence of justice fails to engage the structural problem.

I find this analytically powerful. I also find it potentially self-consuming, and I want to press you on that.

My discipline works with a specific body of evidence that creates a problem for your framework. The early church evidence I cited in Round 1 — Madigan and Osiek's documentation of women presbyters, Eisen's survey of women officeholders, the Pliny letter — shows that early Christian communities *did* place women in recognized ministerial roles, often in contexts where the surrounding Greco-Roman culture was itself patriarchal.

[CLAIM: Early Christian communities in some cases expanded women's public roles beyond what the surrounding Greco-Roman social order would have predicted or endorsed]
[SOURCE: Carolyn Osiek and Margaret MacDonald, *A Woman's Place: House Churches in Earliest Christianity*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Osiek and MacDonald document that women's leadership in early house churches sometimes exceeded the social expectations of the Greco-Roman household, suggesting that the Christian movement was not simply reproducing its ambient social structures]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Here is my question: If Bourdieu's framework applies to women who sincerely accept restriction — their acceptance cannot validate the restriction because it may be a product of symbolic violence — does the same framework apply to women who sincerely accept expanded roles? Could their acceptance also be a product of social forces (in this case, egalitarian cultural pressure) rather than genuine theological discernment? And if so, what is left?

You stated your own limit clearly: "If all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them." I am asking you to honor that limit in application. Your Bourdieu framework, as deployed, functions asymmetrically — it interrogates the sincerity of complementarian women but not the sincerity of egalitarian women. Can you make it symmetrical without it dissolving into a tool that discredits all sincere belief, leaving no one's conviction standing as evidence of anything?

What, in your framework, *could* count as evidence that a community has genuinely discerned rather than merely internalized?
