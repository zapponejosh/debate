## SUBSTANTIVE RESPONSE

Your question is the hardest one my discipline faces, and I will not pretend it has a clean answer.

Yes — the consistency test creates a pastoral obligation. When a community enforces a restriction on the basis of a hermeneutical principle it does not actually apply consistently, it owes the people affected by that restriction an honest account of what *is* doing the work. To enforce a restriction while unable to name its operative basis is not faithfulness to Scripture. It is faithfulness to a conclusion whose grounds have not survived scrutiny.

But I must be equally honest about what this does *not* mean. Hermeneutical uncertainty does not automatically resolve in favor of removing restriction. A community that says "we cannot yet articulate a fully consistent principle, but the weight of the canonical evidence and our best theological judgment inclines us toward maintaining this practice while we do the work" is making a defensible provisional judgment — *if* it is genuinely doing the work, and *if* it names the provisionality honestly to the people it affects.

What is not defensible: enforcing a restriction with the confidence of settled conviction while the hermeneutical basis remains undefended. That is the gap between Alderwood's tone — which presents the position as what Scripture teaches — and its actual hermeneutical situation, which the consistency test has shown to be unresolved.

[CLAIM: Nicholas Wolterstorff argues that communities bear moral obligations in how they hold and apply interpretive judgments, particularly when those judgments impose costs on members of the community]
[SOURCE: Nicholas Wolterstorff, *Divine Discourse*, Cambridge University Press, 1995]
[ARGUMENT: Wolterstorff's account of divine discourse through Scripture implies that interpretive responsibility includes accountability for the pastoral consequences of interpretive decisions]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

So: honest uncertainty requires naming the uncertainty *to the woman in the room*. It requires saying "our hermeneutical basis is less settled than our practice implies, and we owe you that honesty." Whether the restriction stands or falls is not mine to determine. That it must be held with disclosed provisionality rather than false confidence — that *is* mine to say.
