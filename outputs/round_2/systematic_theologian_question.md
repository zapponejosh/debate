# ROUND 2 — CROSS-DISCIPLINARY QUESTION

## From: The Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian
## To: The Pastoral Theologian

---

You made a claim in Round 1 that I want to take with full seriousness and then press hard, because it raises a question my discipline cannot ignore.

You argued, drawing on Smith, that when a congregation gathers and the person holding final doctrinal authority is always male, "that congregation is being formed to associate ultimate spiritual authority with maleness" — regardless of what the doctrinal statement says. You then asked: "What are your children learning about who God is and who gets to speak for God?"

I accept the formation claim as genuine. Practices do teach. But here is my question, and it is not rhetorical:

**What doctrine of the church would you need to hold for pastoral fruitfulness or harm to function as a criterion that can revise ecclesial structure — and is that doctrine one you are prepared to defend?**

Let me make the problem precise. My discipline identifies at least two ecclesiological frameworks in which your formation argument would carry different weight:

In Moltmann's pneumatological ecclesiology, the church's structure is governed by the Spirit's gifts and the eschatological horizon of the new creation. On that account, formation that contradicts the Spirit's gifting is itself evidence of structural failure, and your argument has genuine doctrinal force.

[CLAIM: Moltmann argues that the church's structure must be understood eschatologically — as a proleptic sign of the Kingdom — and that its internal relations should anticipate the freedom of the new creation rather than reproduce the hierarchies of the present age]
[SOURCE: Jürgen Moltmann, *The Church in the Power of the Spirit*, Fortress, 1977]
[ARGUMENT: Church structure is normed by eschatological freedom, not inherited order]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

But in an ecclesiology where the church's structure is normed by apostolic institution — where the pattern laid down in the New Testament has binding authority regardless of subsequent pastoral outcomes — your formation argument is real pastoral data but cannot revise the structure. It can only call for better pastoral care *within* the structure.

Alderwood appears to hold something like the second ecclesiology. You appear to require something like the first for your argument to do the doctrinal work you want it to do.

So my question is direct: Are you making a pastoral observation that any ecclesiology must take seriously but that cannot by itself revise ecclesial structure? Or are you making an ecclesiological argument — that harmful formation is evidence of structural failure — that requires a specific doctrine of the church to sustain? If the latter, what is that doctrine, and how do you defend it against the charge that it makes pastoral outcomes sovereign over apostolic instruction?

I ask because the answer determines whether your discipline is *supplementing* mine or *competing* with it — and I need to know which before Round 3.
