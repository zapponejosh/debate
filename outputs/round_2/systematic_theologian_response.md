You are right, and the concession costs me something.

Yes — my three models are socially embedded. The ontological model carries late Roman imperial DNA. The functional model carries Protestant vocational culture. The relational model carries democratic congregationalist assumptions. I presented them as analytical tools; you have shown they are also historical artifacts. I accept the critique.

But I want to resist the conclusion that social embeddedness is fatal. Here is why.

Your Bourdieu citation establishes that taxonomies naturalize social conditions. Granted. But Bourdieu's own taxonomy — including "symbolic violence" — is itself socially produced, emerging from twentieth-century French academic culture. If social embeddedness delegitimizes frameworks, the delegitimizing framework falls too. You acknowledged this limit in your opening: "If all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them." I am holding you to that.

What systematic theology offers is not a view from nowhere but a method of *testing* frameworks against their claimed theological warrants. The representational model claims to ground eldership in the elder's function of representing divine authority. The test is whether that theological warrant actually requires maleness — and here Peeler's work is decisive:

[CLAIM: Amy Peeler argues that the Son's representative role is grounded in his humanity, not his maleness, and that Scripture's use of masculine divine language does not establish maleness as necessary for representing God]
[SOURCE: Amy Peeler, *Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022]
[ARGUMENT: If representation is grounded in shared humanity with the incarnate Christ rather than shared maleness, the representational model does not yield the gendered conclusion it has historically produced]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

So my answer is this: systematic theology distinguishes theological truth from smuggled social assumption by testing whether the conclusion follows from the stated warrant *on the warrant's own terms*. The representational model claims the elder represents God. Alderwood itself affirms the *imago Dei* requires both sexes. If representation requires imaging God, and imaging God requires male-and-female-together, then the model's own logic undermines the restriction — and *that* is how you identify the smuggled assumption. Not by genealogy alone, but by internal incoherence exposed when the framework is pressed to its own conclusions.

You have sharpened my argument. The representational model is not only undefended by Alderwood — it may be socially pre-loaded to deliver a gendered conclusion its theological warrants cannot sustain.
