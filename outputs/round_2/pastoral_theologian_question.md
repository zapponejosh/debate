# ROUND 2 — CROSS-DISCIPLINARY QUESTION
## From the Pastoral Theologian to the Hermeneutician

---

You have demonstrated, with precision I find compelling, that Alderwood's stated hermeneutical principle — creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms — does not survive the consistency test with 1 Corinthians 11. You have shown that "something other than creation-order grounding is doing the real hermeneutical work in both cases." And you have honestly stated that your discipline "does not tell you whether women should be elders" but rather "what kind of argument you need to have before you can answer that question responsibly."

I accept all of that. And now I need to press you on what it requires in practice.

A pastor sits across from a woman who has been told by three mentors that she is called to eldership. She has preached to the congregation. She leads a staff team. She holds the title "pastor." She is weeping — not from weakness, but from the accumulated weight of being told her gifts are real but her vocation has a ceiling. The elder board meets next month to revisit the policy. They are not scholars. They cannot wait for the hermeneutical question to be "closed by hermeneutics alone," as you rightly note it cannot be.

[CLAIM: The experience of women who believe themselves called to pastoral or elder roles, and who are told that calling cannot be fulfilled, constitutes a distinct pastoral problem that cannot be resolved by redirecting them to other forms of ministry]
[SOURCE: Ruth Tucker, *Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*, Zondervan, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Tucker argues that calling has a vocational shape that cannot be redirected without pastoral cost that must be owned]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

You identified four requirements for a responsible argument: a consistent account of transcultural criteria, a defended hermeneutical framework, honesty about pre-understanding, and recognition that hermeneutics alone cannot close the question. My question is this:

**When the consistency test reveals that the stated hermeneutical principle is not actually operative — when, as you put it, "something else is doing the work" — does that finding create a *pastoral* obligation to name that something else before enforcing the restriction? Or can a church honestly maintain a restriction whose hermeneutical basis its own practice has undermined, while it continues to search for the principled account you say is needed?**

What does honest hermeneutical uncertainty *require* of a community that must act now — not eventually, but this month?
