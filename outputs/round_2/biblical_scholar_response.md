You are right, and I owe this debate a direct answer.

Yes — canonical breadth is functioning as a tiebreaker, and I should have named it. Here is my defense, and its limits.

When a hapax legomenon is genuinely ambiguous, the exegete has three options: leave it unresolved, import external lexical data to tip the scale, or read the ambiguous term within the communicative act of the broader canon. I chose the third. That is a method, and it needs controls.

The control I apply is this: canonical adjudication must privilege *what texts actually attest* over *what patterns the interpreter constructs*. The complementarian reads a "consistent pattern of male headship" — but that pattern requires treating Deborah, Huldah, Junia, Phoebe, and Priscilla as exceptions to a rule the texts themselves never articulate as a rule. The canon does not say "leadership belongs to men with these exceptions." It presents women exercising prophetic, judicial, teaching, and apostolic functions *without apology or qualification* — and then presents restriction texts whose scope is lexically contested.

But here is the honest limit: this method *does* carry the risk you identify. A canonical tiebreaker can ratify prior commitments if the interpreter selects which canonical data counts as "the pattern." My control — privilege what texts attest over constructed patterns — is a principle, not a guarantee. It requires the complementarian's counter-canon to be engaged, not dismissed. Schreiner's reading of the same canon as displaying consistent male headship is a serious reading. I find it requires more silence-about-exceptions than my method permits, but I cannot claim my canonical judgment is neutral.

What I can claim: my method is *more* constrained than a bare lexical tiebreak, because canonical attestation is public and arguable. Whether it is sufficiently constrained — that is your question to adjudicate, not mine.
