# ROUND 2 — CROSS-DISCIPLINARY QUESTION
## Social and Cultural Analyst → Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian

---

Your Round 1 statement identified three models of eldership — ontological, functional, relational — and argued that Alderwood operates with a functional account while its creation-order argument implies a representational one. You then argued this unacknowledged tension is "the central ecclesiological problem Alderwood has not solved." I found this genuinely illuminating.

But I want to press on the framework itself. You present these three models as though they are neutral analytical categories available for selection on theological merit. My discipline asks: are they? Each model emerged from a specific social world. The ontological model developed within the late Roman imperial administrative structure, where office-holding *was* a change in social status. The functional model tracks with post-Reformation Protestant cultures that valorized competence and vocation. The relational model maps onto congregationalist and democratic social forms.

[CLAIM: Pierre Bourdieu argues that intellectual taxonomies presented as neutral analytical frameworks often naturalize the social conditions of their production, making historically contingent categories appear as timeless options]
[SOURCE: Pierre Bourdieu, *Masculine Domination*, Stanford University Press, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: The categories through which we analyze power — including the categories of systematic theology — are themselves products of the social worlds that generated them, not neutral instruments applied from outside those worlds]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

My question is specific: You argued that Alderwood's position requires a representational account of eldership it has not defended. But the representational model itself — the idea that a leader *represents* divine authority to a community — carries a social history. It emerged in contexts where representation was gendered, where the paterfamilias represented the household to the polis, where male bodies were the default bearers of public authority.

[CLAIM: David deSilva documents that in the first-century Mediterranean honor/shame system, public representation of a household or community was a gendered activity, with male heads of household serving as the normative representatives in civic and religious contexts]
[SOURCE: David deSilva, *Honor, Patronage, Kinship and Purity*, IVP Academic, 2000]
[ARGUMENT: The concept of "representation" in public authority was not gender-neutral in the social world where early Christian ecclesiology took shape]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

So here is my question: **When you identify the representational model as what Alderwood's argument requires, have you considered that the model itself may carry gendered assumptions from its social origins — assumptions that make the conclusion (male-only eldership) feel natural precisely because the framework was built in a world where representation *was* male? And if so, how does systematic theology distinguish between a theological truth discovered through a socially embedded framework and a social assumption smuggled in by that framework?**

This is not a rhetorical question. I genuinely want to know how your discipline handles this, because the answer determines whether the ecclesiological analysis you offered in Round 1 is itself subject to the same genealogical scrutiny you applied to Alderwood.
