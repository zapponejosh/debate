This is an excellent question, and it identifies a genuine methodological tension in my discipline that I must answer honestly rather than evade.

The short answer: reception history treats the obscuring as evidence about *both* — but asymmetrically.

The genealogy of suppression is at least as significant as the genealogy of construction, and in the Junia case, more so. Here is why. When a reading is *constructed* — when it emerges at a particular moment — reception history asks what produced it. The answer may be exegetical insight, cultural pressure, or both. The emergence alone does not discredit it. But when a reading is *suppressed* — when an attested earlier reading is replaced by one without independent textual support — reception history must ask what force was strong enough to override the documentary evidence. In Junia's case, the force was not textual discovery. It was theological discomfort with the implication.

[CLAIM: Eldon Jay Epp documents that the masculinization of Junia to Junias in critical editions began with Aegidius of Rome in the thirteenth century and was cemented in modern critical texts, despite the unanimous witness of patristic commentators who recognized the name as female]
[SOURCE: Eldon Jay Epp, *Junia: The First Woman Apostle*, Fortress, 2005]
[ARGUMENT: The shift from female to male reading had no manuscript basis and can be traced to interpretive pressure rather than textual evidence]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is evidence about the interpreters' commitments — but it is *also* evidence about the text's meaning, because it demonstrates that the reading requiring suppression was the one the earliest competent readers found natural. When the older reading is the one that required no special explanation and the later reading is the one that required invention of an unattested name, reception history does assign differential weight. Not because older is automatically better, but because the *mechanism* of the change is identifiable and non-exegetical.

However — and this is where I must honor my own stated limits — this finding does not settle what Junia's apostleship *means* for ecclesiology. Chrysostom recognized her and still maintained restriction. That datum is genuinely inconvenient for any narrative that treats the suppression as the whole story. The recovery of Junia tells us the text says what the earliest readers said it says. It does not tell us what ecclesial conclusion follows. That requires your discipline and the systematician's.

The genealogy of suppression is powerful evidence that a reading was *changed* for non-textual reasons. It is not, by itself, evidence that the suppressed reading resolves the broader question about women's authority. I hold both.
