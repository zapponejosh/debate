# ROUND 2 — CROSS-DISCIPLINARY QUESTION
## From the Hermeneutician to the Biblical Scholar

---

You made a move in your Round 1 statement that I want to press on, because it is the most important methodological moment in your paper and you did not defend it.

You presented the *authenteō* dispute with admirable honesty — Payne's negative reading, Wolters' neutral reading, both with "serious lexical warrant." You then concluded: "The strongest exegetical case for restriction operates at the level of a specific authoritative-teaching-and-adjudicating function — something like what Alderwood calls the elder's role — rather than all leadership or all public speech."

My question is about the gap between those two statements. You acknowledged genuine lexical ambiguity. You then reached a directional conclusion. Something bridged that gap, and it was not lexical evidence — because the lexical evidence, by your own account, does not resolve.

Here is what I think happened, and I want you to tell me if I am wrong. You brought a *canonical judgment* — the weight of Romans 16, Acts 2, Judges 4–5, the breadth of women's attested ministry — and used it to adjudicate the ambiguous lexical evidence in 1 Timothy 2:12. The canonical picture of women in prophetic, teaching, and leadership roles made the narrower reading of *authenteō* more plausible to you than the broader one. The lexical data permitted both readings; the canonical context tipped the scale.

If that is what you did, I need you to name it as a hermeneutical method and defend it. Because it is one.

[CLAIM: Kevin Vanhoozer argues that the text's communicative act, anchored in its canonical context, rather than bare lexical analysis, is what grounds determinate meaning]
[SOURCE: Kevin Vanhoozer, *Is There a Meaning in This Text?*, Zondervan, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Vanhoozer's speech-act approach holds that meaning is located in the illocutionary act of the text within its canonical setting, not in isolated word studies — which would support using canonical context to adjudicate lexical ambiguity, but only if that move is acknowledged and defended]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Specifically: Is canonical breadth functioning as your hermeneutical tiebreaker? If so, what controls prevent that canonical judgment from simply ratifying the interpreter's prior theological commitments? The complementarian reads the same canon and finds a consistent pattern of male headship. You read it and find women in prophetic and leadership roles that create "exegetical pressure" on restriction. The canon is the same. The readings diverge. What in your method — not your conclusions, your *method* — adjudicates?

I am not asking this rhetorically. I need your answer before I can evaluate whether the consistency test I have applied to Alderwood applies equally to the egalitarian hermeneutic.
