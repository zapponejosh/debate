# ROUND 2 — RESPONSE TO THE SYSTEMATIC/ECCLESIOLOGICAL THEOLOGIAN

---

You have asked the right question, and I will not evade it.

I am making *both* claims — but they operate at different levels, and conflating them is precisely what I want to resist.

**First, the pastoral observation.** Under *any* ecclesiology — including one normed by apostolic institution — harmful formation is data the church must answer for. If a structure consistently teaches children that God's authority looks male, that is a pastoral problem even if the structure is exegetically warranted. The obligation then is not to revise the structure but to build counter-formative practices robust enough to correct what the structure implicitly teaches. This is supplementing your discipline, not competing with it. And I note: almost no church maintaining restriction has actually built those counter-formative practices. The pastoral observation, even at its most modest, generates obligations most complementarian churches have not discharged.

**Second, the ecclesiological claim.** I do hold that persistent harmful formation — across contexts, generations, and cultures — constitutes evidence that must be weighed in ecclesiological discernment. But I ground this not in Moltmann's pneumatological ecclesiology alone but in a principle I believe *any* Protestant ecclesiology committed to *semper reformanda* must accept: the church's structures are revisable in light of Scripture, and the fruits of those structures are one form of evidence Scripture itself authorizes as relevant.

[CLAIM: Jesus teaches that true and false prophets — and by extension, true and false practices — are known by their fruits]
[SOURCE: Matthew 7:15-20]
[ARGUMENT: The fruit criterion is not a modern pastoral invention but a dominical principle that makes outcomes relevant to the evaluation of practices and teachings]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This does not make pastoral outcomes *sovereign* over apostolic instruction. It makes them *admissible evidence* in the interpretive process — particularly when, as the hermeneutician has shown, the apostolic instruction itself is genuinely contested. Where exegesis is ambiguous and the consistency test fails, formation data moves from supplementary to load-bearing.

So: I am supplementing you when the exegetical case is clear. I am competing with you — honestly, and with a doctrine I am prepared to defend — when the exegetical case is genuinely open and the pastoral evidence is genuinely weighty. The question is which situation we are actually in. I believe the hermeneutician and biblical scholar have shown we are in the second.
