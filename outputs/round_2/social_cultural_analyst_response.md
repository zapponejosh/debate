This is the sharpest question anyone has put to me, and it deserves a direct answer rather than evasion.

You are right that I deployed Bourdieu asymmetrically. That is a genuine methodological failure, and I correct it now: symbolic violence as a framework applies to *all* sincerely held positions that emerge within structures of social power. Women who embrace egalitarianism under cultural pressure from progressive environments are equally subject to the sociological observation that sincerity does not self-validate. Bourdieu's framework is symmetrical or it is nothing.

So what is left? Three things.

First, Bourdieu does not destroy the concept of genuine discernment — he destroys the concept of *unexamined* sincerity as evidence. A community that has examined the social forces shaping its convictions holds those convictions differently from one that has not. The test is reflexivity, not the conclusion reached.

Second, your own evidence provides a criterion. When early Christian communities expanded women's roles *against* the dominant honor/shame structure of the Greco-Roman world — as Osiek and MacDonald document — that expansion carried social *cost*, not social reward. Bourdieu's framework distinguishes between convictions that align with ambient power structures (which require scrutiny for internalization) and convictions that resist them (which carry a different evidentiary weight, because they are costly rather than rewarding to hold).

Third, the framework points toward *process* criteria rather than *outcome* criteria for genuine discernment: Has the community heard voices with standing to challenge the dominant reading? Has it examined its own genealogy? Has it allowed the costly testimony — the woman weeping in the pastoral theologian's account — to function as evidence rather than anomaly?

Symbolic violence is not a verdict. It is a diagnostic that asks: *whose comfort does this arrangement serve, and has that question been asked aloud?*
