# RECEPTION HISTORIAN — ROUND 4 CLOSING ARGUMENT

---

## Statement of Disciplinary Limits

Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time in response to forces that are not purely exegetical. It cannot show that a reading is therefore wrong. My discipline cannot establish what the texts mean — that is the biblical scholar's work. It cannot adjudicate whether the history of a reading counts as evidence against its validity — that is the hermeneutician's work. What I can do is put the current reading in its actual historical context and ask: do you know where this reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

---

## I. What My Discipline's Findings Require of the Church

Three findings from this debate are not optional for any honest church.

**First**, the church must know the history of its own reading. Alderwood's position is presented as what the Bible teaches. This debate has demonstrated — across every discipline — that it is what the Bible has been made to teach by a specific interpretive tradition with a traceable genealogy. The Vulgate rendered *authenteō* as *dominari* for fifteen centuries. Junia was female in every patristic source and was masculinized without textual warrant beginning in the thirteenth century. The creation-order argument was applied to marriage and domestic life by Augustine, not to church office. The specific configuration Alderwood holds — elder restriction grounded in Genesis 2 sequence and 1 Timothy 2:12 read as prohibiting women from the elder-specific function of authoritative teaching — crystallized in the late twentieth century.

[CLAIM: The specific application of Genesis 2 creation-order arguments to church office restriction, as distinct from marital ordering, is a distinctively modern evangelical development]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: The move from domestic application (patristic) to ecclesial application (modern complementarian) is a traceable historical shift, not a continuous tradition]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This does not make the reading wrong. It means a church that presents this reading as simply what Scripture teaches — without disclosing its history — is not being fully honest with its own people. **What is required**: every church holding this position must tell its people where the reading came from. Not as an apology, but as intellectual honesty.

**Second**, the church must reckon with the recognition-and-containment pattern. This is my most important finding across four rounds. The patristic interpreters — Chrysostom, Origen, Jerome — *knew* about Phoebe, Junia, Priscilla, Deborah, Huldah. They recognized these women's roles. They chose not to extend them as precedents.

[CLAIM: The patristic pattern across Romans 16 is one of recognition without extension — women's ministerial roles were acknowledged but not allowed to function as precedents for ecclesial practice]
[SOURCE: Kevin Madigan and Carolyn Osiek, *Ordained Women in the Early Church*, Johns Hopkins University Press, 2005]
[ARGUMENT: This pattern was characteristic of patristic reception and was driven by interpretive choices about what would count as normative, not by ignorance of the evidence]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**What is required**: a church must name that this containment happened, explain why it considers the containment justified, or explain why it does not. Silence on this point is not neutrality — it is inheritance without examination.

**Third**, the gap between official doctrine and actual practice is itself theological data. Medieval abbesses exercised quasi-episcopal authority. Nineteenth-century missionary women performed apostolic functions their home churches denied them. Today, Alderwood permits women to preach on Sunday mornings, teach men in Bible studies, and hold the title "pastor" — while restricting the elder office. The gap between what women *do* and what they are *called* has been a constant across two millennia. **What is required**: the church must stop treating this gap as an administrative detail. It is evidence that the restriction has never been applied with the consistency its stated theological basis would demand.

## II. Where Another Discipline Genuinely Changed My Thinking

The Systematic Theologian's *imago Dei* argument challenged me in a way I did not anticipate. I came to this debate equipped to trace genealogies — to show *where* readings came from. The Systematician showed that Alderwood's own theological affirmation — "the fullness of the way that humanity images God can only be seen in the partnership of male and female together" — creates an internal problem I had not framed. If the elder represents divine authority to the community, and divine image requires male-and-female-together, then an all-male eldership represents something less than the fullness of what it claims to represent.

Reception history can trace how the *imago Dei* theology developed. It cannot generate the internal-coherence argument the Systematician made. That argument does not depend on history — it depends on logic. And it is, to my mind, the single most difficult challenge Alderwood's position faces in this debate.

---

## III. What My Discipline Cannot Decide

Reception history cannot determine what the texts mean. It cannot tell you whether Paul intended 1 Timothy 2:12 as a universal prohibition or a local instruction. It cannot tell you whether the creation-order appeal is transcultural or culturally embedded. It cannot tell you whether the recognition-and-containment pattern was theologically justified or theologically mistaken.

The hermeneutician's unanswered question — what principled distinction justifies treating 1 Timothy 2:13 as transcultural while not applying the same logic to 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 — remains unanswered. My discipline generated evidence relevant to this question. It cannot resolve it.

**What this irreducible uncertainty requires**: it requires that the church hold its position with the provisionality its actual hermeneutical situation demands. Alderwood's own document classifies this as secondary doctrine. The church's practice should match that classification — not in the sense of indifference, but in the sense of disclosed humility. The people affected by the restriction deserve to know that the basis is genuinely contested, not settled.

---

## IV. The Woman in the Room

A woman in my envisioned church who believes she is called to the elder role would be told the truth: the church holds a position it believes is biblically grounded, but that position has a history, and that history includes forces that are not purely exegetical. She would be told that the church's restriction is held provisionally, as secondary doctrine, and that her disagreement does not place her outside orthodoxy. She would be told about Phoebe, Junia, Priscilla, Deborah, and Huldah — not as proof texts for her position, but as evidence that the Spirit has placed women in authoritative roles without apology, and that the tradition's containment of those precedents is itself a human choice that requires justification.

She would not be told the question is settled. She would not be told her sense of calling is illegitimate. She would be given every opportunity to exercise her gifts — including teaching, leading, and shepherding — that the church's current position permits. And she would be told that the church is willing to be wrong, and that if it is wrong, it will repent.

That last commitment is not optional. It is what the history demands. A tradition that has suppressed Junia's name, contained Phoebe's title, explained away Deborah's leadership, closed convents, restricted missionaries, and shifted translations — all while claiming to simply read Scripture — owes at least this: the willingness to say, *we may be the ones who need to repent next*.

---

*Reception history does not tell the church what to conclude. It tells the church what it has done. And a church that knows what it has done holds its current position differently than one that does not.*
