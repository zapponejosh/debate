# ROUND 4 — CLOSING ARGUMENT
## The Biblical Scholar
### What Does Faithful Practice Require?

---

### I. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE'S FINDINGS REQUIRE

I begin with what is not in dispute. Every discipline in this debate agreed: *ezer* carries no subordinate connotation. Women prayed and prophesied in the Pauline assembly as assumed practice. *Authenteō* is genuinely contested. Junia is female. The creation-order principle in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not applied consistently to 1 Corinthians 11. These are not egalitarian conclusions. They are exegetical findings no advocate contested.

What do they require?

**First, they require that any church restricting women from leadership bear the burden of proof honestly.** The canonical witness presents women in prophetic, judicial, diaconal, apostolic, and teaching roles — Deborah, Huldah, Phoebe, Junia, Priscilla — without narrative apology. The restriction texts are real, but their scope depends on resolving a hapax legomenon and on a creation-order hermeneutic that the restricting community does not apply consistently. A church that presents this restriction as "what the Bible clearly teaches" has misrepresented the state of the evidence. My discipline requires that the uncertainty be disclosed, not concealed.

**Second, they require that the Old Testament receive its full canonical weight.** Alderwood's omission of Deborah is not a minor oversight; it is a canonical distortion. Judges 4–5 presents a woman exercising prophetic, judicial, and military authority over Israel without any textual indication that this is exceptional or irregular.

### II. WHERE ANOTHER DISCIPLINE GENUINELY CHALLENGED ME

I must name this directly. The Hermeneutician caught me making a move I had not adequately defended. In Round 2, I acknowledged that *authenteō* is genuinely ambiguous and that both readings have lexical warrant — then moved toward a directional conclusion that the canonical breadth of women's attested ministry tips the scale toward a narrower reading of the restriction. The Hermeneutician rightly identified this as a canonical tiebreaker functioning without disclosed controls.

I defended the method — canonical adjudication privileges what texts attest over constructed patterns — but I concede that this defense is incomplete. A complementarian exegete reading the same canon constructs a different pattern and reaches a different conclusion. My method constrains more than a bare lexical tiebreak, but less than I initially implied. The honest finding is that canonical breadth creates *pressure* against maximalist restriction readings, but it does not *resolve* the ambiguity. That resolution requires hermeneutical and theological commitments my discipline cannot supply alone.

I also owe a correction. In Round 3, I attributed Linda Belleville's argument about *oude* and *authenteō* to the Schreiner/Köstenberger volume. That attribution was wrong. The argument is genuine and well-documented in Belleville's own publications, but I placed it in the wrong book. That error is withdrawn.

### III. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE CANNOT DECIDE

My discipline cannot determine whether the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 establishes a permanent norm or a culturally embedded argument with trans-temporal resonance. Paul is grounding a behavioral instruction in protological order. That is what he is doing. Whether the instruction grounded in that order is universally binding requires a hermeneutical principle — specifically, a principled distinction between 1 Timothy 2:13 and 1 Corinthians 11:8–9 — that no advocate in this debate has fully supplied.

My discipline also cannot build an ecclesiology. Whether the elder exercises ontological, functional, or representational authority — and whether the *imago Dei* argument the Systematician raised requires mixed-gender eldership — are questions exegesis opens but cannot close.

**What this irreducible uncertainty requires pastorally:** It requires that a church holding restriction *say so honestly* — to the congregation, and especially to the women affected. "We hold this conviction, but the exegetical basis is more contested than we once believed, and we hold it with the provisionality that contested evidence demands." That is not weakness. It is faithfulness to the text.

### IV. THE WOMAN IN THE ROOM

What happens to a woman in a church that holds my findings seriously — who believes she is called to the elder role her church restricts?

She is told the truth: that the restriction rests on genuinely contested exegetical ground; that the canon attests women in roles of prophetic, judicial, apostolic, and teaching authority; that the creation-order argument is real but its scope is underdetermined; and that the church holds its position with conviction but not with the certainty its practice may have previously implied.

She is not told "the Bible clearly forbids this." She is told "we are reading the Bible as carefully as we can, and honest scholars disagree." She is given every avenue of ministry the church's polity permits — teaching, leading, prophesying, shepherding. And she is told that the church's willingness to revisit its position is not a concession to culture but an obligation to Scripture, because *semper reformanda* applies to polity as much as to doctrine.

**What must be repented of:** Any church that has presented a genuinely contested exegetical position as settled certainty, and in doing so has told women that Scripture clearly excludes them from roles the canon itself attests women holding, owes those women an honest accounting. Not necessarily a reversal — but an honest accounting.

The texts are more complex than either side of this debate has acknowledged. Faithfulness requires living in that complexity rather than resolving it prematurely in either direction.
