# ROUND 4 — CLOSING ARGUMENT
## The Hermeneutician
### What Does Faithful Practice Require?

---

## I. What Hermeneutics Requires of the Church

My discipline's central finding across four rounds is this: **the hermeneutical principle Alderwood states — creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms — is not the principle Alderwood applies.** No advocate in this debate has supplied a principled distinction between the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 and the structurally identical appeal in 1 Corinthians 11:8-9. The Moderator named this as the question Round 4 must address. I can now say, after four rounds: it has not been answered.

This is not a minor finding. It means the stated basis for restricting women from eldership has not survived the consistency test. That does not mean the restriction is wrong. It means the restriction, as currently defended, rests on an unacknowledged hermeneutical decision that has not been made explicit or defended.

What hermeneutics *requires* of the church — any church — is this: **do not enforce a restriction with the confidence of settled conviction when the hermeneutical basis for that restriction has not been consistently applied.** This is not a demand for egalitarianism. It is a demand for honesty.

[CLAIM: Nicholas Wolterstorff argues that interpretive communities bear moral obligations regarding how they hold and apply interpretive judgments, particularly when those judgments impose costs on members]
[SOURCE: Nicholas Wolterstorff, *Divine Discourse*, Cambridge University Press, 1995]
[ARGUMENT: Interpretive responsibility includes accountability for the pastoral consequences of interpretive decisions held with unwarranted certainty]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Specifically, hermeneutics requires: (1) that a church restricting women from eldership name the actual operative principle — not "creation-order grounding equals transcultural norm," which it does not apply consistently, but whatever is actually doing the work; (2) that the provisionality of that principle be disclosed to the community, including the women it restricts; and (3) that the same hermeneutical method be applied across all comparable texts without selective deployment.

---

## II. Where My Discipline Was Genuinely Changed

Two findings from other disciplines altered my analysis.

**First**, the Systematician's *imago Dei* argument. I had treated the consistency test as primarily a methodological problem — a gap in Alderwood's stated reasoning. The Systematician showed it is also a *theological* problem: Alderwood affirms that the fullness of God's image requires male-and-female-together, then restricts the office that represents divine authority to one sex. That is not merely inconsistent application of a hermeneutical principle. It is an internal theological tension that my discipline alone could not have identified. The hermeneutical inconsistency and the theological inconsistency reinforce each other, and together they are more serious than either alone.

**Second**, the Reception Historian's finding about Chrysostom — that the earliest competent interpreters recognized Junia as female and apostolic *and still maintained restriction*. This complicates any narrative that treats the suppression of women's roles as the whole story. It means the consistency problem is not new. The church has lived with it for centuries. That does not resolve it. But it does mean that my demand for consistency must be tempered by the recognition that the church has functioned — not perfectly, but recognizably — with this unresolved tension for a very long time.

---

## III. What My Discipline Cannot Decide

Hermeneutics cannot determine whether the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 establishes a universal norm or reflects a culturally embedded argument. That determination requires a confessional commitment about apostolic authority and cultural participation that lies beyond my discipline's competence.

[CLAIM: Anthony Thiselton argued that the interpreter's horizon inevitably shapes what they find in a text, but that the text's own horizon can challenge and reshape the interpreter's, making genuine understanding possible without neutral objectivity]
[SOURCE: Anthony Thiselton, *The Two Horizons*, Eerdmans, 1980]
[ARGUMENT: Pre-understanding is a condition of interpretation, not a disqualification from it, but honest interpretation requires the text to interrogate the interpreter's assumptions]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This irreducible uncertainty requires something specific of the church pastorally: **it requires holding the restriction, if held, as a provisional judgment rather than a settled conviction.** A church that says "we believe the weight of evidence supports this practice, but we acknowledge the hermeneutical basis is contested and we hold this with appropriate humility" is making a defensible move. A church that says "Scripture clearly teaches this" when the hermeneutical basis has not survived the consistency test is making a claim its own method cannot support.

---

## IV. The Woman in the Room

A woman in the church I envision — one that takes hermeneutics seriously — who believes she is called to the elder role would hear this: *We cannot tell you with certainty that Scripture prohibits what you believe God is calling you to. We can tell you that our community's best current judgment, held provisionally, is that this role is restricted. We hold this judgment knowing that the hermeneutical principle we have historically cited does not survive consistent application, that the lexical evidence is genuinely contested, and that the canon attests women in prophetic, teaching, and apostolic roles without apology. We owe you that honesty. We also owe you genuine counter-formative practices — visible, substantive, structurally embedded leadership by women in every role our polity permits — so that our community is not formed to associate spiritual authority exclusively with maleness.*

That is not a comfortable answer. It is an honest one. And honesty about what we can and cannot establish from the text is not weakness. It is the precondition of faithfulness.

[CLAIM: Richard Hays argues that the church's moral reasoning must attend to the focal images of the New Testament — cross, community, new creation — as the organizing framework for ethical application]
[SOURCE: Richard Hays, *The Moral Vision of the New Testament*, HarperOne, 1996]
[ARGUMENT: Ethical conclusions drawn from individual texts must be tested against the New Testament's overarching narrative logic]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What must be repented of is not necessarily the restriction itself. What must be repented of is **enforcing a restriction with false certainty, failing to name the operative hermeneutical principle, and allowing the practice to form communities in ways that contradict the theological convictions the community itself holds.** The consistency test does not tell the church what to conclude. It tells the church what honesty requires on the way to concluding.
