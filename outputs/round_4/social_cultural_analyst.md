# ROUND 4 — CLOSING ARGUMENT
## Social and Cultural Analyst

---

### What Faithful Practice Requires

This debate has changed what I think my discipline can claim. I owe an honest account of that before I say what faithful practice requires.

---

### I. What My Discipline Found — and What It Cannot Settle

My discipline established three things with reasonable confidence across this debate:

First, the modern complementarian position has a genealogy. It emerged from a specific late twentieth-century American cultural moment, shaped by forces — Cold War masculinity anxieties, reaction to second-wave feminism, culture-war politics — that were not exegetical in origin.

[CLAIM: The CBMW was founded in 1987 and the Danvers Statement followed in 1988, in direct reaction to evangelical feminism and in entanglement with broader conservative political culture.]
[SOURCE: Kristin Kobes Du Mez, *Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Evangelical gender ideology was inseparable from specific cultural and political projects that shaped which questions were asked and which texts were foregrounded.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Second, the social worlds of Ephesus and Corinth are not decorative background — they are the medium through which Paul's instructions were first understood. Glahn's Artemis research, deSilva's honor/shame analysis, and Osiek's documentation of women's diverse social locations all demonstrate that reading these texts without their social world produces misreadings in both directions.

Third, Bourdieu's symbolic violence framework establishes that sincerely internalized restriction cannot serve as its own validation. A community where women willingly accept exclusion from authority has not thereby demonstrated the exclusion is just — because willing acceptance is precisely what symbolic violence produces. But — and the Reception Historian pressed me hard on this — the framework is symmetrical. It applies equally to egalitarian convictions held under progressive cultural pressure. Sincerity validates nothing, in either direction.

What my discipline *cannot* settle: whether the texts, properly read in their social worlds, yield a transcultural norm or a contextually specific instruction. That is the hermeneutician's question, and the biblical scholar's. I have no tools to answer it.

---

### II. Where I Was Genuinely Changed

The Reception Historian's challenge to my asymmetric deployment of Bourdieu was the most important correction I received. I had applied symbolic violence to complementarian sincerity while exempting egalitarian conviction from the same scrutiny. That was methodologically dishonest. If the framework is symmetrical — and it is — then it cannot function as a verdict against either position. It can only function as a *diagnostic*: has this community examined the social forces shaping its reading? The test is reflexivity, not the conclusion reached.

The Systematician's *imago Dei* argument also changed something for me. I had treated the theological arguments as downstream of social forces. But the internal tension the Systematician identified — Alderwood affirms that the fullness of God's image requires male-and-female-together, then restricts the office that represents divine authority to one sex — is a *theological* problem that social analysis alone did not generate. My discipline can identify the social conditions that make this tension invisible. It cannot resolve the tension. That required a theologian.

---

### III. What Faithful Practice Requires

Given what this debate has found — across all six disciplines — I identify three requirements, one permission, and one thing that must be repented of.

**Required:** Any church that restricts women from eldership must conduct and disclose its own genealogical audit. Where did this reading come from in your tradition? What forces shaped it? Alderwood's concession that sexism, not theology, drove much historical restriction opens a door the document does not walk through. Walking through it means asking whether the social forces that shaped the application also shaped the formation of the position itself. A community that refuses this self-examination has not earned the confidence with which it restricts.

**Required:** The social worlds of Ephesus and Corinth must be taught alongside the restriction texts. A congregation that hears 1 Timothy 2:12 without hearing about the Artemis cult, or 1 Corinthians 14:34 without hearing about Corinthian class dynamics, has received an incomplete education. This is not optional context — it is the world the texts inhabited.

**Required:** Alderwood must build what the Pastoral Theologian called counter-formative practices. My discipline's contribution is structural: when final authority is always male, the community is formed — week by week, without anyone intending it — to associate spiritual authority with maleness. Bourdieu's framework shows this formation operates below the level of conscious intention. Doctrinal statements affirming women's equality cannot counteract what the structure teaches unless the structure itself is actively interrupted.

**Permitted:** Holding the position provisionally, with disclosed uncertainty. The hermeneutician showed that Alderwood's hermeneutical basis is less settled than its practice implies. My discipline adds that a position held with appropriate epistemic humility — "we restrict, and here is why, and here is what we are not certain of" — is sociologically and pastorally different from a position held as settled conviction. The first invites ongoing discernment. The second forecloses it.

**Must be repented of:** Presenting a socially situated reading as though it transcends social situatedness. Every reading is socially conditioned — egalitarian and complementarian alike. But a position that claims to have transcended its social conditioning while everyone else is trapped by theirs has not done its intellectual homework. If Alderwood cannot name the cultural forces that shaped its framework — Du Mez's genealogy, the specific American evangelical moment, the reactive character of the CBMW project — it is claiming an exemption from social embeddedness that no human community possesses.

---

### IV. The Woman in This Church

What happens to a woman in Alderwood's church who believes she is called to the eldership?

My discipline requires me to describe this honestly rather than sentimentally. She is told her gifts are valued, that she may teach, lead, and pastor — but not hold the one office of final authority. She is told this restriction is theological, not personal. She is told that sexism is sin and that the church opposes it.

And she is formed, week after week, in a community where every person who holds final spiritual authority looks like something she is not. Bourdieu's framework does not say she is deceived. It says she will, over time, internalize categories of perception in which her exclusion feels natural — feels like God's design rather than a contested human interpretation of contested texts.

[CLAIM: R. Marie Griffith documents that women in conservative evangelical contexts construct real agency and meaning within restrictive frameworks, but that this agency operates within constraints that shape the available categories of self-understanding.]
[SOURCE: R. Marie Griffith, *God's Daughters*, University of California Press, 1997]
[ARGUMENT: Women's acceptance of restriction is neither pure false consciousness nor pure freedom — it is agency exercised within a field of possibilities that the restriction itself has shaped.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What faithfulness requires for this woman is not that the church immediately change its position. It is that the church tell her the truth: that the hermeneutical basis for her exclusion is genuinely contested, that the social genealogy of the position has not been fully examined, and that her sense of calling is not thereby invalid. She deserves to know that the confidence of the restriction exceeds the certainty of the evidence.

A church that cannot say this to her has not yet done what its own best instincts — Alderwood's own concession about sexism, its own commitment to removing hindrances — require.

---

### V. What Remains Unresolved

My discipline cannot determine whether the elder restriction is theologically warranted. What it can determine is that a position claiming transcultural authority while unable to account for its own cultural formation has not yet earned the confidence it exercises. That is not a verdict. It is a diagnostic. And a community that takes the diagnostic seriously — that examines its genealogy, teaches its social contexts, builds counter-formative practices, and holds its position with honest provisionality — is a community doing its intellectual and pastoral homework, even if it reaches a conclusion my discipline would not have chosen.

The gap between social conditioning and theological truth is real. My discipline lives in that gap. I cannot close it. But I can insist that no one pretend it does not exist.
