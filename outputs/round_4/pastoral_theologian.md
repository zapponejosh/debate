# CLOSING ARGUMENT — PASTORAL THEOLOGIAN

## What Does Faithful Practice Require?

---

### I. What My Discipline's Findings Require

Across four rounds, pastoral theology has carried three findings that the church cannot set aside, regardless of where it lands on the exegetical question.

**First, the formation finding.** What a church *does* teaches more powerfully than what it *says*. When only men hold final authority, the congregation is being formed — week by week, below the level of conscious doctrine — to associate God's authority with maleness. This is not speculation; it is what James K.A. Smith's liturgical formation analysis predicts and what pastoral observation across contexts confirms.

[CLAIM: Repeated embodied practices form communal imagination about God and authority at a level deeper than doctrinal instruction]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Liturgical practices — including who stands where and who holds what office — communicate theology prior to and more powerfully than propositional teaching]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Any church maintaining an all-male eldership must build active, sustained counter-formative practices — not merely correct doctrine — to prevent that formation from teaching what its theology denies. Alderwood's document does not address this obligation. Almost no complementarian church does.

**Second, the harm-abuse connection.** Diane Langberg's work and the documented patterns of the ChurchToo movement establish that gender-exclusive authority structures are one structural condition — not the only one, but a real one — under which spiritual abuse is more likely to occur and less likely to be named. This does not mean complementarian churches are abusive. It means the structural risk is real and the accountability burden is specific: external review, women in governance-adjacent roles with genuine authority to name problems, and transparent grievance processes that do not route through the very authority structure being questioned.

[CLAIM: Hierarchical authority structures that concentrate unchecked power create conditions under which abuse is more likely and less likely to be named]
[SOURCE: Diane Langberg, *Redeeming Power*, Brazos Press, 2020]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Third, the calling question.** A woman who has preached to the congregation, led staff, been called "pastor," and is told her vocation has a ceiling she cannot cross — that woman is not a hermeneutical abstraction. She is a person whose experience of calling has a shape, and the church that closes that shape bears a pastoral obligation it cannot discharge with "your gifts are affirmed elsewhere." Ruth Tucker's testimony and the broader evidence show that this redirection, offered without honest acknowledgment of the cost, produces spiritual harm that accumulates across years.

---

### II. Where Another Discipline Genuinely Changed My Thinking

The Systematician's *imago Dei* argument did not merely supplement my formation concern — it gave it theological architecture I lacked. Alderwood affirms that "the fullness of the way that humanity images God can only be seen in the partnership of male and female together." The Systematician pressed this to its conclusion: if the elder represents divine authority to the community, and if God's image requires both male and female, then an all-male eldership cannot fully represent the God whose image it claims to bear. This is not a pastoral intuition. It is a doctrinal incoherence that my discipline sensed but could not name precisely. The Systematician named it.

I was also genuinely challenged by the Reception Historian's finding that Chrysostom recognized Junia as female and apostolic — and still maintained restriction. Fruitfulness and recognition do not automatically produce structural change. The history of the church includes long periods where the evidence was available and the conclusion was not drawn. That sobers my confidence that naming the evidence will produce the reform I believe is needed.

---

### III. What My Discipline Cannot Decide

Pastoral theology cannot settle what Scripture requires. It cannot determine whether *authenteō* means "dominate" or "exercise authority." It cannot resolve whether Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is transcultural or culturally embedded. The Hermeneutician showed that Alderwood's own hermeneutical principle does not survive the consistency test with 1 Corinthians 11 — but pastoral theology cannot close that gap.

What this irreducible uncertainty *requires* pastorally is honesty. A church that presents a genuinely contested exegetical conclusion as settled — that tells women "Scripture restricts you" when the decisive term is a hapax legomenon whose meaning is disputed by serious scholars on both sides — has failed a pastoral obligation prior to any question about the conclusion itself. The uncertainty must be disclosed to those who bear its cost.

---

### IV. What Happens to Her in My Envisioned Church

In the church my discipline envisions — whether it restricts the eldership or opens it — this is what happens to the woman who believes she is called:

She is told the truth. She is told that the exegetical evidence is genuinely contested. She is told that the church has made a decision, and what the decision is, and that the decision may be wrong. She is told that her calling is taken seriously — not as a problem to manage, but as a possible work of the Spirit that the church must discern with her, not against her. If the church restricts the eldership, she is given a genuine account of why — not with the false confidence of settled conviction, but with the honest weight of a church acting under uncertainty. She is given access to roles with real authority and real voice, not decorative inclusion. And she is told that the church will revisit the question — not as a concession to culture, but as an obligation of *semper reformanda* — and that her experience, her gifts, and her sense of call are part of the evidence the church is obligated to weigh.

If the church cannot keep that promise — if the structural logic of restriction expands rather than contracts, if the promised flourishing does not materialize, if the women who were told "your gifts are valued" find that the valuation has a ceiling that lowers over time — then the church owes her not just an apology but a structural reckoning.

Fruitfulness is real evidence. Harm is real evidence. Neither settles the exegetical question. But a church that produces systematic harm and calls it faithfulness must answer for that harm — and the answer must be more than an appeal to transcultural principle. It must be a life the church is actually living.
