# SYSTEMATIC/ECCLESIOLOGICAL THEOLOGIAN
## Round 4 — Closing Argument: What Does Faithful Practice Require?

---

### I. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE'S FINDINGS REQUIRE

Across four rounds, I have pressed a single question: *what doctrine of the church, ministry, and authority is actually implied by a given position on women's leadership — and is that doctrine coherent?* The answer, applied to Alderwood, is that the position rests on theological commitments it has not examined, and that when those commitments are made explicit, they generate internal tensions the document cannot resolve on its own terms.

Three findings are load-bearing.

**First**, Alderwood operates with a functional account of eldership — elders guard doctrine, teach authoritatively, delegate teaching to non-elders — while its creation-order argument implies a representational account: the elder represents divine authority to the community, and that representation requires maleness. These are different doctrines of ministry. A functional account asks whether women can perform the functions; Alderwood's own document concedes they largely can. A representational account asks whether maleness is necessary for representing God's authority — and here Alderwood's own affirmation that the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires male-and-female-together creates a problem the document never addresses. No advocate in this debate supplied a resolution. The *imago Dei* tension stands.

[CLAIM: Amy Peeler argues that the Son's representative role is grounded in his humanity, not his maleness, and that Scripture's masculine divine language does not establish maleness as necessary for representing God]
[SOURCE: Amy Peeler, *Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022]
[ARGUMENT: If representation of God to the community is grounded in shared humanity with Christ rather than shared maleness, the representational model does not yield the gendered conclusion it has historically produced]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

**Second**, the ESS/EFS debate remains directly relevant. The strongest theological grounding for permanent male authority — that the eternal subordination of the Son to the Father establishes a model for human authority structures — departs from Nicene orthodoxy as Kevin Giles has argued. Alderwood does not invoke ESS, but any representational argument must eventually name its theological ground. If it is not the immanent Trinity, what is it? Creation order alone cannot bear this weight without a doctrine of how creation order governs ecclesial structure — which brings us back to the first finding.

[CLAIM: Kevin Giles argues that the eternal functional subordination of the Son is inconsistent with Nicene trinitarianism]
[SOURCE: Kevin Giles, *The Trinity and Subordinationism*, IVP Academic, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Nicene orthodoxy affirms the full equality of the divine persons in power and authority; grounding permanent male authority in trinitarian relations requires a departure from that orthodoxy]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Third**, the new creation question remains unanswered. If the church is the community of the inaugurated new creation, constituted by the Spirit, its structures should embody the reality of the age to come — not merely reproduce the patterns of the old creation. Alderwood orients its argument toward creation order and redemptive-historical pattern. It never asks whether the church's eschatological vocation requires something different. That question is not optional. It is constitutive of ecclesiology.

What faithful practice requires: a church that restricts women from eldership must be able to articulate — with precision, not gesture — what kind of authority the elder exercises, where that authority comes from, and why that authority requires maleness. If it cannot, the restriction is ecclesiologically ungrounded.

### II. WHERE ANOTHER DISCIPLINE GENUINELY CHALLENGED ME

The Pastoral Theologian changed my thinking. I asked whether formation data could bear ecclesiological weight or merely supplemented it. The answer I received — that when exegesis is genuinely contested and the hermeneutical principle is inconsistently applied, formation data moves from supplementary to load-bearing — is one I now accept with qualification. The qualification is that formation evidence requires a doctrine of the church to interpret it. But the Pastoral Theologian is right that what a practice *teaches* about God is not irrelevant to whether the practice is faithful. An all-male eldership teaches something about whose face God wears. That teaching must be evaluated theologically, not merely acknowledged pastorally.

The Social Analyst also sharpened my argument by showing that my three models of eldership are themselves socially embedded. I accept this. But social embeddedness is not epistemic disqualification — it is a reason for testing frameworks against their own stated warrants. That test still stands.

---

### III. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE CANNOT DECIDE

Systematic theology cannot determine the meaning of *authenteō*. It cannot settle whether 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 is Paul's instruction or a Corinthian slogan he refutes. It cannot weigh the historical evidence for early women's ministry against the near-universal restriction that followed. These are tasks for the biblical scholar and the reception historian.

What this irreducible uncertainty requires of the church: *honesty about the nature of the position being held*. Alderwood's document presents its restriction with the confidence of settled conviction. The hermeneutician has shown that the hermeneutical basis does not support that confidence. The biblical scholar has shown that the lexical evidence is genuinely contested. The reception historian has shown that the specific form of this complementarianism has a traceable genealogy shaped by non-exegetical forces.

A church may hold this position. But it must hold it as a *provisional judgment under genuine uncertainty*, not as a clear biblical teaching about which reasonable Christians merely disagree on secondary matters. The pastoral cost of false certainty falls on specific people.

---

### IV. THE WOMAN IN THIS CHURCH

A woman in Alderwood who believes the Spirit has called her to the full ministry of eldership — teaching, guarding doctrine, shepherding the community — faces this: a church that affirms her gifts, permits her to preach on Sunday mornings, allows her the title of pastor, and then tells her the one office that exercises final spiritual authority is closed to her because of her sex. She is told this restriction is biblical. She is not told that the hermeneutical principle grounding it is applied inconsistently, that the lexical evidence is genuinely contested, that the theological foundation has not been examined for coherence, or that the *imago Dei* affirmation the church itself makes creates an unresolved tension with the restriction.

What faithfulness requires is not necessarily removing the restriction. It is telling her the truth about the nature of the position — that it is held under genuine uncertainty, that serious Christians reading the same texts reach different conclusions, and that the church owes her an account of why it holds the position it holds that is more precise than what the document currently provides. She deserves to know that the restriction is a judgment call, not a settled reading — and that the church is willing to keep examining it.

A church that restricts must restrict honestly. Anything less is not complementarianism. It is institutional self-protection with theological vocabulary.
