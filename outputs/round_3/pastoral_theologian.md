### TEXT 1: Genesis 2:18-23 — *Ezer*, Formation, and the Weight of Origin Stories

**~200 words**

The pastoral question this text raises is not primarily exegetical — it is formative. How does a congregation hear this text? What does it teach, week by week, about the structure of human relationship?

Alderwood correctly refuses to read *ezer* as subordinate assistance. The biblical scholar and systematician have confirmed this. But the pastoral observation I carry is different: even when *ezer* is correctly taught as "indispensable strength," the surrounding narrative — woman created after man, from man, for man's need — is being received by congregations whose imagination has been formed by centuries of hierarchical reading. Correct doctrine does not automatically correct formative damage.

[CLAIM: Embodied practices and repeated narrative framings shape communal imagination at a level deeper than conscious doctrinal instruction, meaning that correct teaching about *ezer* does not automatically undo what the surrounding narrative structure has formed people to feel]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Smith argues that liturgical and narrative practices form desire and imagination prior to conscious belief, making counter-formation an active and ongoing pastoral task, not a one-time doctrinal correction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The pastoral obligation this creates: any church that teaches Genesis 2 must actively build counter-formative practices — naming women as *ezer* in the military/divine sense, not the domestic-helper sense — or it will form its children to believe something its doctrine denies. That counter-formation work is largely absent from Alderwood's document.

What this text does *not* establish for my discipline: it does not settle the hierarchy question. What it does establish is that origin stories are among the most powerful formative texts a community possesses. How they are taught matters as much as what they say.

### TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14 — The Pastoral Cost of Contested Certainty

**~300 words**

My colleagues have established the exegetical and hermeneutical terrain with care. The *authenteō* dispute is genuine. The consistency problem with 1 Corinthians 11 is unresolved. The Ephesian context is relevant. I accept all of this and add the pastoral layer.

The pastoral problem with Alderwood's handling of this text is not its conclusion. It is its tone. The document presents this passage as yielding a relatively clear restriction — "it is most likely that Paul is prohibiting women from performing the specific kind of authoritative teaching reserved for the elders" — while the hermeneutician has demonstrated that the hermeneutical basis for that confidence has not survived the consistency test.

What does that mean for the woman sitting in the pew who is told this text settles her vocation's ceiling? She is being asked to bear the pastoral cost of a restriction whose exegetical basis is genuinely contested, presented with the confidence of settled conviction.

[CLAIM: The pastoral cost of restriction falls with particular severity on women who experience genuine vocational calling, and the church's response to that cost has often been inadequate]
[SOURCE: Ruth Tucker, *Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*, Zondervan, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Tucker argues that calling has a vocational shape that cannot be redirected without pastoral cost that must be owned — and that the church's typical response has been to acknowledge the gift while permanently closing the vocational form]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutician established in Round 2 that honest uncertainty requires naming that uncertainty to the people affected. I press this further: naming the uncertainty is not merely an epistemic obligation. It is a pastoral one. A woman told "Scripture clearly restricts you" when Scripture does not clearly restrict her — when the text is genuinely contested at the decisive lexical point — has been misled about the nature of the burden she is being asked to carry.

This does not resolve the exegetical question. It requires that however the question is held, it be held honestly — with the uncertainty disclosed to those who bear its cost.

### TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 — The Underused Text and What It Costs to Ignore It

**~200 words**

The pastoral observation here is simple and significant: Alderwood does not build on this text, and the silence is formative.

Paul assumes women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. He regulates *how* they do it — not *whether* they do it. The pastoral implication is that women's public, Spirit-directed speech in the gathered church is not a concession Paul makes reluctantly; it is a practice he treats as ordinary enough to regulate.

[CLAIM: Paul's instructions in 1 Corinthians 11:5 presuppose that women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly as a normal practice, not an exceptional one]
[SOURCE: Lucy Peppiatt, *Unveiling Paul's Women*, Cascade, 2018]
[ARGUMENT: Peppiatt argues that Paul's regulation of women's prophesying assumes its legitimacy and that the head-covering discussion is intelligible only if women's public speech is already an established practice in Corinth]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The formation question: if Alderwood's women can preach on Sunday mornings — which the document affirms — why is 1 Corinthians 11:5 not a central text in the document's argument? Its absence teaches something. It suggests that women's public Spirit-directed speech is still, at some level, an accommodation rather than an assumption. The text, if centered, would form the congregation differently.

### TEXT 4: 1 Corinthians 14:33-35 — Pastoral Implications of the Slogan Hypothesis

**~150 words**

My discipline does not adjudicate whether Peppiatt's reading of this passage as a Corinthian slogan Paul refutes is exegetically correct. The biblical scholar and hermeneutician must carry that. What I observe pastorally is this: Alderwood presents both interpretive options without choosing between them, then builds its restriction primarily on 1 Timothy 2.

The pastoral implication is that the restriction rests on a narrower exegetical foundation than the document implies. If 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 is either a refuted slogan or a restriction on disruptive questioning rather than a universal silence command — both of which Alderwood acknowledges as possible — then the weight falls entirely on 1 Timothy 2:12, whose decisive term (*authenteō*) is lexically contested.

A restriction built on a single contested hapax legomenon, presented as though it rests on multiple converging texts, creates a pastoral problem: the confidence exceeds the evidence.

### TEXT 5: Galatians 3:28 — Ecclesiological Weight and Pastoral Formation

**~200 words**

The debate about whether this text is soteriological only or also ecclesiological is a debate my colleagues must adjudicate. What I bring is the formation observation.

Regardless of what the text *establishes* for church polity, it forms something in the community that hears it. "Neither male nor female" is one of the most repeated texts in Christian liturgy and formation. When a congregation hears it Sunday after Sunday while experiencing a structure in which male-and-female distinction is precisely what determines access to the highest office, a dissonance is created — not just intellectually, but imaginatively.

[CLAIM: The gap between what a community teaches propositionally and what its practices embody creates formative dissonance that can produce either critical reflection or cynicism, depending on whether the community names the tension or obscures it]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Smith's formation analysis implies that unaddressed gaps between doctrine and practice do not simply coexist — they form something, and what they form is not always what the doctrine intends]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Alderwood does not name this dissonance to its congregation. The pastoral obligation is to name it — and to give the congregation a theologically honest account of why the church holds both the Galatians 3:28 affirmation and the elder restriction simultaneously. That account needs to be more than "these are different kinds of statements." It needs to be formatively adequate to the dissonance people are actually experiencing.

### TEXT 6: Romans 16 — Phoebe, Junia, and the Pastoral Specific

**~350 words**

This is where my discipline has the most to contribute, because this text is not an abstraction. These are specific women, named, with specific roles, in a specific community.

The reception historian has established that Junia was recognized as female and as an apostle by the earliest commentators, and that the masculinization of her name was a later, non-textual development. The biblical scholar has established that Phoebe's designation as *diakonos* and *prostatis* carries substantive ministerial and patronal weight. These are not marginal figures — they are commended by Paul to the Roman church as people of genuine authority and function.

The pastoral question this raises for Alderwood specifically: the document commends Priscilla in passing but does not engage Phoebe or Junia systematically. Given that Alderwood promises a culture of flourishing for gifted women, and given that these women represent the most expansive picture of women's ministry in the Pauline corpus, why are they not central to the document's positive vision?

[CLAIM: Phoebe's designation as *prostatis* — a term carrying connotations of patronal authority and leadership — means she exercised a form of authority over the community she served, not merely service within it]
[SOURCE: Nijay Gupta, *Tell Her Story*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Gupta argues that *prostatis* in Romans 16:2 designates Phoebe as someone who exercised genuine leadership authority, and that diminishing translations obscure this]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Junia's identification as "outstanding among the apostles" in Romans 16:7 represents Paul's recognition of a woman in the highest functional category of early church ministry]
[SOURCE: Richard Bauckham, *Gospel Women*, Eerdmans, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Bauckham argues that Junia's apostolic identification is not marginal or exceptional in Paul's usage — apostles are the foundation of the church's mission and authority]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The pastoral implication for the Alderwood-specific question: if Phoebe exercised *prostatis* — patronal leadership — over a congregation including men, and if Junia was recognized as an apostle, then the claim that women cannot hold the highest authority in the church is not simply a reading of the Pauline letters. It requires explaining why these women's roles do not constitute a precedent. Alderwood does not provide that explanation. The pastoral promise of flourishing requires engaging these women's actual roles, not treating them as footnotes.

The intersectionality dimension: Phoebe was a woman of means, likely a freedwoman or property owner, whose patronage made her ministry possible. The women in contemporary complementarian churches who bear the cost of restriction are not a homogeneous group. Women of color in predominantly white complementarian contexts face the gender restriction on top of racial dynamics that compound the pastoral weight.

[CLAIM: The specific experiences of women of color in predominantly white complementarian contexts involve the intersection of gender restriction with racial dynamics that are not addressed by gender-only analysis]
[SOURCE: Marlena Graves, *The Way Up Is Down*, IVP, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Graves argues that marginalization operates through multiple overlapping structures, and that pastoral care for women in restrictive contexts must attend to the specific weight borne by women whose gender restriction is compounded by racial marginalization]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

### TEXT 7: Judges 4-5 and 2 Kings 22 — Deborah, Huldah, and the Pastoral Weight of Undefended Exceptions

**~300 words**

The biblical scholar noted in Round 1 that Alderwood does not mention Deborah. This omission is not merely exegetical — it is pastoral and formative.

Deborah exercises judicial, prophetic, and military authority. The text does not call her an exception. It does not apologize for her. It presents her as a leader Israel sought out, and the Song of Deborah celebrates her. Huldah is consulted for authoritative scriptural interpretation by the king's officials — not despite being a woman, but as the recognized prophetic authority of her moment.

[CLAIM: Deborah's role in Judges 4-5 includes judicial, prophetic, and military command functions, presented without narrative apology or qualification as exceptional]
[SOURCE: Phyllis Trible, *Texts of Terror*, Fortress, 1984]
[ARGUMENT: Trible's canonical reading notes that the text presents Deborah's authority as unremarkable within the narrative — the anomaly the text marks is not that a woman leads, but that Barak will not lead without her]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The pastoral and formative weight of these texts is significant. If a church's teaching on women's authority never engages Deborah and Huldah — never asks what it means that God chose these women for these roles without apology — it is forming its congregation to read the Old Testament through the lens of the restriction texts rather than letting the canon speak with its full complexity.

The question my discipline raises is not whether Deborah and Huldah are "exceptions" or "precedents" — that is the biblical scholar's question. My question is: what does a community teach its daughters when it systematically ignores the women the canon presents as leaders? What does the silence form?

Alderwood promises to champion women with gifts of teaching and leadership. A document that does not mention Deborah or Huldah has not yet made good on that promise at the level of formative imagination. The canon contains these women. The community's teaching should too.
