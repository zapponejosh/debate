### TEXT 1: Genesis 2:18-23 — *ezer*, sequence, and the social world of interpretation

**~200 words**

The Alderwood document rightly resists loading *ezer* with subordination. My contribution is different: I ask what social world the *sequence* argument inhabits when it travels from the text into church polity.

The claim that "Adam was formed first, therefore men lead" is not self-evidently derived from the text. It requires a social logic — specifically, the logic of primogeniture and birth-order status that was deeply embedded in the ancient Near Eastern honor/shame world.

[CLAIM: In ancient Near Eastern culture, birth order carried social and legal weight, with firstborn status conferring inheritance rights and social precedence.]
[SOURCE: David deSilva, *Honor, Patronage, Kinship and Purity*, IVP Academic, 2000]
[ARGUMENT: deSilva documents that primogeniture logic was pervasive in the Mediterranean world, making sequence-to-authority inferences feel natural within that social framework — but this naturalness is culturally specific, not universal.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutician has shown that Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not applied consistently. My discipline adds: the *inference* from sequence to authority is itself a social logic imported from the text's ambient world. That does not make it wrong. It means it requires defense rather than assumption.

What Genesis 2 establishes for church polity — if anything — cannot be read off the narrative without a social logic that must be named.

### TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14 — The Ephesian social world and the limits of transcultural claims

**~280 words**

This is where my discipline has the most to contribute, and I want to be precise rather than merely assertive.

Alderwood states Paul "appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations," making the principle transcultural. This claim has a social-scientific problem: it assumes that creation-order appeals are themselves culture-independent, when in fact the *use* of creation-order arguments to ground social arrangements was a common rhetorical move in the first-century Mediterranean world.

[CLAIM: First-century Greco-Roman moral philosophers regularly appealed to "nature" and creation order to ground existing social hierarchies, including gender hierarchy, making such appeals characteristic of the ambient intellectual culture rather than uniquely revelatory.]
[SOURCE: David deSilva, *Honor, Patronage, Kinship and Purity*, IVP Academic, 2000]
[ARGUMENT: Appeals to natural order as grounding for social arrangements were standard in the Stoic and Aristotelian traditions that shaped Paul's intellectual environment, which means such appeals cannot automatically be read as transcending their social context.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Now add the Ephesian specificity:

[CLAIM: Sandra Glahn argues that the Artemis cult in Ephesus gave women unusual religious authority and public prominence, creating specific pressures on the Ephesian Christian community to negotiate women's roles in ways that would not apply universally.]
[SOURCE: Sandra Glahn, *Nobody's Mother: Artemis of the Ephesians in Antiquity and the New Testament*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: The specific religious environment of Ephesus — where female religious authority was culturally dominant — means that instructions to the Ephesian community about women's roles were addressed to a specific social situation, complicating any straightforward universalization.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

My discipline cannot settle the *authenteō* dispute. But it can establish that the claim "Paul appeals to creation, not culture" underestimates how deeply creation-order rhetoric was itself embedded in first-century cultural discourse. The hermeneutician must account for this.

### TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 — Honor/shame and what Paul's assumption establishes

**~220 words**

The social world here is honor/shame, and it is doing significant work that Alderwood does not examine.

Paul's concern in this passage is not primarily about gender hierarchy in the abstract. It is about honorable public comportment in a community that meets in households where Greco-Roman social conventions governed appearance and behavior.

[CLAIM: In the first-century Mediterranean honor/shame system, a woman's uncovered head in public assembly carried social meaning — it was associated either with sexual availability or with social insubordination — making head covering a marker of honorable household membership.]
[SOURCE: Bruce Malina and John J. Pilch, *Social-Science Commentary on the Letters of Paul*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: The head-covering instruction is intelligible as Paul navigating the honor/shame implications of Christian assembly practice, not as a timeless statement about gender hierarchy.]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

What is sociologically significant is what Paul *assumes*: women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. This is not a concession or an exception — it is the social baseline from which Paul's argument proceeds. The restriction, if it is one, is about *how* women participate publicly, not *whether* they do.

Alderwood notes this but does not build on it sufficiently. The social analyst's observation is that the canonical baseline — women exercise public prophetic speech — is the socially attested norm in Pauline communities, and any restriction must be read against that norm, not the reverse.

### TEXT 4: 1 Corinthians 14:33-35 — Class conflict, social disruption, and the Corinthian context

**~200 words**

The Corinthian social world matters here in ways neither side typically acknowledges.

[CLAIM: The Corinthian community was marked by significant class conflict, with wealthy patrons and their households in tension with lower-status members, and this social stratification shaped the specific problems Paul addresses throughout 1 Corinthians.]
[SOURCE: Bruce Malina and John J. Pilch, *Social-Science Commentary on the Letters of Paul*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: The social disruptions Paul addresses in 1 Corinthians — including at the Lord's Supper and in the assembly — are intelligible as class-and-status conflicts in a community navigating Greco-Roman social hierarchies, not as abstract theological problems.]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The instruction for women to "ask their husbands at home" presupposes married women with husbands present — a socially specific situation that does not map onto all women in all assemblies. The social analyst notes that instructions addressed to a specific social configuration cannot be universalized without accounting for the social specificity.

Whether Alderwood's "disruptive questioning" reading or Peppiatt's "Corinthian slogan" reading is correct, my discipline's contribution is this: the social world of Corinth — its specific class dynamics, household configurations, and honor/shame pressures — is not background noise. It is the medium through which Paul's instruction was first understood and must be part of any responsible reading.

### TEXT 6: Romans 16 — Women in the Pauline network

### TEXT 7: Judges 4-5 and 2 Kings 22 — Exception, precedent, and the limits of the binary

**~150 words**

My discipline resists the exception/precedent binary because it is a retrospective construction, not a feature of the texts themselves.

[CLAIM: Beth Allison Barr documents that the "exceptional woman" framing — treating Deborah, Huldah, and other women leaders as anomalies that prove the male-leadership rule — is itself a historically specific interpretive move that gained currency in a particular moment of evangelical gender discourse.]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]

What Deborah and Huldah establish sociologically is that the earliest communities who preserved and transmitted these texts did not find female authority in the highest registers narratively problematic. The apologetic work — explaining why these women don't count — is later, not original.
