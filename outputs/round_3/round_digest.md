**Round 3 Digest — The Seven Required Texts**

*One sentence per advocate on their main argument this round:*
- **Biblical Scholar:** The creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is real but its universal application remains unresolved, *authenteō* is genuinely contested between negative and neutral readings, and 1 Corinthians 11:5's assumption of women's public prophecy creates a canonical constraint on how the restriction texts can be read.
- **Reception Historian:** The Vulgate rendered *authenteō* as *dominari* for fifteen hundred years, the "exception" framing of Deborah is an interpretive imposition without textual warrant, and the pattern across Romans 16 is recognition of women's authority followed by deliberate non-extension as precedent.
- **Hermeneutician:** The principle "creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms" cannot be applied to 1 Timothy 2 without also applying it to 1 Corinthians 11, and Alderwood has not supplied a principled distinction between the two passages.
- **Systematic Theologian:** Alderwood has not resolved whether its elder restriction is grounded in ontological status, functional role, or relational recognition — and each option requires a different doctrine of ministry the document does not provide.
- **Pastoral Theologian:** Presenting a restriction built on a single lexically contested hapax legomenon (*authenteō*) with the confidence of settled conviction imposes pastoral cost on women without honest disclosure of the evidentiary uncertainty.
- **Social and Cultural Analyst:** Paul's creation-order rhetoric in 1 Timothy 2 was itself a standard first-century Greco-Roman argumentative move, meaning the claim that it transcends culture underestimates how deeply such appeals were culturally embedded.

*Key tension this round:* Whether 1 Corinthians 11:5's assumption of women's public prophecy functions as a canonical constraint that limits how 1 Timothy 2:12 can be read, or whether the two texts address sufficiently distinct situations to permit differential treatment.
