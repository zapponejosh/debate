# RECEPTION HISTORIAN — ROUND 1 OPENING STATEMENT

---

## Statement of Disciplinary Limits

Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time in response to forces that are not purely exegetical. It cannot show that a reading is therefore wrong — texts can be misread consistently without that making the misreading correct, and a reading that emerges in a specific cultural moment is not invalidated by that emergence alone. My discipline cannot establish what the texts mean — that is the biblical scholar's work. It cannot adjudicate whether the history of a reading counts as evidence against its validity — that is the hermeneutician's work. What I can do is put the current reading in its actual historical context and ask: do you know where this reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

---

## I. What Reception History Affirms in Alderwood

Alderwood's document is more historically self-aware than most comparable church statements. Its acknowledgment that "tradition and structures that restrict women from using their gifts do not glorify Jesus and should be reformed wherever they are encountered" is not a minor concession — it is a remarkable one, because it implicitly admits that the tradition has, in fact, produced unjust restriction. My discipline affirms this admission and will press on it.

I also note, in response to the Biblical Scholar's excellent paper, that the Roman 16 evidence she raises — Phoebe as *diakonos*, Junia among the apostles — is not merely exegetical data. It is reception data. The history of how those designations were read, obscured, and recovered is itself significant. I will return to this.

---

## II. The Document's Historical Thinness

Alderwood's argument is almost entirely synchronic. It moves from ancient text to present application while largely bypassing two thousand years of actual use. This is not a minor gap. The history of how these texts have functioned — what they authorized, what they prohibited, who interpreted them and why — is substantial evidence the document does not examine.

The document presents its reading as what the Bible teaches. Reception history asks a prior question: whose reading of the Bible, formed when, under what conditions?

---

## III. Three Findings the Document Does Not Examine

### Finding One: The Early Epigraphic and Documentary Record

The picture of uniformly male leadership from the earliest period is a retrospective construction.

[CLAIM: Pliny the Younger's letter to Trajan, dated approximately 112 CE, refers to two women called *ministrae* whom he interrogated about Christian practice]
[SOURCE: Pliny the Younger, *Epistulae* X.96, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: *Ministrae* is the Latin equivalent of *diakonos*; Roman officials encountered women in formal ministerial roles sufficiently early that they appear in imperial correspondence within a generation of the apostolic period]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Inscriptions and documentary sources from the early centuries attest women holding the titles *presbytera*, *diakonissa*, and in limited cases *episkopa* in various Christian communities]
[SOURCE: Kevin Madigan and Carolyn Osiek, *Ordained Women in the Early Church*, Johns Hopkins University Press, 2005]
[ARGUMENT: Madigan and Osiek argue that this evidence, while not uniform, cannot be dismissed as purely honorific — it attests real ministerial function in at least some early communities, and its geographic distribution suggests it was not anomalous]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Ute Eisen's survey of women officeholders in early Christianity documents women described as apostles, prophets, teachers, presbyters, and deacons in texts spanning the first through fourth centuries]
[SOURCE: Ute Eisen, *Women Officeholders in Early Christianity*, Liturgical Press, 2000]
[ARGUMENT: Eisen argues that the evidence is sufficient to establish that women held recognized offices in early Christian communities, and that the subsequent restriction of these roles was a historical development rather than the original pattern]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This matters for the Alderwood document specifically. Alderwood asserts that "the instructions to churches in the New Testament... assume and instruct that elders of the local churches will be men." Reception history asks: is that what the earliest communities understood those instructions to require? The epigraphic record suggests the answer is more complicated than the document implies.

### Finding Two: The Genealogy of Contemporary Complementarianism

[CLAIM: The Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood was founded in 1987 and the Danvers Statement issued in 1988, explicitly as a response to what its founders identified as feminist influence on evangelical churches]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: Barr documents that the specific form of complementarianism Alderwood inherits was shaped not only by exegetical conviction but by a reactive cultural and political moment — second-wave feminism, the culture wars of the Reagan era, and anxieties about gender that were not primarily theological in origin]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: The translation of *authenteō* in 1 Timothy 2:12 shifted in major English translations between the 1970s and 1990s toward stronger renderings that supported the emerging complementarian position]
[SOURCE: Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: Translation choices track interpretive communities; the shift in *authenteō* renderings is evidence that the translations were shaped by the theological moment, not merely by fresh lexical analysis]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The Biblical Scholar has already flagged the *authenteō* dispute as genuinely contested. Reception history adds a layer: the contest did not happen in a vacuum. The movement toward stronger renderings coincided precisely with the institutionalization of complementarianism as a movement position. That timing is data.

Alderwood's document lists among its proponents Piper, Grudem, Schreiner, and the CBMW. These are not simply exegetes — they are architects of a movement that emerged in a specific cultural moment. That does not make them wrong. It means their readings cannot be presented as though they arise directly and transparently from Scripture, independent of context.

### Finding Three: The Medieval Gap and the Reformation Contraction

This finding is one the Alderwood document cannot see because it does not look.

[CLAIM: Medieval abbesses in certain double monasteries exercised quasi-episcopal authority, including jurisdiction over male clergy and in some cases liturgical functions normally reserved for ordained men]
[SOURCE: Gary Macy, *The Hidden History of Women's Ordination*, Crossroad, 2007]
[ARGUMENT: Macy argues that the medieval church's actual practice was considerably more varied than its official doctrine, and that women's ministerial roles were progressively restricted through the high medieval period as a result of the clericalization of ministry — a process driven by institutional rather than purely exegetical forces]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

More counterintuitively: the Reformation, often presented as a recovery of biblical authority, resulted in *greater* restriction of women's roles in many Protestant communities than had existed in late medieval Catholicism.

[CLAIM: Lyndal Roper's study of the Reformation in Augsburg documents that the Reformation's closure of convents eliminated the primary institutional space in which women had exercised recognized spiritual authority, teaching, and leadership]
[SOURCE: Lyndal Roper, *The Holy Household*, Oxford University Press, 1989]
[ARGUMENT: Roper argues that the Reformation's reordering of religious life around the household and the male pastor contracted rather than expanded women's institutional religious roles, despite the Reformation's formal emphasis on the priesthood of all believers]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a significant datum for any reading that presents current restriction as simply what Scripture teaches. The Reformation was a moment of intense biblical re-engagement — and its effect on women's roles was largely restrictive. That requires explanation. The explanation is not purely exegetical.

## IV. The Inconvenient Finding

I am required to state what my discipline finds inconvenient.

The history of women's leadership in the church is not a simple story of progressive restriction awaiting modern liberation. The patristic record includes genuine theological arguments for restriction that are not reducible to misogyny.

[CLAIM: Chrysostom, in his Homily 31 on Romans, expressed admiration for Junia being counted among the apostles — yet in other homilies argued against women teaching men in public assemblies]
[SOURCE: John Chrysostom, *Homilies on Romans*, Homily 31; *Homilies on 1 Timothy*, primary sources]
[ARGUMENT: Chrysostom's simultaneous recognition of women's apostolic roles in the New Testament period and maintenance of restriction in his own context demonstrates that restriction was not based on ignorance of the evidence but on theological conviction about order — a conviction held by interpreters who knew the texts well]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

More methodologically inconvenient: Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza's reconstructive project — which argues for a recoverable egalitarian early Christianity beneath the canonical texts — has been foundational to feminist biblical scholarship but is methodologically contested.

[CLAIM: Schüssler Fiorenza's *In Memory of Her* employs a hermeneutic of suspicion that treats the canonical New Testament as a patriarchal suppression of an earlier, more egalitarian Jesus movement]
[SOURCE: Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza, *In Memory of Her*, Crossroad, 1983]
[ARGUMENT: This method has been criticized — including by sympathetic historians — for building a reconstruction on evidence the method itself has declared unreliable; it cannot serve as a foundation for reception history without importing its contested hermeneutical commitments]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Reception history cannot show that there was a pristine egalitarian origin that restriction corrupted. The early evidence is genuinely mixed. Any account that flattens that complexity — in either direction — is doing advocacy, not history.

---

## V. The Central Question I Put to This Debate

The Social and Cultural Analyst and I are working adjacent ground, and I note that her finding about cross-cultural reproduction of male-leadership structures is a genuine complication of the genealogical argument. I accept it as such.

But the genealogical argument retains force even so. The specific *form* that restriction takes in late twentieth-century American evangelicalism — the particular texts foregrounded, the particular translations chosen, the particular institutional structures built — is not simply the universal church's reading. It is a reading with a history. And that history includes forces that are not purely exegetical.

The question I put to Alderwood, and to every position in this debate, is not: *is your reading wrong because it has a history?* It is: *do you know where your reading came from, and does knowing that change anything about how you hold it?*

A community that holds a position without knowing its history is not holding it freely. It is held by it.
