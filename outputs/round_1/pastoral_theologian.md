# ROUND 1 OPENING STATEMENT — PASTORAL THEOLOGIAN

---

## Statement of Limits

Pastoral theology can show that a position has harmful effects or good fruit, but fruitfulness is not the final criterion for theological truth — and harm, while morally serious, does not by itself settle exegetical questions. I resist both the move that ignores human cost entirely and the move that makes human cost the only thing that matters. What I cannot establish alone: what Scripture requires (the biblical scholar's work), what the doctrine actually is (the systematician's work), or whether the pastoral concern I am raising is actually overriding honest engagement with the text (the hermeneutician's work). I need those disciplines to keep me honest. What I bring to them is the insistence that a theological position that produces systematic harm and calls it faithfulness must answer for that harm — and that answer must be more than an appeal to transcultural principle.

---

## I. What I Affirm in the Alderwood Document — and Where the Affirmation Gets Complicated

Alderwood's document contains a sentence I want to read aloud before I say anything critical: *"The roadblocks and hindrances that have unnecessarily kept women from leading and teaching in the church have only served to hurt the body as a whole."* And then: *"Tradition and structures that restrict women from using their gifts do not glorify Jesus and should be reformed wherever they are encountered."*

This is not boilerplate. A document that maintains a restriction while naming the broader pattern as harmful — and calling that pattern *sin* — has done something pastorally significant. Alderwood deserves credit for it. I affirm it without qualification.

Then I have to push on it hard. Because if the harm has been real, and if tradition and sin are genuinely difficult to disentangle — which the document implies — then the reparative obligation is not discharged by removing future barriers. It requires asking, with evidence and accountability, which inherited structures are themselves products of the sin being repented of. Alderwood does not ask that question. It asks women to trust that this particular restriction is on the right side of that line. That is a significant pastoral ask, and it requires more than assertion to sustain.

---

## II. The Formation Question — What Does This Practice Actually Teach?

I want to name a question that is underused in this debate and is mine to carry: not what the doctrinal statement says, but what the practice *forms* people to believe.

[CLAIM: Embodied communal practices shape the imagination and desires of a community more powerfully than doctrinal statements, forming what people believe about God through what they habitually do and see]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Smith argues that human beings are formed primarily by liturgical practices — the repeated, embodied rituals of communal life — and that these practices communicate theology at a level deeper than conscious doctrinal instruction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

When a congregation gathers Sunday after Sunday and the person who holds final doctrinal authority is always male — when the elder board that guards the community's life is exclusively male — that congregation is being formed to associate ultimate spiritual authority with maleness. The doctrinal statement can insist on equal dignity. The practice is teaching something else. This is not a claim about intent. It is a claim about formation, and it operates below the level of what anyone consciously intends.

The question this raises for Alderwood is not rhetorical. It is: what are your children learning about who God is and who gets to speak for God? And is that what you intend to teach them?

---

## III. The Harm-Abuse Connection — Pastoral Data That Cannot Be Dismissed

The Biblical Scholar has noted that the exegetical evidence is genuinely inconclusive at the level of church polity. The Hermeneutician has shown that the consistency problem is unresolved. The Reception Historian has traced the genealogy of the contemporary complementarian position. I want to add a layer of evidence that none of those disciplines is positioned to carry.

[CLAIM: Hierarchical authority structures that concentrate power without adequate accountability create structural conditions under which spiritual abuse is more likely to occur and less likely to be named]
[SOURCE: Diane Langberg, *Redeeming Power*, Brazos Press, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Langberg argues that abuse in Christian contexts is fundamentally a misuse of power, and that any system — including gender-exclusive leadership — that concentrates authority without structural accountability creates environments where the powerful are protected and the harmed are silenced]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Toxic church cultures are enabled by systems that prioritize institutional reputation over individual dignity, and gender-exclusive authority structures contribute to this by limiting the range of voices that can name and challenge institutional failure]
[SOURCE: Scot McKnight and Laura Barringer, *A Church Called Tov*, Tyndale House, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: McKnight and Barringer document patterns across multiple high-profile cases in which the absence of women from authoritative positions was one structural factor in how abuse was concealed and how institutions protected themselves rather than the harmed]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

I am not arguing that complementarian churches are uniformly abusive. I am arguing that structural conditions matter, and that the pastoral and ethical burden falls on any church that maintains those conditions to demonstrate — with evidence, not aspiration — that it has built the accountability structures that mitigate the risk.

## IV. The Calling Question — What the Pastoral Promise Requires

The Systematician has asked what kind of authority the elder exercises and where it comes from. I want to ask a different question that is equally pressing: what does it mean pastorally to tell a gifted woman that she can preach on Sunday, lead a staff team, carry the title "pastor" — but cannot be an elder?

[CLAIM: The pastoral cost of restriction falls with particular severity on women who experience genuine vocational calling, and the church's typical response — affirming the gift while closing the vocational shape — is insufficient as pastoral care]
[SOURCE: Ruth Tucker, *Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*, Zondervan, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Tucker argues from personal experience and documented observation that the experience of calling is not reducible to a list of gifts; it has a shape, a direction, a sense of fit between person and role that cannot be redirected without pastoral cost that must be owned]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Alderwood promises a culture of flourishing. It promises to remove hindrances. It gives women the title of pastor. These are significant promises. What the evidence from comparable contexts suggests is that promises of this kind are extraordinarily difficult to keep within a structure that reserves final authority for men — not because of bad faith, but because the structural logic of the restriction tends to expand over time, not contract.

[CLAIM: Beth Allison Barr documents that the pastoral and institutional costs of restriction have historically been borne disproportionately by women, and that churches which promise flourishing within restriction have rarely produced the evidence to sustain that promise]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: Barr argues that the pattern across evangelical complementarian contexts is one of promised flourishing and actual narrowing, as the logic of restriction expands beyond its stated limits]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

---

## V. Initial Picture of Faithful Practice — Provisional

What does my discipline suggest, provisionally? Not a settled conclusion — the exegetical and doctrinal questions are genuinely open, as my colleagues have shown — but a set of pastoral obligations that any position must discharge.

A church that maintains restriction must demonstrate, not merely assert: that the formation its practices produce is consistent with what it claims to believe about God and human dignity; that it has built structural accountability that does not depend on the goodwill of the powerful; that the promises made to gifted women are kept, with evidence across years and not merely at the point of hire; and that it has genuinely asked which of its inherited structures are products of the sin it has repented of, not merely which future barriers it will remove.

These are not conditions that resolve the exegetical question. They are conditions for pastoral integrity while the exegetical question remains open. And they are far more demanding than most churches maintaining restriction have recognized.
