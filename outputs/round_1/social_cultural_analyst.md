# ROUND 1 — OPENING STATEMENT
## Social and Cultural Analyst

---

### STATEMENT OF LIMITS

Social analysis can show that a reading emerged in a particular social context and serves particular social interests. It cannot show that the reading is therefore wrong — texts can have genuine transcultural authority even when interpreted in socially situated ways, and even readings that serve the interests of the powerful can be correct. If all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them. My discipline's greatest risk is explaining everything and thereby explaining nothing. I need the biblical scholar to establish what the texts actually say and the hermeneutician to adjudicate how much social location counts as evidence against a reading's validity. What I bring is this: a position that cannot examine its own social conditioning, or that claims to have transcended social situatedness while everyone else is trapped by it, has not yet done its intellectual homework.

---

### I. THE CENTRAL ANALYTICAL QUESTION

Alderwood's document contains an admission that opens more than it closes: *"Much of what has kept women from flourishing and using their gifts within the church has not been the theology of the church but has been the sin of sexism and discrimination that is the result of the Fall."*

This is sociologically honest. But it creates a question the document does not ask: if theology and social forces can be separated in the *application* of the position — sexism is sin, genuine restriction is theology — can we be equally confident they can be separated in the *formation* of the position itself? A position shaped by social forces at its origin cannot simply declare itself clean of those forces at the level of application. My discipline's task is to press that question with specificity, not as a verdict, but as intellectual homework the document has not yet done.

### II. THE GENEALOGY OF MODERN COMPLEMENTARIANISM

The Reception Historian has already noted the CBMW's 1987 founding and the *Danvers Statement* of 1988. I want to press further into what Du Mez's documentation actually shows, because it is more specific than a general observation about cultural influence.

[CLAIM: The modern complementarian movement emerged in the late 1980s not primarily from fresh engagement with biblical texts but in direct entanglement with American conservative political culture, Cold War anxieties about male leadership, and the construction of a specifically militant Christian masculinity.]
[SOURCE: Kristin Kobes Du Mez, *Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Du Mez documents that evangelical gender ideology across the twentieth century was inseparable from specific cultural projects — the anxious reassertion of male authority in response to second-wave feminism, the alignment of evangelical masculinity with Cold War toughness, and the culture wars of the 1980s. The CBMW was a reactive institution, and its founding documents reflect that reaction.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This matters because Alderwood's position paper cites Piper, Grudem, Schreiner, and the CBMW as the intellectual framework for its own "Male Elder Complementarian" category. These are not simply exegetes. They are movement architects operating within a specific social moment, producing documents shaped by that moment's anxieties. That does not make their exegesis wrong. It means their work cannot be read as socially neutral, and a church that inherits their framework without examining that genealogy has not yet done what Alderwood's own intellectual honesty requires.

I note that the Reception Historian flags this same genealogy. My contribution is to specify the mechanism: it is not merely that the CBMW emerged in a cultural context. It is that the specific questions asked, the specific texts foregrounded, and the specific institutional structures defended were shaped by forces that had nothing to do with first-century Ephesus or Corinth.

### III. THE SOCIAL WORLD THE TEXTS INHABITED

The biblical scholar correctly identifies that the Pauline restriction texts are real and cannot be dissolved by hermeneutical fiat. I do not contest that. What I contribute is the social world those texts inhabited — because reading them without that world produces precisely the misreading my discipline exists to prevent.

**Ephesus:**

[CLAIM: The city of Ephesus in the first century was home to the dominant cult of Artemis, whose temple was one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world, and whose religious economy gave women unusual cultic prominence and public religious authority in that specific urban context.]
[SOURCE: Sandra Glahn, *Nobody's Mother: Artemis of the Ephesians in Antiquity and the New Testament*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Glahn argues that the Artemis cult and its specific social pressures on the Ephesian church are essential context for reading 1 Timothy. The community Paul addresses was navigating a religious environment in which female religious authority was the dominant cultural norm — which significantly complicates any reading of 1 Timothy 2 as a transcultural principle rather than a contextually specific negotiation.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutician has identified the consistency problem: Paul grounds head coverings in creation order in 1 Corinthians 11, yet virtually no complementarian applies that as transcultural. My social-scientific contribution to that problem is this: both texts are embedded in specific social worlds — Corinth's honor/shame dynamics around women's public appearance, Ephesus's specific religious environment — and the creation-order appeals in both cases are made by an author who is also navigating those social worlds. The question of whether the creation-order appeal lifts the instruction out of its social context, or whether the appeal itself is socially shaped, cannot be settled by noting that the appeal exists.

**The Household Codes:**

[CLAIM: In the first-century Mediterranean world, the oikos was the basic unit of social organization, and the New Testament household codes engaged directly with Greco-Roman conventions for ordering the household, including the subordination of wives, children, and slaves to the paterfamilias.]
[SOURCE: Bruce Malina and Richard Rohrbaugh, *Social-Science Commentary on the Letters of Paul*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Paul's household instructions are intelligible only within the honor/shame framework of Mediterranean social life, where a household that violated conventional order brought shame on the community associated with it. The codes are not simply endorsements of Greco-Roman hierarchy — they contain subversive reframings — but they are also not independent of that hierarchy.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Carolyn Osiek's work on women's actual social locations in early Christian households adds texture here:

[CLAIM: Women in early Christian communities occupied a range of social positions — from wealthy patrons who hosted house churches, to slaves with no legal standing — and the New Testament instructions addressed to "women" were not addressed to a socially homogeneous group.]
[SOURCE: Carolyn Osiek, *A Woman's Place: House Churches in Earliest Christianity*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Osiek argues that the diversity of women's social locations in early Christianity means that instructions addressed to "women" in the Pauline letters were received differently by different women, and that flattening this diversity into a single universal principle about women's roles misreads the social world the texts inhabited.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

### IV. BOURDIEU AND THE QUESTION SINCERITY CANNOT ANSWER

The systematic theologian raises the question of what kind of authority the elder exercises. The pastoral theologian raises the formation question. I want to introduce a framework that connects both: Bourdieu's concept of symbolic violence.

[CLAIM: Pierre Bourdieu's concept of "symbolic violence" describes the process by which hierarchies are maintained not through overt coercion but through the internalization of the hierarchy by those subject to it, such that subordination is experienced as natural, willed, or spiritually appropriate.]
[SOURCE: Pierre Bourdieu, *Masculine Domination*, Stanford University Press, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Bourdieu argues that the most durable forms of domination are those in which the dominated participate in their own domination through categories of perception that are themselves products of the domination. The sociologically significant question is not whether women in complementarian churches sincerely believe their restriction is God's will — many do — but whether that sincere belief can serve as its own validation.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is the hardest thing I bring to this debate, and I want to be precise about what it does and does not establish. It does not establish that complementarian women are deceived or that their sincere belief is false. It establishes that sincerity is not self-validating as a sociological matter, because sincerely internalized hierarchies are precisely what symbolic violence produces. A church that points to women's willing acceptance of restriction as evidence that the restriction is just has not yet engaged this problem. Alderwood's document does not engage it.

The pastoral theologian's formation question — what does this practice teach about who God is? — is the lived-experience version of the same problem. My discipline adds the structural dimension: the formation does not require anyone to intend it.

### V. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE POINTS TOWARD — PROVISIONALLY

My discipline does not determine whether women should serve as elders. What it establishes, with reasonable confidence, is a set of questions any position must answer before it can claim intellectual honesty:

First, has the position examined its own genealogy? Alderwood's framework was formed in a specific cultural moment by movement architects responding to specific cultural anxieties. That does not make it wrong. It means it should know where it came from.

Second, has the position engaged the social world of the texts it reads? The Ephesian and Corinthian contexts are not decorative background. They are the social world inside which Paul's instructions were written and first received. Reading those instructions as transcultural without engaging that world is not careful exegesis — it is exegesis that has skipped a step.

Third, can the position distinguish between the theology it is defending and the social forces that have always surrounded it? Alderwood's own concession — that sexism, not theology, has driven much of the restriction — makes this question unavoidable. A position that can name the sin in the application but not examine the social forces in the formation has done half the work.

I hold these findings as evidence to be weighed, not as verdicts to be delivered. The biblical scholar and hermeneutician must carry weight my discipline cannot. But a position that has not passed the test of social self-awareness is not yet fully honest — and full honesty is what this inquiry requires.
