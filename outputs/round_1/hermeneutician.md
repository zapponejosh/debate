# ROUND 1 — OPENING STATEMENT
## The Hermeneutician
### *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

---

## STATEMENT OF LIMITS

Hermeneutics can expose inconsistency and interrogate method. It can show that the same principle is applied selectively without saying whether the principle should be applied universally or abandoned. What it cannot do alone: determine which hermeneutical approach is ultimately correct — that requires theological and philosophical commitments that go beyond hermeneutics itself. I can show that the creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not applied consistently elsewhere. I cannot tell you from hermeneutics alone whether it should be applied universally or treated as culturally situated. For that I need the systematician's doctrine of scriptural authority and the biblical scholar's textual work. My discipline is a tool for honest inquiry, not a position in the debate.

---

## I. WHAT THE BIBLICAL SCHOLAR AND RECEPTION HISTORIAN HAVE ESTABLISHED

My colleagues have done significant work. The biblical scholar has correctly identified that the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is a real exegetical feature that cannot be dissolved — Paul is doing something with protological grounding that must be engaged rather than explained away. That is honest. The reception historian has correctly identified that contemporary complementarianism has a traceable genealogy in the late twentieth century, that the epigraphic record is more complex than either standard position acknowledges, and that the gap between official doctrine and actual practice has been wide and persistent. That is also honest.

Both colleagues have, appropriately, handed questions to me. The biblical scholar explicitly: "I cannot determine from exegesis alone whether that grounding settles the question of universal application. That requires the hermeneutician." The reception historian implicitly: knowing where a reading came from does not tell us whether it is valid. My discipline is where both referrals land.

What I want to do now is name the methodological problem precisely, because imprecision here is what allows this debate to continue past the point where it should be generating more light.

## II. THE CONSISTENCY TEST: ALDERWOOD'S CENTRAL VULNERABILITY

Alderwood makes a specific hermeneutical claim about 1 Timothy 2:13. I will quote it exactly:

> "Paul gives his reasoning for this prohibition and he appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations. Whatever the principle is that Paul is applying, it is a principle that transcends individual cultures."

[CLAIM: Alderwood uses Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 as the criterion marking the prohibition as transcultural rather than culturally conditioned]
[SOURCE: Alderwood Community Church, *What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership*, Appendix 1]
[ARGUMENT: The document treats creation-order grounding as the distinguishing marker between culturally situated instructions and universally binding principles]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a hermeneutical principle, not an exegetical finding. The principle is: *creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms*. It is a defensible principle. But it must be applied consistently across the canon, not selectively.

Now apply it to 1 Corinthians 11:8-9:

[CLAIM: Paul grounds the head-covering instruction in 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 in creation order, stating that "man was not made from woman, but woman from man" and "neither was man created for woman, but woman for man"]
[SOURCE: 1 Corinthians 11:8-9, ESV; Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 11:2-16]
[ARGUMENT: The creation-order rationale in 1 Corinthians 11 is structurally identical to the creation-order rationale in 1 Timothy 2:13 — both ground a behavioral norm for women in the sequence and purpose of creation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11's appeal to creation order does not settle whether the practice grounded in that appeal is culturally conditioned, because Paul uses the same argumentative structure for practices most interpreters treat as culturally specific]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee's point is that the presence of a creation-order argument does not automatically confer transcultural status on the practice grounded in it — the argumentative structure and the cultural bindingness of the conclusion are separable questions]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood almost certainly does not require head coverings. This is not a minor point — it is the consistency test, and it is the most important methodological challenge this debate must answer. The differential treatment of 1 Timothy 2:13 and 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 requires a principled explanation.

Three options exist, and I want to be precise about what each costs:

**Option 1:** Accept both as transcultural and require head coverings. This is consistent, but virtually no contemporary church takes this position. Its near-universal rejection is itself evidence that something other than the stated principle is doing the hermeneutical work.

**Option 2:** Argue that the creation-order appeals function differently in each passage in ways the text itself supports — that the appeal in 1 Timothy 2 is more foundational, or more tightly integrated with the prohibition, than the appeal in 1 Corinthians 11. This is a defensible move, but it requires a detailed argument from the texts themselves, and it must not simply reassert the conclusion it is trying to establish.

**Option 3:** Acknowledge that something other than creation-order grounding is doing the real hermeneutical work in both cases — that interpreters are bringing a prior judgment about what is culturally conditioned to both texts, and then deploying creation-order language to ratify conclusions reached by other means. This is the most honest starting point, and it requires naming what that prior judgment is and defending it.

I am not asserting that Option 3 is correct. I am asserting that every advocate in this debate — including Alderwood, including the egalitarian position, including me — is operating somewhere in this space, and that the failure to name which option one is taking is the primary source of unclarity in this debate.

## III. THE MAJOR HERMENEUTICAL OPTIONS AND THEIR GENUINE COSTS

I want to lay out the options honestly, because each has genuine strengths and genuine dangers that their proponents do not always acknowledge.

**Historical-grammatical interpretation** aims to recover the author's intended meaning in its original context. Its strength is that it takes the text's own categories seriously and resists importing alien frameworks. Its danger is that it can mistake the interpreter's cultural assumptions for the text's original meaning — the horizon of the interpreter is not as transparent as the method implies.

[CLAIM: E.D. Hirsch argued that meaning is determinate and lodged in the author's original intention, and that interpretation's goal is to recover that intention, distinguishing "meaning" from "significance"]
[SOURCE: E.D. Hirsch, *Validity in Interpretation*, Yale University Press, 1967]
[ARGUMENT: Hirsch's distinction between meaning and significance preserves textual determinacy while allowing for varied application, but requires that the original meaning be recoverable with sufficient confidence to ground normative claims]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The question Hirsch's framework opens is whether we can recover Paul's intended meaning with sufficient precision to determine whether he intended a universal or local application — and the honest answer, given the *authenteō* dispute and the Ephesian context debate, is that we cannot do so with certainty.

**William Webb's redemptive-movement hermeneutic** is the most directly relevant framework to this debate. Webb argues that the trajectory of Scripture — the direction of ethical movement from ancient Near Eastern context through the New Testament — rather than the static statements of any single text should govern ethical application.

[CLAIM: William Webb proposes eighteen criteria for identifying when a text's ethic is "frozen" at a culturally accommodated point versus expressing the full redemptive ideal, arguing that the NT's teaching on women moves toward greater liberation without reaching the cultural destination]
[SOURCE: William Webb, *Slaves, Women and Homosexuals*, IVP Academic, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Webb's method attempts to provide principled criteria for trajectory extrapolation, distinguishing the culturally accommodated from the transcultural by examining the direction and endpoint of biblical ethical development]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Webb's genuine strength is that it accounts for the slavery analogy honestly — the church went beyond the explicit teaching of the New Testament on slavery, and Webb provides a principled account of why that was legitimate. His genuine danger is identified precisely by his critics:

[CLAIM: Denny Burk argued that Webb's redemptive-movement hermeneutic lacks adequate controls against the interpreter importing contemporary ethical intuitions as the endpoint of the "trajectory," effectively making cultural consensus the hermeneutical authority]
[SOURCE: Denny Burk, response to Webb, *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2003]
[ARGUMENT: If the criterion for moving beyond the text is a constructed trajectory toward greater human liberation or flourishing, the method provides no principled textual stopping point — the interpreter's construction of the trajectory's destination does the real normative work]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I find this criticism partially compelling. But I want to note that it applies with equal force to the hermeneutic that freezes all creation-order texts as transcultural: that method also cannot adjudicate its own application without resources from outside itself — specifically, the prior judgment about which creation-order appeals are culturally conditioned and which are not.

**I. Howard Marshall's more cautious trajectory argument** is hermeneutically more defensible than Webb's precisely because it is less systematized:

[CLAIM: I. Howard Marshall argued that the task of Scripture is not to give final answers but to stimulate and guide moral reasoning in ways that require going "beyond the Bible," with the direction and purpose of biblical instruction as the guide rather than a multi-criteria extrapolation]
[SOURCE: I. Howard Marshall, *Beyond the Bible: Moving from Scripture to Theology*, IVP Academic, 2004]
[ARGUMENT: Marshall's approach is more modest than Webb's — it acknowledges the limits of the method rather than overclaiming its precision, and focuses on the spirit and direction of biblical instruction rather than constructing a trajectory endpoint]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Marshall's version is less vulnerable to Burk's critique because it does not claim to have an eighteen-criteria mechanism for determining the trajectory's destination. Its cost is that it provides less guidance, not more. It tells us we must go beyond the Bible without telling us precisely how far.

## IV. THE SLAVERY ANALOGY: GENUINE FORCE AND GENUINE LIMITS

The slavery analogy has genuine hermeneutical force that cannot be dismissed. The church went beyond the explicit teaching of the New Testament on slavery — which nowhere commands abolition and in several places commands obedience — by appealing to something like a redemptive trajectory. If that move was legitimate, the question of whether the same move applies to gender restrictions cannot be dismissed by simply asserting that Paul grounds his gender teaching in creation order.

But the analogy has genuine limits that its proponents do not always acknowledge:

The New Testament contains no creation-order grounding for slavery. Paul grounds the slavery instructions in household management norms and missional pragmatics. He does not say "slaves obey your masters because God created masters before slaves." The asymmetry between the slavery texts and the gender texts is not decisive — a creation-order appeal could itself be culturally conditioned — but it is real, and a hermeneutic that treats the slavery analogy as fully parallel to the gender question without accounting for this asymmetry is not being fully honest about the evidence.

[CLAIM: Richard Hays argues that the New Testament's moral logic must be read through its narrative logic, attending to the overall story of God's redemptive action rather than extracting propositions from individual texts]
[SOURCE: Richard Hays, *The Moral Vision of the New Testament*, HarperOne, 1996]
[ARGUMENT: Hays's method of moving from text to community to norm requires attending to the focal images of the New Testament — cross, community, new creation — as the organizing framework for ethical application, which may generate different conclusions than either proof-texting or trajectory extrapolation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What Hays's approach requires is asking what the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2 is doing within the larger narrative logic of Pauline theology — and that is a question the biblical scholar and systematician must help answer.

## V. WHAT THE CONSISTENCY TEST REVEALS ABOUT PRE-UNDERSTANDING

[CLAIM: Anthony Thiselton argued that the interpreter's "horizon" inevitably shapes what they find in a text, but that the text's own horizon can challenge and reshape the interpreter's, making genuine understanding possible without claiming neutral objectivity]
[SOURCE: Anthony Thiselton, *The Two Horizons*, Eerdmans, 1980]
[ARGUMENT: Thiselton's appropriation of Gadamer for evangelical hermeneutics establishes that pre-understanding is a condition of interpretation, not a disqualification from it, but that honest interpretation requires the text to be allowed to interrogate the interpreter's assumptions]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Every advocate in this debate reads these texts through a horizon shaped by gender, ecclesial tradition, and cultural location. The reception historian and the social analyst have demonstrated this with precision about the genealogy of contemporary complementarianism. But the egalitarian reading has its own genealogy, its own social location, its own cultural pressures acting on it. Acknowledging this symmetry is not relativism — it is the precondition of honest inquiry.

What the consistency test reveals is not that Alderwood's conclusion is wrong. It reveals that the hermeneutical principle Alderwood states — creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms — is not the principle Alderwood actually applies. Something else is doing the work. The task of this debate is to name that something else and evaluate it honestly.

## VI. INITIAL ORIENTATION: WHAT MY DISCIPLINE POINTS TOWARD

My discipline does not tell you whether women should be elders. It tells you what kind of argument you need to have before you can answer that question responsibly.

That argument requires:

**First**, a consistent account of what distinguishes culturally situated instructions from transcultural principles — an account that applies the same criteria to 1 Corinthians 11 and 1 Timothy 2, to the slavery texts and the gender texts, without special pleading for the conclusion already preferred.

**Second**, a defense of whichever hermeneutical framework is being employed — historical-grammatical, redemptive-movement, canonical whole-Bible, or other — that acknowledges that framework's genuine costs and does not merely invoke its strengths.

**Third**, honesty about where pre-understanding is operating. The reception historian and social analyst have shown that contemporary complementarianism has a genealogy. The hermeneutician must add: so does contemporary egalitarianism. Both readings have horizons. The question is not which reading is socially located — they both are — but which reading, when its horizon is made explicit, survives the encounter with the text most honestly.

**Fourth**, recognition that the hermeneutical question cannot be closed by hermeneutics alone. Where the biblical scholar finds genuine exegetical ambiguity, and where the consistency test reveals that the stated hermeneutical principle is not actually being applied, the systematician's doctrine of scriptural authority and the pastoral theologian's account of formative consequences become load-bearing. My discipline prepares the ground for those disciplines. It does not replace them.

The picture that begins to emerge from my lens is provisional but pointed: Alderwood's position is more methodologically vulnerable than it appears, not because its conclusion is demonstrably wrong, but because the hermeneutical principle it states does not survive the consistency test. A more honest version of Alderwood's position would need to name what is actually doing the hermeneutical work — and defend it. That defense is what the next rounds of this debate must produce.
