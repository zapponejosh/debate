# ROUND 1 OPENING STATEMENT — THE BIBLICAL SCHOLAR
## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

---

### STATEMENT OF LIMITS

Exegesis can establish what texts mean within their canonical context. It can identify where translation choices carry theological weight and insist that those choices be defended. It can ask what the whole-Bible pattern looks like when we resist focusing only on the texts most convenient to our preferred conclusion. What it cannot do alone: determine how much weight a first-century cultural situation should carry in a twenty-first century church; build an ecclesiology from its findings without assistance from systematic theology; adjudicate between two exegetically defensible readings without a hermeneutical principle that itself requires defense. I need the hermeneutician to adjudicate method, the reception historian to test whether my readings have been consistent, and the systematician to build something from what I find.

---

### I. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE AFFIRMS IN THE ALDERWOOD DOCUMENT

Alderwood's treatment of *ezer* is better than most church documents at this level. The document correctly refuses to load "helper" with inferiority, and it correctly observes that *ezer* language is applied to God in contexts of military and saving help.

[CLAIM: The Hebrew *ezer* appears approximately twenty-one times in the Old Testament, and the majority of uses refer either to God or to military allies — contexts carrying no connotation of subordinate status]
[SOURCE: John Goldingay, *Old Testament Theology*, Vol. 2, IVP Academic, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Goldingay notes that *ezer* language applied to God carries connotations of indispensable strength, not secondary status]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood's acknowledgment that 1 Corinthians 11:5 creates genuine tension with 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 is honest and necessary. Its treatment of Phoebe as *diakonos* — acknowledging the term's weight while noting the deacon office is open to women — represents responsible engagement with Romans 16.

---

### II. WHERE MY DISCIPLINE PUSHES HARDER

**The Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles.** Alderwood states that "God consistently calls men to fill the roles of the highest level of spiritual authority" and cites the Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles as evidence. This argument requires exegetical work the document does not supply.

The Levitical priesthood was a typological institution whose specific requirements — tribe, gender, physical wholeness — are not straightforwardly binding on the church. The New Testament explicitly describes the church as a "royal priesthood" of all believers (1 Peter 2:9), a designation that cuts across the Levitical categories entirely. The selection of twelve male apostles corresponding to the twelve tribes carries typological freight — it is a gesture toward Israel's reconstitution — not a gender policy for church office.

[CLAIM: The twelve apostles were chosen as a symbolic reconstitution of Israel's twelve tribes, which is a typological function inseparable from their Jewishness]
[SOURCE: N.T. Wright, "Women's Service in the Church: The Biblical Basis," NTWrightPage.com]
[ARGUMENT: Wright argues the maleness of the Twelve is inseparable from their function as the new patriarchs of Israel; applying it as a gender rule for church leadership requires a hermeneutical step the text does not authorize]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Alderwood makes this argument without supplying that step. My discipline requires it.

**The creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13.** Alderwood asserts that Paul's appeal to creation order — rather than cultural considerations — means the prohibition "transcends individual cultures." This is a significant hermeneutical claim presented as an exegetical finding.

The problem: Paul uses creation-order grounding in 1 Corinthians 11:8–9 to ground the head-covering instruction, citing that "man was not made from woman, but woman from man." This is structurally identical to the argument in 1 Timothy 2:13. Virtually no complementarian applies head coverings as universally binding. Alderwood cannot assert that creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2:12 transcultural without explaining why the same logic does not bind churches to head coverings. This is a gap in the document's argument — not a refutation of its conclusion, but a gap that must be filled.

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11's appeal to creation order does not settle whether the practice grounded in that appeal is culturally conditioned]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee argues Paul uses the same argumentative structure for practices most interpreters treat as culturally specific, so creation-order grounding alone cannot determine universal normativity]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I want to be equally honest in the other direction: the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 cannot be dissolved. Paul is doing what he appears to be doing — grounding a behavioral norm in protological order. Egalitarian readings that wave past verse 13 as though it were not there are not more honest than complementarian readings that wave past 1 Corinthians 11. Both must account for the parallel.

**Deborah and Huldah — the absent witnesses.** Alderwood does not mention Deborah. Canonically, this is a significant omission. Judges 4–5 presents Deborah as prophet, judge, and military commander without narrative apology. The Song of Deborah (Judges 5) is among the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible. The text does not call her an exception; that characterization requires argument Alderwood does not supply.

[CLAIM: Huldah the prophet (2 Kings 22) was consulted by Josiah's male officials for authoritative interpretation of the law, despite Jeremiah and Zephaniah being active contemporaries]
[SOURCE: Richard Bauckham, *Gospel Women*, Eerdmans, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Bauckham notes that Huldah's role as the authoritative interpreter of the newly discovered law is not explained or apologized for by the text, and that the presence of male prophets makes her selection theologically significant rather than merely circumstantial]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

### III. THE LEXICALLY CONTESTED GROUND

**Romans 16.** Phoebe is designated *diakonos* — the masculine form, the same term Paul uses of himself and of male deacons. Translating it "servant" when applied to Phoebe while rendering it "deacon" when applied to men is a translation choice carrying argumentative weight that must be defended, not assumed.

[CLAIM: Phoebe is also called *prostatis* in Romans 16:2, a term that in its cognate form refers to a patron or leader, not merely a helper]
[SOURCE: Nijay Gupta, *Tell Her Story*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Gupta argues that *prostatis* carries connotations of patronal authority and leadership, and that rendering it "helper" imports a diminutive the Greek does not require]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Junia in Romans 16:7 is most naturally read as a woman described as "outstanding among the apostles." The attempt to read the name as male (*Junias*) lacks manuscript support and appears to be driven by the theological difficulty of a female apostle.

[CLAIM: Junia is an unambiguously attested female Latin name; the masculine form *Junias* has no independent attestation in ancient sources]
[SOURCE: Richard Bauckham, *Gospel Women*, Eerdmans, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Bauckham argues that the attempt to read Junia as male is driven by theological commitment rather than linguistic evidence, and that the phrase *en tois apostolois* most naturally includes her among the apostles rather than merely commending her to them]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**1 Timothy 2:12 — *authenteō*.** This verb appears once in the New Testament. Its meaning in the first century is genuinely contested among specialists with serious lexical competence on both sides.

[CLAIM: Philip Payne surveys pre-second-century usage and argues *authenteō* carries a negative or domineering connotation, which would change the force of Paul's prohibition significantly]
[SOURCE: Philip Payne, *Man and Woman, One in Christ*, Zondervan, 2009]
[ARGUMENT: If *authenteō* means "to domineer" rather than "to exercise authority," Paul may be prohibiting abusive authority rather than all authority]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Al Wolters surveys a broader range of cognates and argues the term does not carry an inherently negative force]
[SOURCE: Al Wolters, "A Semantic Study of *authentes* and its Derivatives," *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Wolters' reading supports a more general authority prohibition, strengthening the complementarian reading]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Both positions have serious lexical warrant. I will not pretend otherwise.

### IV. WHAT THE CANONICAL PICTURE LOOKS LIKE

The Old Testament presents women in prophetic, judicial, and teaching roles without narrative condemnation — Deborah, Huldah, Miriam, the daughters of Zelophehad. The new-covenant inauguration at Pentecost cites Joel 2 explicitly: "your daughters will prophesy" and "my female servants" will receive the Spirit (Acts 2:17–18). This is not incidental. It is the canonical announcement of the new-covenant community's character.

The New Testament presents women as deacons (Phoebe), apostles in some sense (Junia), prophets (1 Corinthians 11:5), teachers (Priscilla, Acts 18:26), and patrons of missionary work (Romans 16). The Pauline restriction texts are real, are grounded in creation-order appeals, and cannot be dismissed as purely local without a hermeneutical argument that is not itself exegetical. But those same texts are lexically contested at precisely the points that determine their scope.

**My initial finding:** The exegetical evidence does not unambiguously establish either a universal male-only leadership norm or an undifferentiated egalitarianism. The strongest exegetical case for restriction operates at the level of a specific authoritative-teaching-and-adjudicating function — something like what Alderwood calls the elder's role — rather than all leadership or all public speech. But that case depends on resolving *authenteō* in the complementarian direction and treating the Levitical/apostolic precedents as gender paradigms rather than typological institutions — neither of which is as settled as Alderwood implies.

**What I need from the other disciplines:** The hermeneutician must adjudicate the consistency problem — why creation-order grounding is transcultural in 1 Timothy 2 but not in 1 Corinthians 11. The systematician must tell me what kind of authority the elder exercises and where it comes from, because the answer to that question changes which texts are decisive. The reception historian must test whether my readings of Junia and Phoebe are consistent with how these figures have been read across history — and if not, whether that inconsistency is evidence of exegetical error or of theological pressure distorting the reading.

I note the social analyst's point about the Ephesian context and Sandra Glahn's work on the Artemis cult. This is relevant to 1 Timothy 2, and I will need to engage it more fully in subsequent rounds. The pastoral theologian's formation question — what does it teach when only men hold final authority? — is not an exegetical question, but it is a question the exegete must hold honestly while doing the work.
