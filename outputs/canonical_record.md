# Pre-Debate — Position Papers

## The Biblical Scholar

# BIBLICAL SCHOLAR'S PRE-DEBATE POSITION PAPER
## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

---

### STATEMENT OF LIMITS (read aloud — maximum 150 words)

Exegesis can establish what texts mean within their canonical context. It can identify where translation choices carry theological weight and insist that those choices be defended. It can ask what the whole-Bible pattern looks like when we resist focusing only on the texts most convenient to our preferred conclusion. What it cannot do alone: determine how much weight a first-century cultural situation should carry in a twenty-first century church; build an ecclesiology from its findings without assistance from systematic theology; adjudicate between two exegetically defensible readings without a hermeneutical principle that itself requires defense. I need the hermeneutician to adjudicate method, the reception historian to test whether my readings have been consistent, and the systematician to build something from what I find.

---

### I. DISCIPLINARY METHOD AND CORE ASSUMPTIONS

My method is canonical exegesis with full attention to original languages. I operate with the following commitments:

First, texts must be read in their literary context before being read in their canonical context. A verse does not mean something in Romans 16 that it cannot mean in Romans 16.

Second, translation choices are arguments, not neutral renderings. When a Greek word is contested — *authenteō*, *kephalē*, *ezer*, *diakonos*, *prostatis* — the translation selected by a commentary or church document is already a position in the debate. I require those choices to be defended lexically, not merely asserted.

Third, the Old Testament carries full canonical weight. The gravitational pull of the "problem texts" in the Pauline corpus — 1 Timothy 2, 1 Corinthians 14 — has produced a distorted debate. Genesis 1–2, Judges 4–5, Joel 2, and the Psalms belong to this inquiry as much as the Pastoral Epistles do.

Fourth, genuine scholarly contestation must be acknowledged as such. Where two careful exegetes read the same Greek differently and both have lexical warrant, I will not pretend the matter is settled.

---

### II. ENGAGEMENT WITH THE ALDERWOOD DOCUMENT

**What my discipline affirms:**

Alderwood's treatment of *ezer* (Genesis 2:18) is better than most church documents.

[CLAIM: The Hebrew word *ezer* is used of God in contexts of military and saving help, not subordinate assistance]
[SOURCE: John Goldingay, *Old Testament Theology*, Vol. 2, IVP Academic, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Goldingay notes that *ezer* language applied to God carries connotations of indispensable strength, not secondary status]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood correctly refuses to load "helper" with inferiority. That is good lexical work.

Alderwood's acknowledgment that 1 Corinthians 11:5 creates genuine tension with 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 is honest and necessary. Almost no church document at this level of pastoral accessibility engages that tension directly.

Alderwood's distinction between elder and pastor in Appendix 2, and its willingness to allow women the title "pastor" while restricting eldership, represents a serious attempt to read the New Testament's office-language carefully rather than collapsing all terms into one.

**What my discipline questions:**

*The Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles as straightforward precedents.* Alderwood states: "After Genesis 2, God consistently calls men to fill the roles of the highest level of spiritual authority. The Levitical priesthood… God only appoints male kings of Israel… Jesus called 12 men to form his inner circle."

This argument requires far more exegetical work than Alderwood provides. The Levitical priesthood was a typological institution whose specific requirements — tribe, gender, physical wholeness — are not straightforwardly binding on the church, which the New Testament explicitly describes as a "royal priesthood" of all believers (1 Peter 2:9). The selection of twelve male apostles corresponding to the twelve tribes of Israel is a typological gesture toward Israel's reconstitution, not a gender policy.

[CLAIM: The twelve apostles were chosen as a symbolic reconstitution of Israel's twelve tribes, which is a typological function, not a gender paradigm]
[SOURCE: N.T. Wright, "Women's Service in the Church: The Biblical Basis," NTWrightPage.com]
[ARGUMENT: Wright argues the maleness of the Twelve is inseparable from their Jewishness and their function as the new patriarchs of Israel; applying it as a gender rule for church leadership requires a hermeneutical step the text does not authorize]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Alderwood makes this argument without that hermeneutical step. My discipline requires it to be supplied.

*The creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13.* Alderwood asserts that Paul "appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations" and that "whatever the principle is that Paul is applying, it is a principle that transcends individual cultures." This is a significant hermeneutical claim presented as an exegetical finding. But the claim that creation-order appeals in Paul are always trans-cultural is contested.

[CLAIM: Paul uses creation-order arguments in 1 Corinthians 11 to ground a requirement for head coverings, which virtually no complementarian applies as universally binding]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11's appeal to creation order does not settle whether the practice grounded in that appeal is culturally conditioned, because Paul uses the same argumentative structure for practices most interpreters treat as culturally specific]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood cannot simply assert that creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2:12 universally normative without explaining why the same logic does not bind churches to head coverings. This is a gap in the document's argument, not a refutation of its conclusion.

**Questions my discipline opens that Alderwood does not ask:**

What does Deborah's role in Judges 4–5 establish canonically? Alderwood does not mention her. The text presents her as judge, prophet, and military commander without narrative apology, and the Song of Deborah (Judges 5) is among the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible. Is she an exception? The text does not say so. Is she a precedent? That requires argument. But she cannot simply be omitted from a canonical inquiry.

What does Romans 16 establish about women's actual ministry roles in the Pauline churches? Alderwood mentions Priscilla in passing but does not engage Phoebe's double designation or Junia's apostolic identification systematically.

---

### III. INITIAL POSITION ON THE GOVERNING QUESTION

The canonical evidence does not unambiguously establish either a universal male-only leadership norm or an undifferentiated egalitarianism. What it does establish, with high confidence, is more complex than either standard position acknowledges:

The Old Testament presents women in prophetic, judicial, and teaching roles without narrative condemnation. The New Testament presents women as deacons, apostles (in some sense), prophets, and teachers. The Pauline restriction texts are real, are grounded in creation-order appeals, and cannot be dismissed as purely local without a hermeneutical argument that is not itself exegetical. But those same texts are lexically contested at precisely the points that determine their scope.

My initial position is that the exegetical evidence is genuinely inconclusive at the level of church polity, that the strongest exegetical case for restriction operates at the level of a specific authoritative-teaching function rather than all leadership, and that the case for the elder restriction specifically depends heavily on how one resolves the *authenteō* question and the canonical weight of the Levitical/apostolic precedents — neither of which is as settled as Alderwood's document implies.

---

### IV. THREE MOST SIGNIFICANT FINDINGS

**Finding 1: The lexical evidence for *authenteō* is genuinely disputed.**

[CLAIM: The Greek verb *authenteō* in 1 Timothy 2:12 is a hapax legomenon in the New Testament and its meaning in the first century is debated among specialists]
[SOURCE: Philip Payne, *Man and Woman, One in Christ*, Zondervan, 2009]
[ARGUMENT: Payne surveys the lexical evidence and argues that *authenteō* carries a negative or domineering connotation in its pre-second-century usage, which would change the force of Paul's prohibition significantly]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Al Wolters' 2006 JETS article argues for a more neutral or positive meaning of *authenteō*, supporting a general authority prohibition]
[SOURCE: Al Wolters, "A Semantic Study of *authentes* and its Derivatives," *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Wolters surveys a broader range of cognates and argues the term does not carry an inherently negative force]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Both positions have serious lexical warrant. This is not a case where one side has the evidence and the other is special pleading.

**Finding 2: Romans 16 establishes that women held significant ministry roles in the Pauline churches, including roles described with titles Paul uses for men.**

[CLAIM: Phoebe is designated *diakonos* in Romans 16:1, the same term used of male deacons and of Paul himself]
[SOURCE: Nijay Gupta, *Tell Her Story*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Gupta argues that the masculine form *diakonos* applied to Phoebe is a substantive designation of ministry role, not a diminutive, and that translating it "servant" when applied to Phoebe while rendering it "deacon" when applied to men is a translation choice that requires defense]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Junia in Romans 16:7 is most naturally read as a woman described as "outstanding among the apostles"]
[SOURCE: Richard Bauckham, *Gospel Women*, Eerdmans, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Bauckham argues that Junia is an unambiguously female Latin name, that the phrase *en tois apostolois* most naturally means she is numbered among the apostles, and that the attempt to read her as male is driven by theological commitment rather than linguistic evidence]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Finding 3: Joel 2 / Acts 2 establishes that the Spirit's prophetic gift is explicitly poured out on daughters and female servants without qualification.**

[CLAIM: Peter's Pentecost speech cites Joel 2 as fulfilled, including the prophecy that "your daughters will prophesy" and "my female servants" will receive the Spirit]
[SOURCE: Acts 2:17–18, citing Joel 2:28–29]
[ARGUMENT: The canonical function of this citation is to establish that the new-covenant community is characterized by the Spirit's prophetic activity distributed without gender restriction — which creates exegetical pressure on any restriction of women's public speech that must be accounted for]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

---

### V. A FINDING INCONVENIENT FOR THE CONCLUSION I MIGHT PREFER

The creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not easily dismissed. Paul does not appeal to Ephesian culture or to a local problem. He appeals to Adam being formed first, then Eve. This is the same kind of grounding he uses in 1 Corinthians 11 for head coverings — but the parallel cuts both ways. The honest exegetical finding is that Paul believed creation order had normative force for community practice. Whether that normative force extends to all churches in all times requires a hermeneutical argument, but the exegete must not pretend Paul is doing something other than what he is doing. He is grounding a behavioral norm in creation. That must be engaged, not dissolved.

[CLAIM: The creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 represents Paul grounding a community practice in protological order, not in local circumstances]
[SOURCE: Thomas Schreiner, in Andreas Köstenberger and Thomas Schreiner, eds., *Women in the Church*, 3rd ed., Crossway, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Schreiner argues that creation-order grounding in Paul signals trans-cultural intent, and that egalitarian readings must account for why this grounding does not function as Paul apparently intends it]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

I find Schreiner's reading of the rhetorical function of v.13 more exegetically careful than many egalitarian dismissals of it. What I cannot determine from exegesis alone is whether that grounding settles the question of universal application. That requires the hermeneutician.

## The Reception Historian

# Reception History Position Paper
## Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry

**Advocate: Reception Historian**
**Debate: Women, Authority, and the Church — A Six-Discipline Inquiry**

---

## Statement of Disciplinary Limits

Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time in response to forces that are not purely exegetical. It cannot show that a reading is therefore wrong — texts can be misread consistently without that making the misreading correct, and a reading that emerges in a specific cultural moment is not invalidated by that emergence alone. My discipline cannot establish what the texts mean — that is the biblical scholar's work. It cannot adjudicate whether the history of a reading counts as evidence against its validity — that is the hermeneutician's work. What I can do is put the current reading in its actual historical context and ask: do you know where this reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

---

## I. Disciplinary Method and Core Assumptions

Reception history asks not what texts mean in the abstract but what they have been made to mean in concrete communities across time. My method is documentary and epigraphic: I work from letters, inscriptions, church orders, liturgical texts, and material culture — the actual residue of how real communities organized themselves. I trace interpretive genealogies: when did a specific reading emerge, under what conditions, serving whose interests? I treat the gap between official doctrine and actual practice as significant data, not noise to be explained away.

Two core assumptions govern this discipline. First, no reading arrives innocent of context. Every interpreter reads from somewhere — a social location, an institutional interest, a rhetorical situation. Identifying that location does not invalidate the reading, but it does require us to ask whether the reading is doing exegetical work, cultural work, or both. Second, the history of a text's use is itself evidence — about the text's interpretive range, about the communities that used it, and about which readings required the most effort to sustain against contrary evidence.

---

## II. Engagement with the Alderwood Document

### What Reception History Affirms

Alderwood's document is genuinely careful at several points that most popular treatments are not. Its acknowledgment that *authenteō* in 1 Timothy 2:12 is difficult; its recognition that "husband of one wife" may be about marital fidelity rather than gender; its honest admission that its position is secondary doctrine on which reasonable Christians disagree — these represent genuine intellectual honesty. I affirm them.

I also affirm Alderwood's statement that "tradition and structures that restrict women from using their gifts do not glorify Jesus and should be reformed wherever they are encountered." This is a significant concession, and it is one that reception history will test rigorously.

### What Reception History Questions

Alderwood's argument is almost entirely synchronic. It reads texts in their ancient context and in their present application, but it largely skips the two thousand years in between. This is not a minor omission. The history of how these texts have functioned — what they have authorized, what they have prohibited, who has read them and why — is substantial evidence that the document does not examine.

The document treats its reading as though it emerges directly and transparently from Scripture. Reception history shows that it does not. It emerges from a specific late twentieth-century evangelical tradition with a traceable genealogy, shaped by forces that are not purely exegetical. That does not make the reading wrong. It does mean that Alderwood should know where its reading came from.

### Questions Alderwood Does Not Ask

- What did the first three centuries of Christian communities actually look like in terms of women's roles, before the institutionalization of the episcopate?
- When did the reading of 1 Timothy 2 as a universal, trans-cultural prohibition become dominant, and what produced that dominance?
- What does the epigraphic record — inscriptions on tombs and church buildings — show about women's actual offices in early Christianity?
- Why did the Reformation, often cited as a recovery of biblical authority, result in *greater* restriction of women's roles in many Protestant communities than had existed in late medieval Catholicism?
- What does the inconsistency of the nineteenth-century missionary movement — women exercising apostolic functions on the field while being restricted at home — reveal about the actual basis for restriction?

---

## III. Initial Position on the Governing Question

The governing question is whether the restriction of women from the highest church office is grounded in Scripture alone, in Scripture as read through a particular cultural tradition, or in cultural tradition that has recruited Scripture to its support. Reception history cannot answer this definitively — that requires the biblical scholar and the hermeneutician. What reception history can say is this: the specific form of restriction that dominates contemporary evangelical complementarianism is not simply a direct reading of ancient texts. It has a history. Parts of that history are theologically principled. Parts of it are not. Any community that wishes to hold this position should hold it with full awareness of that history — including the parts that are inconvenient.

---

## IV. Three Most Significant Findings

### Finding One: The Early Epigraphic Record

The documentary evidence for women in formal ministerial roles in the early church is more substantial than either side of the modern debate typically acknowledges.

[CLAIM: Inscriptions and documentary sources from the early centuries attest women holding the titles *presbytera*, *diakonissa*, and in at least a small number of cases *episkopa*]
[SOURCE: Kevin Madigan and Carolyn Osiek, *Ordained Women in the Early Church*, Johns Hopkins University Press, 2005]
[ARGUMENT: Madigan and Osiek argue that this epigraphic and documentary evidence, while not uniform and while subject to interpretive debate, cannot be dismissed as purely honorific; it attests real ministerial function in at least some communities]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Pliny the Younger's letter to Trajan (c. 112 CE) refers to two women called *ministrae* whom he tortured for information about Christian practice]
[SOURCE: Pliny the Younger, *Epistulae* X.96, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: The term *ministrae* is a Latin translation of *diakonos*, suggesting women in formal diaconal roles were known to Roman officials as early as the second century]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This evidence does not settle the question of whether these women held equivalent authority to male presbyters or bishops. It does establish that the picture of uniformly male leadership from the earliest period is a retrospective construction.

### Finding Two: The Genealogy of Contemporary Complementarianism

The specific form of complementarianism that Alderwood inherits is not ancient. It has a precise genealogy in the late twentieth century.

[CLAIM: The Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood was founded in 1987, and the *Danvers Statement* was issued in 1988, explicitly as a response to what its founders identified as the cultural influence of feminism on evangelical churches]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: Barr argues that the complementarian movement of the late twentieth century was shaped not only by exegetical conviction but by a specific cultural and political reaction to second-wave feminism, and that this context shaped both the questions asked and the answers given]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: The translation of *authenteō* in 1 Timothy 2:12 shifted significantly between the 1970s and the 1990s in major English translations, moving toward stronger renderings that supported the complementarian position]
[SOURCE: Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: Barr documents this translation shift as evidence that translation choices are not purely linguistic but are shaped by the interpretive communities producing the translations]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM — the general argument is well-documented; specific translation comparison data would require verification against the translations themselves]

The point is not that the CBMW position is wrong because it emerged in a specific context. The point is that Alderwood's document presents this reading as simply what the Bible teaches, without acknowledging that it is what the Bible has been made to teach by a specific late twentieth-century movement with identifiable cultural pressures acting on it.

### Finding Three: Medieval Women's Leadership and the Gap Between Doctrine and Practice

[CLAIM: Medieval abbesses in certain double monasteries exercised quasi-episcopal authority, including jurisdiction over male clergy, hearing confessions, and in some cases giving liturgical blessings]
[SOURCE: Gary Macy, *The Hidden History of Women's Ordination*, Crossroad, 2007]
[ARGUMENT: Macy argues that the medieval church's actual practice was considerably more varied than its official doctrine, and that women's ministerial roles were progressively restricted through the high medieval period as part of the clericalization of ministry — a process driven by institutional rather than purely exegetical forces]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This finding matters for the present debate because it demonstrates that even within traditions formally committed to male-only ordination, the gap between official restriction and actual practice has been wide and persistent. The restriction was enforced unevenly, and its enforcement intensified at specific historical moments for identifiable institutional reasons.

---

## V. A Finding Inconvenient for the Conclusion I Might Prefer

The history of women's leadership in the church is not a simple story of progressive restriction that awaits modern liberation. The patristic record includes genuine theological arguments for restriction that are not reducible to misogyny or cultural accommodation.

[CLAIM: Chrysostom, commenting on Romans 16, expressed surprise and admiration at Junia being called an apostle, but also wrote extensively in other contexts about the dangers of women teaching men in public assemblies]
[SOURCE: John Chrysostom, *Homilies on Romans*, Homily 31; and *Homilies on 1 Timothy*, primary sources]
[ARGUMENT: Chrysostom's treatment demonstrates that even interpreters who clearly recognized women's ministerial roles in the New Testament period maintained restrictions in their own ecclesial context — the restriction was not based on ignorance of the evidence but on theological conviction about order]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM — the general content of these homilies is well-attested; specific page references would require access to the primary texts]

More inconveniently: Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza's reconstructive method — which argues that we can recover an egalitarian early Christianity beneath the patriarchal overlay of the canonical texts — has been widely influential but is methodologically contested. Her reconstruction requires treating silences as evidence and canonical texts as distortions, which is a significant hermeneutical commitment that not all historians share.

[CLAIM: Schüssler Fiorenza's *In Memory of Her* employs a hermeneutic of suspicion that treats the canonical New Testament as a patriarchal suppression of an earlier, more egalitarian Jesus movement]
[SOURCE: Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza, *In Memory of Her*, Crossroad, 1983]
[ARGUMENT: While foundational to feminist biblical scholarship, this method has been criticized by historians — including sympathetic ones — for building a reconstruction on evidence that the method itself has declared unreliable]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What this means for reception history: the discipline can show that restriction has a history and has served certain interests. It cannot show that there was a pristine egalitarian origin that restriction corrupted. The early evidence is genuinely mixed, and any account that flattens that complexity — in either direction — is doing advocacy, not history.

---

## Concluding Orientation

The question I bring to this debate is not whether Alderwood's position is right or wrong. It is whether Alderwood knows where its position came from. A community that holds a position without knowing its history is not holding it freely — it is held by it. The historical record I have outlined does not tell Alderwood what to conclude. It tells Alderwood what questions it has not yet asked. That is what reception history is for.

## The Hermeneutician

# Hermeneutical Position Paper
## Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry
### Prepared by the Hermeneutician

---

## Statement of Limits (read aloud at debate opening — 150 words maximum)

Hermeneutics can expose inconsistency and interrogate method. It can show that the same principle is applied selectively without saying whether the principle should be applied universally or abandoned. What it cannot do alone: determine which hermeneutical approach is ultimately correct — that requires theological and philosophical commitments that go beyond hermeneutics itself. I can show that the creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not applied consistently elsewhere. I cannot tell you from hermeneutics alone whether it should be applied universally or treated as culturally situated. For that I need the systematician's doctrine of scriptural authority and the biblical scholar's textual work. My discipline is a tool for honest inquiry, not a position in the debate.

---

## I. Disciplinary Method and Core Assumptions

Hermeneutics asks the question that every other discipline assumes it has already answered: *How do we move from what a text meant to what it means?* This is not a skeptical question. It is a foundational one. Every interpreter — Alderwood's elders, the biblical scholar presenting at this debate, the egalitarian and the complementarian — brings a set of interpretive decisions to the text before the text is read. My discipline's task is to name those decisions, test them for consistency, and evaluate them on their own terms.

[CLAIM: E.D. Hirsch argued that meaning is determinate and lodged in the author's original intention, and that interpretation's goal is to recover that intention]
[SOURCE: E.D. Hirsch, *Validity in Interpretation*, Yale University Press, 1967]
[ARGUMENT: Hirsch distinguished "meaning" (the author's intended content, stable and reproducible) from "significance" (the text's relevance for later readers, variable), arguing that only the former grounds valid interpretation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Hans-Georg Gadamer argued that meaning arises not from authorial intention alone but from the "fusion of horizons" between text and interpreter, making the reader's situation constitutive of meaning rather than merely incidental to it]
[SOURCE: Hans-Georg Gadamer, *Truth and Method*, various editions]
[ARGUMENT: Gadamer's "wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewusstsein" (historically effected consciousness) holds that no interpreter stands outside their tradition, and that understanding is always a productive event, not mere reproduction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

These two positions — Hirsch's authorial intentionalism and Gadamer's fusion of horizons — represent the poles between which every working hermeneutic in this debate operates. Recognizing this is not relativism. It is intellectual honesty about what is actually at stake.

[CLAIM: Kevin Vanhoozer proposed a middle position in which the text's communicative act, rather than either bare authorial psychology or reader experience, anchors meaning]
[SOURCE: Kevin Vanhoozer, *Is There a Meaning in This Text?*, Zondervan, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Drawing on speech-act theory, Vanhoozer argues that texts perform illocutionary acts that are recoverable and normative, preserving determinacy without collapsing into pure intentionalism]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

My working assumption is that meaning is constrained — texts are not infinitely malleable — but that the application of ancient texts to contemporary communities requires methodological decisions that are themselves contestable and must be defended, not assumed.

---

## II. Engagement with the Alderwood Document

### What Alderwood gets right, hermeneutically

Alderwood deserves credit for one hermeneutical move that many comparable documents avoid: it does not pretend that interpretation is simply "reading what the text says." It acknowledges that 1 Timothy 2:12 is difficult, that 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 cannot be applied literally without contradiction, and that the phrase "husband of one wife" in 1 Timothy 3:2 is doing something more nuanced than a simple gender restriction. This is hermeneutical honesty. The document is also right that Paul's appeal to creation order in 1 Timothy 2:13 is significant — it is a feature of the text that any hermeneutic must account for, not dismiss.

### The consistency problem: the document's sharpest vulnerability

Alderwood argues that Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 ("Adam was formed first, then Eve") makes the prohibition on women's authoritative teaching transcultural — grounded not in Ephesian social custom but in the structure of creation itself.

[CLAIM: Alderwood's document states that Paul's appeal to creation order rather than cultural considerations means the prohibition "is a principle that transcends individual cultures"]
[SOURCE: Alderwood Community Church, *What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership*, Appendix 1, 1 Timothy 2:11-14 section]
[ARGUMENT: The document uses Paul's creation-order grounding as the criterion distinguishing transcultural principle from culturally situated instruction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a specific hermeneutical principle: *appeals to creation order mark transcultural norms*. It is a defensible principle. But it must be applied consistently.

[CLAIM: Paul grounds the head-covering instruction in 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 in creation order, citing that "man was not made from woman, but woman from man" and "woman was created for man"]
[SOURCE: 1 Corinthians 11:8-9, ESV]
[ARGUMENT: The creation-order rationale appears in the head-covering passage with equal explicitness to its appearance in 1 Timothy 2:13]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutical question is unavoidable: if creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2:12 transcultural, why does it not make head coverings equally transcultural? Alderwood almost certainly does not require head coverings. I do not say this to score a point — I say it because this is the consistency test, and it is the most important methodological challenge this debate must answer. The differential treatment of these two texts requires a principled explanation. Three options exist: (1) accept both as transcultural and require head coverings; (2) argue that the creation-order appeals function differently in each passage in ways the text itself supports; or (3) acknowledge that something other than creation-order grounding is doing the real hermeneutical work in both cases. Option 3 is the most honest starting point, and it requires naming what that something is.

### Questions the document does not ask

Alderwood does not name its hermeneutical method. It applies one, but it does not defend it. The questions it does not ask include: What is the criterion for distinguishing culturally accommodated instructions from transcultural principles, and is that criterion applied consistently? When two exegetically defensible readings of the same text yield opposite conclusions, what adjudicates between them? Is the community's discernment — in this case, Alderwood's elder body — a legitimate hermeneutical authority, and on what grounds?

---

## III. The Major Hermeneutical Options

[CLAIM: William Webb's "redemptive-movement hermeneutic" argues that the trajectory of Scripture — the direction of ethical movement from ancient Near Eastern context through the New Testament — rather than the static statements of any single text should govern ethical application]
[SOURCE: William Webb, *Slaves, Women and Homosexuals*, IVP Academic, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Webb proposes eighteen criteria for identifying when a text's ethic is "frozen" at a culturally accommodated point versus expressing the full redemptive ideal, and argues that the NT's teaching on women, like its teaching on slavery, moves toward greater liberation without reaching the cultural destination]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Denny Burk and Thomas Schreiner criticized Webb's redemptive-movement hermeneutic as insufficiently anchored to the text's propositional content, arguing that it allows the interpreter's construction of a "trajectory" to override explicit apostolic instruction]
[SOURCE: Denny Burk and Thomas Schreiner, responses to Webb in *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*]
[ARGUMENT: The critics argue that Webb's method lacks adequate controls against the interpreter importing contemporary ethical intuitions as the endpoint of the "trajectory," effectively making cultural consensus the hermeneutical authority]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This is a genuine methodological danger in Webb's framework, and I note it as such. The trajectory argument works by extrapolating beyond what the text says — which is both its strength (accounting for the slavery analogy) and its vulnerability (the extrapolation requires criteria that are themselves contestable).

[CLAIM: I. Howard Marshall proposed a more cautious version of the trajectory argument, arguing that the task of Scripture is not to give final answers but to stimulate and guide moral reasoning in ways that require going "beyond the Bible"]
[SOURCE: I. Howard Marshall, *Beyond the Bible: Moving from Scripture to Theology*, IVP Academic, 2004]
[ARGUMENT: Marshall's approach is more modest than Webb's, focusing on the direction and purpose of biblical instruction rather than constructing a multi-criteria extrapolation, making it less vulnerable to the charge of unconstrained reader-construction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Marshall's version of the argument is hermeneutically more defensible than Webb's precisely because it is less systematized — it acknowledges the limits of the method rather than overclaiming its precision.

---

## IV. Most Significant Findings

**Finding 1: The consistency test reveals that the hermeneutical principle Alderwood applies to 1 Timothy 2 (creation-order grounding = transcultural norm) is not applied to 1 Corinthians 11. This is the debate's central methodological problem, and it is not resolved by either side.**

**Finding 2: The slavery analogy has genuine hermeneutical force.** The church went beyond the explicit teaching of the New Testament on slavery — which nowhere commands abolition — by appealing to something like a redemptive trajectory. If that move was legitimate, the question of whether the same move applies to gender cannot be dismissed. But the analogy has genuine limits: the NT contains no creation-order grounding for slavery, while it does contain such grounding for gender distinctions. This asymmetry is not decisive, but it is real.

**Finding 3: Pre-understanding is inescapable but not determinative.**

[CLAIM: Anthony Thiselton argued that the interpreter's "horizon" — their set of pre-understandings — inevitably shapes what they find in a text, but that the text's own horizon can challenge and reshape the interpreter's, making genuine understanding possible without claiming neutral objectivity]
[SOURCE: Anthony Thiselton, *The Two Horizons*, Eerdmans, 1980]
[ARGUMENT: Thiselton's appropriation of Gadamer for evangelical hermeneutics establishes that pre-understanding is a condition of interpretation, not a disqualification from it, but that honest interpretation requires the text to be allowed to interrogate the interpreter's assumptions]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Every advocate in this debate — including me — reads these texts through a horizon shaped by gender, ecclesial tradition, and cultural location. Acknowledging this is not relativism. It is the precondition of honest inquiry.

---

## V. The Inconvenient Finding

The finding most inconvenient for a hermeneutic that favors expanding women's roles is this: **the redemptive-movement hermeneutic, if applied consistently, does not stop where its proponents want it to stop.** Webb himself applies it to argue that the NT's teaching on homosexuality is *not* on a liberatory trajectory — but critics have noted that the same methodological apparatus could be used to reach the opposite conclusion.

[CLAIM: Webb's critics argue that the redemptive-movement hermeneutic lacks internal controls that would prevent its application from being extended to sexual ethics beyond what Webb intends]
[SOURCE: Denny Burk, response to Webb, *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2003]
[ARGUMENT: If the criterion for moving beyond the text is a constructed trajectory toward greater human liberation or flourishing, the method provides no principled textual stopping point]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I find this criticism partially compelling. A hermeneutic that opens questions it cannot close with its own resources is a hermeneutic that requires supplementation from elsewhere — from systematic theology's doctrine of Scripture, from the community's discernment, from the biblical scholar's textual work. That is not a refutation of the method. It is an honest account of its limits. And it applies equally to the hermeneutic that freezes all creation-order texts as transcultural: that method also cannot adjudicate its own application without resources from outside itself.

This is precisely why I arrive third in the debate order.

## The Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian

# SYSTEMATIC/ECCLESIOLOGICAL THEOLOGIAN
## Pre-Debate Position Paper: Women, Authority, and the Church

---

### STATEMENT OF DISCIPLINARY LIMITS (read aloud — maximum 150 words)

Systematics can clarify what is at stake and expose hidden assumptions. It can ask what doctrine of the church, ministry, and authority is actually implied by a given position on women's leadership — and whether that doctrine is coherent and defensible. What it cannot do alone: determine the meaning of specific texts or the weight of historical evidence. A beautiful systematic account of new creation ecclesiology means nothing if it cannot show exegetical grounding. My conclusions must be tested against what Scripture actually says — the biblical scholar's work — and what the church has actually done — the reception historian's work. Systematic theology that ignores inconvenient textual or historical evidence is not systematic theology; it is rationalization with theological vocabulary.

---

### I. DISCIPLINARY METHOD AND CORE ASSUMPTIONS

My method is doctrinal analysis from first principles. I ask: what theological doctrines are actually operative in a given position, whether or not they are named? I then test those doctrines for internal coherence and for coherence with the broader theological commitments the position claims to hold.

Traditions — Baptist, Anglican, Reformed, Catholic, Pentecostal, Orthodox — are resources I draw on as evidence of how serious theologians have answered underlying questions. They are not constraints. Where traditions disagree, that disagreement is data about the genuine difficulty of the underlying question, not a reason to prefer any one answer.

My core assumption is that doctrinal positions are not free-floating. A claim about who may hold authority in the church implies a claim about what the church is, what authority is, and where it comes from. These prior questions must be made explicit before the surface debate can be resolved.

---

### II. ENGAGEMENT WITH THE ALDERWOOD DOCUMENT

**What Alderwood does well:** The document is admirable in its pastoral generosity. It correctly identifies that the debate is not between those who affirm women's dignity and those who do not. Its reframing — "What may a non-elder do?" rather than "What may a woman do?" — is ecclesiologically significant: it locates the restriction in an office rather than in a person's sex as such. This is a more defensible position than traditions that restrict women from all teaching or leadership.

Alderwood also correctly acknowledges that *ezer* carries no connotation of inferiority, and that the Spirit distributes gifts without gendered restriction.

[CLAIM: Alderwood's document states that "the Spirit distributes all gifts freely to both men and women" and that women can and should teach Scripture and lead ministries of all ages and both genders]
[SOURCE: Alderwood Community Church, "What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership," internal document, undated]
[ARGUMENT: Alderwood's position is that the restriction is located in the office of elder, not in women's giftedness or competence]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**What Alderwood leaves unresolved — the central ecclesiological problem:**

Alderwood defines the elder as the "spiritual father of the church family." This is a relational and familial description. But the document does not ask a prior question that its own argument requires: *what kind of authority does the elder exercise, and where does that authority come from?*

Three distinct models of eldership are available in the tradition, and they generate different conclusions:

1. **The ontological model**: Ordination confers a distinct status on the elder. The elder is not merely someone who performs certain functions but someone who *is* something different by virtue of the office. This is broadly the Catholic, Orthodox, and some Anglican understanding. On this model, gender might belong to the ontological status conferred.

2. **The functional model**: The elder performs certain functions — teaching, guarding doctrine, shepherding. The office is defined by what it does. On this model, the question becomes whether there is a functional reason why women cannot perform these functions — and that functional reason must be specified and defended.

3. **The relational model**: The elder is the community's recognized shepherd — the person to whom the community looks for guidance and in whom it recognizes authority. Authority is recognized from below as much as conferred from above. This is closer to some Baptist and congregationalist understandings.

[CLAIM: Miroslav Volf argues that in free-church ecclesiology, the church is constituted from below by the gathered community, and that authority is recognized rather than hierarchically conferred]
[SOURCE: Miroslav Volf, *After Our Likeness: The Church as the Image of the Trinity*, Eerdmans, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Volf's trinitarian ecclesiology grounds church structure in the mutual relations of the Trinity rather than in hierarchical delegation, with implications for how authority is distributed]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Alderwood appears to operate with a functional account** — elders guard and teach doctrine, they delegate teaching to non-elders — but its creation-order argument implies something closer to a *representational* account: the elder represents God's authority to the community, and that representation requires maleness because God has consistently appointed male leaders. These are not the same argument, and they are in tension.

If the account is purely functional, then the question is whether women can perform the functions. Alderwood's own document suggests they largely can — women can teach Scripture, lead mixed-gender groups, and serve as pastors. The functional restriction then collapses to a narrow residual: the specific function of "guarding doctrine" as an elder. This requires a much more precise argument than Alderwood provides.

If the account is representational, then we need a doctrine of representation: what is it about maleness that is required for representing divine authority to the community? This brings us directly to the most contested theological territory in this debate.

---

### III. THREE SIGNIFICANT FINDINGS

**Finding 1: The ESS/EFS debate and the grounding of authority structures**

The most theologically serious argument for male-only eldership is the claim that the eternal relation of the Son to the Father — a relation of authority and submission — establishes a model for human authority structures, including in the church. Wayne Grudem and Bruce Ware have argued versions of this position.

[CLAIM: Wayne Grudem has argued that the eternal subordination of the Son to the Father provides a model for male-female authority relations]
[SOURCE: Wayne Grudem, *Systematic Theology*, Zondervan, 1994]
[ARGUMENT: Grudem connects the eternal procession and submission of the Son to the Father with the proper ordering of male-female relations in family and church]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Kevin Giles has argued at length that this position — Eternal Subordination of the Son (ESS) — represents a departure from Nicene orthodoxy. The Nicene tradition insists on the full equality of the divine persons in power and authority; the Son's submission in the economy of salvation is temporal and voluntary, not an eternal ontological feature of the immanent Trinity.

[CLAIM: Kevin Giles argues that the eternal functional subordination of the Son is inconsistent with Nicene trinitarianism, which affirms the full equality of the divine persons]
[SOURCE: Kevin Giles, *The Trinity and Subordinationism*, IVP Academic, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Giles contends that using ESS to ground human authority structures imports a subordinationist heresy into trinitarian doctrine, and that Nicene orthodoxy cannot support the complementarian use of the Trinity]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This matters decisively for systematic theology. If the strongest theological grounding for male-only eldership requires a doctrine of the Trinity that departs from Nicene orthodoxy, that is a serious problem — not merely an exegetical dispute, but a question about whether the position is theologically coherent.

**Finding 2: The imago Dei and the representational model**

Amy Peeler's work raises a question that cuts across the representational argument. If the fullness of the *imago Dei* is expressed only in the partnership of male and female together — a point Alderwood itself affirms — then a representational account of eldership faces a structural problem: an all-male eldership does not fully represent the God whose image is most completely expressed in male-and-female-together.

[CLAIM: Amy Peeler argues that the gender of God as revealed in Scripture is not straightforwardly male, and that the masculine language for God in Scripture does not straightforwardly ground male-only representation in ministry]
[SOURCE: Amy Peeler, *Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022]
[ARGUMENT: Peeler contends that the incarnation of the Son as male does not imply that maleness is necessary for representing divine authority, because the Son's maleness is not the primary ground of his representative role]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This does not settle the question, but it significantly complicates the representational argument. If the argument is that elders represent God to the community, and if Alderwood itself affirms that the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires both male and female, then the representational argument may actually require *including* women in eldership to represent God more fully — not excluding them.

**Finding 3: New creation ecclesiology and the church's eschatological vocation**

If the church is the community of the new creation — the first fruits of the age to come — then its structures should embody the reality inaugurated in Christ, not merely reflect the patterns of the present age.

[CLAIM: Jürgen Moltmann argues that the church's structure must be understood eschatologically — the church is a proleptic sign of the coming Kingdom, and its internal relations should anticipate the freedom of the new creation]
[SOURCE: Jürgen Moltmann, *The Church in the Power of the Spirit*, Fortress, 1977]
[ARGUMENT: Moltmann's pneumatological ecclesiology grounds church structure in the Spirit's gifts rather than in hierarchical office, with eschatological freedom as the normative horizon]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood's argument is oriented primarily toward the creation order and the pattern of redemptive history. A new creation ecclesiology asks whether that orientation is sufficient — whether the church's structures should be governed by the patterns of the old creation or by the inaugurated reality of the new.

---

### IV. AN INCONVENIENT FINDING

The most inconvenient finding for an egalitarian conclusion is that the church's dominant practice across most traditions and most of history has been male-only eldership and priesthood — and this is not easily explained as mere cultural accommodation.

[CLAIM: Paul Bradshaw's historical work on ordination rites in the early church shows that the restriction of priestly and episcopal ordination to men was near-universal in the early centuries]
[SOURCE: Paul Bradshaw, *Rites of Ordination in the Early Church*, Pueblo, 1990]
[ARGUMENT: Bradshaw's liturgical history documents that ordination rites were consistently restricted to men in early Christian practice, suggesting this was not merely a later imposition]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

A systematic theology that ignores this datum is not systematic theology. The question of *why* this was the case — whether it reflects theological conviction, cultural assumption, or both — is a question for the reception historian. But the datum itself must be reckoned with.

---

### V. INITIAL POSITION ON THE GOVERNING QUESTION

Alderwood's position is more defensible than it first appears, but it rests on a theological foundation it has not examined. The document defines the elder as "spiritual father" — a relational description — but requires a representational argument to ground the male restriction. These are different accounts of eldership, and they generate different ecclesiologies.

The most pressing question this debate must answer is not exegetical but doctrinal: *what kind of authority does the elder exercise, and where does it come from?* Until that question is answered, the surface debate about specific texts cannot be resolved. My discipline's task in this debate is to make that question unavoidable.

## The Pastoral Theologian

# Pastoral Theology Position Paper
## Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry

**Advocate: Pastoral Theologian**
**Disciplinary Lead: Pastoral and Practical Theology, with Missiology**

---

### Statement of Limits (read aloud — maximum 150 words)

Pastoral theology can show that a position has harmful effects or good fruit, but fruitfulness is not the final criterion for theological truth — and harm, while morally serious, does not by itself settle exegetical questions. I resist both the move that ignores human cost entirely and the move that makes human cost the only thing that matters. What I cannot establish alone: what Scripture requires (the biblical scholar's work), what the doctrine actually is (the systematician's work), or whether the pastoral concern I am raising is actually overriding honest engagement with the text (the hermeneutician's work). I need those disciplines to keep me honest. What I bring to them is the insistence that a theological position that produces systematic harm and calls it faithfulness must answer for that harm — and that answer must be more than an appeal to transcultural principle.

---

### I. Disciplinary Method and Core Assumptions

Pastoral theology begins with people — specific, embodied people in specific situations — and asks what theological positions *do* to them, not merely what those positions *say*. It treats lived experience not as anecdote to be set aside once the real argument begins, but as a form of evidence with genuine theological weight. This does not mean pastoral concern overrides exegesis. It means that a theological interpretation that consistently produces identifiable harm across contexts, generations, and cultures carries an evidentiary burden that must be answered, not merely acknowledged.

My method operates through four lenses: *formation analysis* (what does this practice teach, implicitly, about God and human dignity?), *pastoral case reasoning* (what does this position actually require of specific people?), *missiological reflection* (what does the church's outward witness require, and what does majority-world practice reveal?), and *empirical attention* (what do the data and documented patterns show?).

The core assumption undergirding all of this: theology is not merely propositional. It is formative. What the church does shapes what the church believes, and what the church believes about God is communicated as much through its embodied practices as through its doctrinal statements.

---

### II. Engagement with the Alderwood Document

#### What I Affirm

Alderwood's document is unusually honest. Its acknowledgment that "the roadblocks and hindrances that have unnecessarily kept women from leading and teaching in the church have only served to hurt the body as a whole" is remarkable — not because it resolves the debate, but because it names the harm as real and as requiring repentance. This is not a minor concession. It is a pastoral and ethical admission that should be taken with full seriousness, including by those who agree with Alderwood's restriction.

I also affirm Alderwood's framing question: not "What may a woman do?" but "What may a non-elder do?" This is a genuine attempt to locate the restriction narrowly and to resist the cultural drift toward broader exclusion. It reflects pastoral instinct even within a complementarian framework.

#### What I Question

The document's repentance language creates a pastoral obligation it does not fully discharge. If tradition and sin are "genuinely hard to disentangle" — and the document implies they are — then the church's reparative work cannot stop at removing *future* hindrances. It requires active discernment about which inherited structures are themselves products of the sin being repented of. Alderwood does not ask that question. It asks women to trust that the restriction is not sin while acknowledging that the broader pattern has been.

#### Questions My Discipline Opens

Alderwood does not ask: *What does it form people to believe about God when only men hold final authority?* It does not ask: *What does the evidence show about the relationship between hierarchical authority structures and the conditions for institutional abuse?* It does not ask: *What does it mean, pastorally, to tell a gifted woman she can preach on Sunday, lead a staff team, carry the title 'pastor' — but cannot be an elder?* These are not rhetorical questions. They have answers — some of them inconvenient for every position in this debate.

---

### III. Initial Position on the Governing Question

My initial position is this: the pastoral, formative, and missiological evidence is weighty enough that any church maintaining a restriction on women in eldership bears a significant ongoing burden of proof — not merely exegetical, but pastoral. That burden includes demonstrating, with evidence rather than aspiration, that the promised culture of flourishing is actually being produced; that the restriction is not functioning as a permission structure for broader exclusion; and that the formation produced by the practice is consistent with what the church claims to believe about God and human dignity.

I do not hold that fruitfulness alone settles the question. I hold that fruitlessness and harm are theological data that demand a theological answer.

---

### IV. Three Significant Findings

#### Finding 1: The Formation Question — What Does This Practice Teach?

[CLAIM: Embodied practices form the imagination of a community more powerfully than doctrinal statements do, and liturgical structures communicate theology whether or not that theology is explicitly articulated]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Smith argues that human beings are fundamentally formed by what they love and desire, and that desire is shaped not primarily by ideas but by practices, rituals, and liturgies — including the liturgies of Sunday morning]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

When a congregation gathers Sunday after Sunday and the person who holds final doctrinal authority is always male, that congregation is being formed — regardless of what the doctrinal statement says — to associate ultimate spiritual authority with maleness. This is not a claim about intent. It is a claim about formation. The question Alderwood must answer is not only "Is our doctrine correct?" but "What are our practices teaching our children about who God is and who gets to speak for God?"

#### Finding 2: The Harm-Abuse Connection

[CLAIM: Hierarchical authority structures that concentrate unchecked power create the conditions under which spiritual abuse flourishes, and the absence of women in authoritative roles removes a structural check on that power]
[SOURCE: Diane Langberg, *Redeeming Power*, Brazos Press, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Langberg argues that abuse in Christian contexts is fundamentally a misuse of power, and that systems which concentrate authority without accountability — including gender-exclusive leadership structures — create environments where abuse is more likely to occur and less likely to be named]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: The ChurchToo movement documented patterns of spiritual and sexual abuse in complementarian church contexts that were enabled, in part, by institutional structures that concentrated authority in male leaders without meaningful accountability]
[SOURCE: Scot McKnight and Laura Barringer, *A Church Called Tov*, Tyndale House, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: McKnight and Barringer argue that toxic church cultures are enabled by systems that prioritize institutional protection over the dignity of individuals, and that gender-exclusive authority structures contribute to this by limiting the range of voices that can name and challenge abuse]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is not an argument that complementarian churches are uniformly abusive. It is an argument that the structural conditions matter, and that the absence of women from authoritative roles is one of those conditions.

#### Finding 3: The Calling Question

[CLAIM: The experience of women who believe themselves called to pastoral or elder roles, and who are told that calling cannot be fulfilled, constitutes a distinct pastoral problem that cannot be resolved by redirecting them to other forms of ministry]
[SOURCE: Ruth Tucker, *Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*, Zondervan, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Tucker argues from personal experience and broader observation that the pastoral cost of complementarian restriction falls unevenly and severely on women who experience genuine calling, and that the church's response to this cost has often been inadequate]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What does it mean, pastorally, to tell a woman that her sense of call is real, her gifts are genuine, her teaching is welcome on Sunday morning — but she cannot hold the office that would give that calling institutional recognition and authority? It is not sufficient to say "the gifts are affirmed, the office is restricted." A calling is not merely a gift. It is a sense of vocation with a shape. To affirm the gift while permanently closing the vocational shape is a pastoral act with consequences that must be owned, not merely acknowledged.

---

### V. An Inconvenient Finding

The evidence of fruitfulness does not run only in one direction.

[CLAIM: Theologically conservative churches in the Global South that practice what might be called functional egalitarianism out of missional necessity do not uniformly abandon complementarian convictions when they gain institutional stability — some return to restricting women's roles as churches mature]
[SOURCE: Lesslie Newbigin, *The Gospel in a Pluralist Society*, Eerdmans, 1989]
[ARGUMENT: Newbigin's missiological framework raises the question of whether missional necessity produces genuine theological revision or pragmatic accommodation — a distinction with significant implications for how majority-world practice is used as evidence in this debate]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

More directly: fruitfulness is real evidence, but it is not self-interpreting. Churches with egalitarian structures have also produced harm, enabled abuse, and failed the women they claimed to liberate. The pastoral advocate who uses harm as evidence must be willing to apply that criterion consistently — and must acknowledge that the question of which structures produce the conditions for flourishing is more complex than either side of this debate typically admits.

The inconvenient truth for my preferred direction is this: pastoral concern, missiological fruitfulness, and formation analysis together do not settle the exegetical question. They raise the stakes of getting it right. That is not the same thing.

## The Social and Cultural Analyst

# Social and Cultural Analysis: A Pre-Debate Position Paper

## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

**Submitted by: The Social and Cultural Analyst**

---

### Statement of Limits

Before proceeding, I must state what my discipline can and cannot do. Social analysis can show that a reading emerged in a particular social context and serves particular social interests. It cannot show that the reading is therefore wrong — texts can have genuine transcultural authority even when interpreted in socially situated ways, and even readings that serve the interests of the powerful can be correct. If all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them. My discipline's greatest risk is explaining everything and thereby explaining nothing. I need the biblical scholar to establish what the texts actually say and the hermeneutician to adjudicate how much social location counts as evidence against a reading's validity. What I bring is this: a position that cannot examine its own social conditioning, or that claims to have transcended social situatedness while everyone else is trapped by it, has not yet done its intellectual homework.

---

### I. Disciplinary Method and Core Assumptions

I bring two complementary lenses to this debate. The first is ancient: social-scientific analysis of the first-century Mediterranean world — its honor/shame dynamics, patronage networks, household structures, and the specific social locations of early Christian communities in cities like Ephesus and Corinth. The second is contemporary: the sociology of modern religious institutions, with particular attention to how gender ideology is constructed, maintained, and transmitted in American evangelical culture.

My core methodological assumption is that every text is written and read inside a social world. Ignoring that social world produces misreading in two directions simultaneously: it makes culturally specific instructions into universal principles, and it misses the ways texts themselves subverted the social worlds in which they first appeared. Social location is not a verdict on a text's authority. It is evidence that must be weighed.

---

### II. Engaging the Alderwood Document

**What my discipline affirms:**

Alderwood makes a genuinely important concession in its commitment section: *"Much of what has kept women from flourishing and using their gifts within the church has not been the theology of the church but has been the sin of sexism and discrimination that is the result of the Fall."* This is sociologically honest. It acknowledges that the social history of gender restriction in churches has been substantially driven by forces independent of — and often contrary to — the theological rationale offered for restriction. Alderwood is right to name this, and right to commit to opposing it.

The document also demonstrates unusual self-awareness in its taxonomy of positions, acknowledging that complementarianism spans a spectrum and that Alderwood's position disagrees with more restrictive complementarians. This is sociologically accurate.

**What my discipline questions:**

The same concession that I affirm opens a gap that Alderwood does not sufficiently explore. If the theology and the social forces can be separated in the *application* of the position — sexism is sin, genuine restriction is theology — can we be equally confident they can be separated in the *formation* of the position? The document does not ask this question. My discipline must.

Alderwood also states that Paul's reasoning in 1 Timothy 2 "appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations," and therefore "the principle transcends individual cultures." This is a hermeneutical claim that my discipline cannot adjudicate alone — but it is a claim that requires social-scientific scrutiny, because the same move (grounding a culturally specific instruction in a creation-order argument) appears throughout the household codes, and the question of whether those grounds lift the instruction out of its social world or whether the grounds themselves are socially shaped is precisely what is at issue.

**What questions my discipline opens that Alderwood does not ask:**

Three questions Alderwood does not raise:

1. When did the specific configuration of complementarianism that shaped this document emerge, and what forces produced it?
2. What is the social world of Ephesus and Corinth, and how does that world bear on the instructions addressed to those communities?
3. How does gender restriction function sociologically in religious institutions, independent of the theological rationale offered for it?

---

### III. Initial Position on the Governing Question

My discipline does not determine whether women should serve as elders. What it does determine is this: the current complementarian position, as institutionalized in American evangelicalism, emerged from a specific cultural and political moment in the late twentieth century, was shaped by forces that had nothing to do with exegesis, and cannot be cleanly separated from those forces by sincerity of belief. This does not make the position wrong. It means the position has not yet passed the test of social self-awareness, and a position that has not passed that test should be held with more epistemic humility than it typically is.

---

### IV. Three Most Significant Findings

**Finding One: The Genealogy of Modern Complementarianism**

[CLAIM: The modern complementarian movement, including the Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood, emerged in the late 1980s in direct response to second-wave feminism and in close entanglement with American conservative political culture, not primarily from a fresh engagement with biblical texts.]
[SOURCE: Kristin Kobes Du Mez, *Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Du Mez documents that evangelical gender ideology in twentieth-century America was inseparable from specific cultural and political projects — the construction of militant Christian masculinity, Cold War anxieties about male leadership, and the culture wars of the 1980s and 1990s. The CBMW was founded in 1987; the *Danvers Statement* followed in 1988. These were reactive documents, not the product of disinterested exegesis.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: The term "complementarian" itself was introduced by the Piper/Grudem project as a rebranding of a position that needed to distinguish itself from both egalitarianism and what was perceived as the discredited language of male supremacy.]
[SOURCE: Kristin Kobes Du Mez, *Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: The naming of a movement is itself a social act — it constructs the terms of the debate and positions its adherents as the reasonable middle.]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The Alderwood document cites Piper, Grudem, Schreiner, and the CBMW as proponents of its own position's category. These are not simply exegetes — they are movement architects operating within a specific social moment. That does not make them wrong. It means their work cannot be read as socially neutral.

**Finding Two: The Social World of Ephesus**

[CLAIM: The city of Ephesus in the first century was home to the dominant cult of Artemis, whose temple was one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world, and whose religious economy gave women unusual cultic prominence and public religious authority.]
[SOURCE: Sandra Glahn, *Nobody's Mother: Artemis of the Ephesians in Antiquity and the New Testament*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Glahn argues that understanding the Artemis cult and its specific social pressures on the Ephesian church is essential for reading 1 Timothy. The community Paul addresses was navigating a religious environment in which female religious authority was the dominant cultural norm, not the countercultural position — which significantly complicates any reading of 1 Timothy 2 as a transcultural principle.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: In the first-century Mediterranean world, the oikos (household) was the basic unit of social organization, and the household codes of the New Testament letters engaged directly with Greco-Roman conventions for ordering the household, which included the subordination of wives, children, and slaves to the paterfamilias.]
[SOURCE: Bruce Malina and Richard Rohrbaugh, *Social-Science Commentary on the Letters of Paul*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Malina and Rohrbaugh argue that Paul's household instructions are intelligible only within the honor/shame framework of Mediterranean social life, where a household that violated conventional order brought shame on its members and on the community associated with it.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Finding Three: Symbolic Violence and Internalized Hierarchy**

[CLAIM: Pierre Bourdieu's concept of "symbolic violence" describes the process by which hierarchies are maintained not through overt coercion but through the internalization of the hierarchy by those subject to it, such that subordination is experienced as natural, willed, or spiritually appropriate.]
[SOURCE: Pierre Bourdieu, *Masculine Domination*, Stanford University Press, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Bourdieu argues that the most durable forms of domination are those in which the dominated participate in their own domination through categories of perception and appreciation that are themselves the product of the domination. The sociologically significant question for this debate is not whether women in complementarian churches believe their restriction is God's will — many sincerely do — but whether that sincere belief is itself evidence that the restriction is just.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This framework is underused in this debate. Its application is not that sincerely held beliefs are false. It is that sincerity cannot serve as its own validation, and that the sociology of how a restriction comes to feel natural is itself evidence that must be examined.

### V. A Finding Inconvenient for My Preferred Conclusion

My discipline is most comfortable with the conclusion that complementarian restriction is a social construct that serves institutional power. But the cross-cultural data complicates this.

[CLAIM: Churches in the Global South — in contexts with no exposure to the American evangelical culture-war moment and no entanglement with CBMW — have independently arrived at male-leadership structures, often reading the same Pauline texts in similar ways.]
[SOURCE: Susan Hylen, *Women in the New Testament World*, Oxford University Press, 2019]
[ARGUMENT: Hylen notes that the diversity of women's roles in the New Testament world itself suggests that early Christian communities negotiated these questions differently in different contexts — which cuts both ways. It means restriction is not the only historically attested pattern, but it also means restriction cannot be explained entirely by American culture-war genealogy.]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

If complementarian restriction were purely a product of late twentieth-century American evangelical culture, we would not expect to find it independently reproduced across genuinely different social worlds. That it does appear cross-culturally does not make it theologically correct — but it does mean my genealogical argument, while significant, is not sufficient. The hermeneutician and the biblical scholar must carry weight that my discipline alone cannot bear.

# Round 1 — Opening Statements

## The Biblical Scholar

# ROUND 1 OPENING STATEMENT — THE BIBLICAL SCHOLAR
## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

### STATEMENT OF LIMITS

Exegesis can establish what texts mean within their canonical context. It can identify where translation choices carry theological weight and insist that those choices be defended. It can ask what the whole-Bible pattern looks like when we resist focusing only on the texts most convenient to our preferred conclusion. I need the hermeneutician to adjudicate method, the reception historian to test whether my readings have been consistent, and the systematician to build something from what I find.

### I. WHAT MY DISCIPLINE AFFIRMS IN THE ALDERWOOD DOCUMENT

Alderwood's treatment of *ezer* is better than most church documents at this level. The document correctly refuses to load "helper" with inferiority, and it correctly observes that *ezer* language is applied to God in contexts of military and saving help.

[CLAIM: The Hebrew *ezer* appears approximately twenty-one times in the Old Testament, and the majority of uses refer either to God or to military allies — contexts carrying no connotation of subordinate status]
[SOURCE: John Goldingay, *Old Testament Theology*, Vol. 2, IVP Academic, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Goldingay notes that *ezer* language applied to God carries connotations of indispensable strength, not secondary status]

### II. WHERE MY DISCIPLINE PUSHES HARDER

**The Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles.** Alderwood states that "God consistently calls men to fill the roles of the highest level of spiritual authority" and cites the Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles as evidence. This argument requires exegetical work the document does not supply.

The Levitical priesthood was a typological institution whose specific requirements — tribe, gender, physical wholeness — are not straightforwardly binding on the church. The New Testament explicitly describes the church as a "royal priesthood" of all believers (1 Peter 2:9), a designation that cuts across the Levitical categories entirely. The selection of twelve male apostles corresponding to the twelve tribes carries typological freight — it is a gesture toward Israel's reconstitution — not a gender policy for church office.

[CLAIM: The twelve apostles were chosen as a symbolic reconstitution of Israel's twelve tribes, which is a typological function inseparable from their Jewishness]
[SOURCE: N.T. Wright, "Women's Service in the Church: The Biblical Basis," NTWrightPage.com]
[ARGUMENT: Wright argues the maleness of the Twelve is inseparable from their function as the new patriarchs of Israel; applying it as a gender rule for church leadership requires a hermeneutical step the text does not authorize]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Alderwood makes this argument without supplying that step. My discipline requires it.

**The creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13.** Alderwood asserts that Paul's appeal to creation order — rather than cultural considerations — means the prohibition "transcends individual cultures." This is a significant hermeneutical claim presented as an exegetical finding.

The problem: Paul uses creation-order grounding in 1 Corinthians 11:8–9 to ground the head-covering instruction, citing that "man was not made from woman, but woman from man." This is structurally identical to the argument in 1 Timothy 2:13. Virtually no complementarian applies head coverings as universally binding. Alderwood cannot assert that creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2:12 transcultural without explaining why the same logic does not bind churches to head coverings. This is a gap in the document's argument — not a refutation of its conclusion, but a gap that must be filled.

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11's appeal to creation order does not settle whether the practice grounded in that appeal is culturally conditioned]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee argues Paul uses the same argumentative structure for practices most interpreters treat as culturally specific, so creation-order grounding alone cannot determine universal normativity]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I want to be equally honest in the other direction: the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 cannot be dissolved. Paul is doing what he appears to be doing — grounding a behavioral norm in protological order. Egalitarian readings that wave past verse 13 as though it were not there are not more honest than complementarian readings that wave past 1 Corinthians 11. Both must account for the parallel.

**Deborah and Huldah — the absent witnesses.** Alderwood does not mention Deborah. Canonically, this is a significant omission. Judges 4–5 presents Deborah as prophet, judge, and military commander without narrative apology. The Song of Deborah (Judges 5) is among the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible. The text does not call her an exception; that characterization requires argument Alderwood does not supply.

[CLAIM: Huldah the prophet (2 Kings 22) was consulted by Josiah's male officials for authoritative interpretation of the law, despite Jeremiah and Zephaniah being active contemporaries]
[SOURCE: Richard Bauckham, *Gospel Women*, Eerdmans, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Bauckham notes that Huldah's role as the authoritative interpreter of the newly discovered law is not explained or apologized for by the text, and that the presence of male prophets makes her selection theologically significant rather than merely circumstantial]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

**Romans 16.** Phoebe is designated *diakonos* — the masculine form, the same term Paul uses of himself and of male deacons. Translating it "servant" when applied to Phoebe while rendering it "deacon" when applied to men is a translation choice carrying argumentative weight that must be defended, not assumed.

[CLAIM: Phoebe is also called *prostatis* in Romans 16:2, a term that in its cognate form refers to a patron or leader, not merely a helper]
[SOURCE: Nijay Gupta, *Tell Her Story*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Gupta argues that *prostatis* carries connotations of patronal authority and leadership, and that rendering it "helper" imports a diminutive the Greek does not require]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Junia in Romans 16:7 is most naturally read as a woman described as "outstanding among the apostles." The attempt to read the name as male (*Junias*) lacks manuscript support and appears to be driven by the theological difficulty of a female apostle.

[CLAIM: Junia is an unambiguously attested female Latin name; the masculine form *Junias* has no independent attestation in ancient sources]

[CLAIM: Philip Payne surveys pre-second-century usage and argues *authenteō* carries a negative or domineering connotation, which would change the force of Paul's prohibition significantly]
[SOURCE: Philip Payne, *Man and Woman, One in Christ*, Zondervan, 2009]
[ARGUMENT: If *authenteō* means "to domineer" rather than "to exercise authority," Paul may be prohibiting abusive authority rather than all authority]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Al Wolters surveys a broader range of cognates and argues the term does not carry an inherently negative force]
[SOURCE: Al Wolters, "A Semantic Study of *authentes* and its Derivatives," *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Wolters' reading supports a more general authority prohibition, strengthening the complementarian reading]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Both positions have serious lexical warrant. I will not pretend otherwise.

### IV. WHAT THE CANONICAL PICTURE LOOKS LIKE

The Old Testament presents women in prophetic, judicial, and teaching roles without narrative condemnation — Deborah, Huldah, Miriam, the daughters of Zelophehad. The new-covenant inauguration at Pentecost cites Joel 2 explicitly: "your daughters will prophesy" and "my female servants" will receive the Spirit (Acts 2:17–18). This is not incidental. It is the canonical announcement of the new-covenant community's character.

The New Testament presents women as deacons (Phoebe), apostles in some sense (Junia), prophets (1 Corinthians 11:5), teachers (Priscilla, Acts 18:26), and patrons of missionary work (Romans 16). The Pauline restriction texts are real, are grounded in creation-order appeals, and cannot be dismissed as purely local without a hermeneutical argument that is not itself exegetical. But those same texts are lexically contested at precisely the points that determine their scope.

**My initial finding:** The exegetical evidence does not unambiguously establish either a universal male-only leadership norm or an undifferentiated egalitarianism. The strongest exegetical case for restriction operates at the level of a specific authoritative-teaching-and-adjudicating function — something like what Alderwood calls the elder's role — rather than all leadership or all public speech. But that case depends on resolving *authenteō* in the complementarian direction and treating the Levitical/apostolic precedents as gender paradigms rather than typological institutions — neither of which is as settled as Alderwood implies.

**What I need from the other disciplines:** The hermeneutician must adjudicate the consistency problem — why creation-order grounding is transcultural in 1 Timothy 2 but not in 1 Corinthians 11. The systematician must tell me what kind of authority the elder exercises and where it comes from, because the answer to that question changes which texts are decisive. The reception historian must test whether my readings of Junia and Phoebe are consistent with how these figures have been read across history — and if not, whether that inconsistency is evidence of exegetical error or of theological pressure distorting the reading.

I note the social analyst's point about the Ephesian context and Sandra Glahn's work on the Artemis cult. This is relevant to 1 Timothy 2, and I will need to engage it more fully in subsequent rounds. The pastoral theologian's formation question — what does it teach when only men hold final authority? — is not an exegetical question, but it is a question the exegete must hold honestly while doing the work.

## The Reception Historian

# RECEPTION HISTORIAN — ROUND 1 OPENING STATEMENT

---

## Statement of Disciplinary Limits

Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time in response to forces that are not purely exegetical. It cannot show that a reading is therefore wrong — texts can be misread consistently without that making the misreading correct, and a reading that emerges in a specific cultural moment is not invalidated by that emergence alone. My discipline cannot establish what the texts mean — that is the biblical scholar's work. It cannot adjudicate whether the history of a reading counts as evidence against its validity — that is the hermeneutician's work. What I can do is put the current reading in its actual historical context and ask: do you know where this reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

---

## I. What Reception History Affirms in Alderwood

Alderwood's document is more historically self-aware than most comparable church statements. Its acknowledgment that "tradition and structures that restrict women from using their gifts do not glorify Jesus and should be reformed wherever they are encountered" is not a minor concession — it is a remarkable one, because it implicitly admits that the tradition has, in fact, produced unjust restriction. My discipline affirms this admission and will press on it.

I also note, in response to the Biblical Scholar's excellent paper, that the Roman 16 evidence she raises — Phoebe as *diakonos*, Junia among the apostles — is not merely exegetical data. It is reception data. The history of how those designations were read, obscured, and recovered is itself significant. I will return to this.

---

## II. The Document's Historical Thinness

Alderwood's argument is almost entirely synchronic. It moves from ancient text to present application while largely bypassing two thousand years of actual use. This is not a minor gap. The history of how these texts have functioned — what they authorized, what they prohibited, who interpreted them and why — is substantial evidence the document does not examine.

The document presents its reading as what the Bible teaches. Reception history asks a prior question: whose reading of the Bible, formed when, under what conditions?

## III. Three Findings the Document Does Not Examine

### Finding One: The Early Epigraphic and Documentary Record

The picture of uniformly male leadership from the earliest period is a retrospective construction.

[CLAIM: Pliny the Younger's letter to Trajan, dated approximately 112 CE, refers to two women called *ministrae* whom he interrogated about Christian practice]

This matters for the Alderwood document specifically. Alderwood asserts that "the instructions to churches in the New Testament... assume and instruct that elders of the local churches will be men." [CLAIM: The Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood was founded in 1987 and the Danvers Statement issued in 1988, explicitly as a response to what its founders identified as feminist influence on evangelical churches]

The Biblical Scholar has already flagged the *authenteō* dispute as genuinely contested. Reception history adds a layer: the contest did not happen in a vacuum. Alderwood's document lists among its proponents Piper, Grudem, Schreiner, and the CBMW. These are not simply exegetes — they are architects of a movement that emerged in a specific cultural moment. This finding is one the Alderwood document cannot see because it does not look.

[CLAIM: Medieval abbesses in certain double monasteries exercised quasi-episcopal authority, including jurisdiction over male clergy and in some cases liturgical functions normally reserved for ordained men]

[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

More counterintuitively: the Reformation, often presented as a recovery of biblical authority, resulted in *greater* restriction of women's roles in many Protestant communities than had existed in late medieval Catholicism.

[CLAIM: Lyndal Roper's study of the Reformation in Augsburg documents that the Reformation's closure of convents eliminated the primary institutional space in which women had exercised recognized spiritual authority, teaching, and leadership]
[SOURCE: Lyndal Roper, *The Holy Household*, Oxford University Press, 1989]
[ARGUMENT: Roper argues that the Reformation's reordering of religious life around the household and the male pastor contracted rather than expanded women's institutional religious roles, despite the Reformation's formal emphasis on the priesthood of all believers]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a significant datum for any reading that presents current restriction as simply what Scripture teaches. The Reformation was a moment of intense biblical re-engagement — and its effect on women's roles was largely restrictive. That requires explanation. The explanation is not purely exegetical.

## IV. The Inconvenient Finding

I am required to state what my discipline finds inconvenient.

The history of women's leadership in the church is not a simple story of progressive restriction awaiting modern liberation. The patristic record includes genuine theological arguments for restriction that are not reducible to misogyny.

[CLAIM: Chrysostom, in his Homily 31 on Romans, expressed admiration for Junia being counted among the apostles — yet in other homilies argued against women teaching men in public assemblies]
[SOURCE: John Chrysostom, *Homilies on Romans*, Homily 31; *Homilies on 1 Timothy*, primary sources]
[ARGUMENT: Chrysostom's simultaneous recognition of women's apostolic roles in the New Testament period and maintenance of restriction in his own context demonstrates that restriction was not based on ignorance of the evidence but on theological conviction about order — a conviction held by interpreters who knew the texts well]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

More methodologically inconvenient: Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza's reconstructive project — which argues for a recoverable egalitarian early Christianity beneath the canonical texts — has been foundational to feminist biblical scholarship but is methodologically contested.

[CLAIM: Schüssler Fiorenza's *In Memory of Her* employs a hermeneutic of suspicion that treats the canonical New Testament as a patriarchal suppression of an earlier, more egalitarian Jesus movement]
[SOURCE: Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza, *In Memory of Her*, Crossroad, 1983]
[ARGUMENT: This method has been criticized — including by sympathetic historians — for building a reconstruction on evidence the method itself has declared unreliable; it cannot serve as a foundation for reception history without importing its contested hermeneutical commitments]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Reception history cannot show that there was a pristine egalitarian origin that restriction corrupted. The early evidence is genuinely mixed. Any account that flattens that complexity — in either direction — is doing advocacy, not history.

## V. The Central Question I Put to This Debate

The Social and Cultural Analyst and I are working adjacent ground, and I note that her finding about cross-cultural reproduction of male-leadership structures is a genuine complication of the genealogical argument. I accept it as such.

But the genealogical argument retains force even so. The specific *form* that restriction takes in late twentieth-century American evangelicalism — the particular texts foregrounded, the particular translations chosen, the particular institutional structures built — is not simply the universal church's reading. It is a reading with a history. And that history includes forces that are not purely exegetical.

The question I put to Alderwood, and to every position in this debate, is not: *is your reading wrong because it has a history?* It is: *do you know where your reading came from, and does knowing that change anything about how you hold it?*

A community that holds a position without knowing its history is not holding it freely. It is held by it.

## The Hermeneutician

# ROUND 1 — OPENING STATEMENT
## The Hermeneutician
### *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

---

## STATEMENT OF LIMITS

Hermeneutics can expose inconsistency and interrogate method. It can show that the same principle is applied selectively without saying whether the principle should be applied universally or abandoned. What it cannot do alone: determine which hermeneutical approach is ultimately correct — that requires theological and philosophical commitments that go beyond hermeneutics itself. I can show that the creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not applied consistently elsewhere. I cannot tell you from hermeneutics alone whether it should be applied universally or treated as culturally situated. For that I need the systematician's doctrine of scriptural authority and the biblical scholar's textual work. My discipline is a tool for honest inquiry, not a position in the debate.

---

## I. WHAT THE BIBLICAL SCHOLAR AND RECEPTION HISTORIAN HAVE ESTABLISHED

My colleagues have done significant work. The biblical scholar has correctly identified that the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is a real exegetical feature that cannot be dissolved — Paul is doing something with protological grounding that must be engaged rather than explained away. That is honest. The reception historian has correctly identified that contemporary complementarianism has a traceable genealogy in the late twentieth century, that the epigraphic record is more complex than either standard position acknowledges, and that the gap between official doctrine and actual practice has been wide and persistent. That is also honest.

Both colleagues have, appropriately, handed questions to me. The biblical scholar explicitly: "I cannot determine from exegesis alone whether that grounding settles the question of universal application. That requires the hermeneutician." The reception historian implicitly: knowing where a reading came from does not tell us whether it is valid. My discipline is where both referrals land.

What I want to do now is name the methodological problem precisely, because imprecision here is what allows this debate to continue past the point where it should be generating more light.

---

## II. THE CONSISTENCY TEST: ALDERWOOD'S CENTRAL VULNERABILITY

Alderwood makes a specific hermeneutical claim about 1 Timothy 2:13. I will quote it exactly:

> "Paul gives his reasoning for this prohibition and he appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations. Whatever the principle is that Paul is applying, it is a principle that transcends individual cultures."

[CLAIM: Alderwood uses Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 as the criterion marking the prohibition as transcultural rather than culturally conditioned]
[SOURCE: Alderwood Community Church, *What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership*, Appendix 1]
[ARGUMENT: The document treats creation-order grounding as the distinguishing marker between culturally situated instructions and universally binding principles]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a hermeneutical principle, not an exegetical finding. The principle is: *creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms*. It is a defensible principle. But it must be applied consistently across the canon, not selectively.

Now apply it to 1 Corinthians 11:8-9:

[CLAIM: Paul grounds the head-covering instruction in 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 in creation order, stating that "man was not made from woman, but woman from man" and "neither was man created for woman, but woman for man"]
[SOURCE: 1 Corinthians 11:8-9, ESV; Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 11:2-16]
[ARGUMENT: The creation-order rationale in 1 Corinthians 11 is structurally identical to the creation-order rationale in 1 Timothy 2:13 — both ground a behavioral norm for women in the sequence and purpose of creation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11's appeal to creation order does not settle whether the practice grounded in that appeal is culturally conditioned, because Paul uses the same argumentative structure for practices most interpreters treat as culturally specific]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee's point is that the presence of a creation-order argument does not automatically confer transcultural status on the practice grounded in it — the argumentative structure and the cultural bindingness of the conclusion are separable questions]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood almost certainly does not require head coverings. This is not a minor point — it is the consistency test, and it is the most important methodological challenge this debate must answer. The differential treatment of 1 Timothy 2:13 and 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 requires a principled explanation.

Three options exist, and I want to be precise about what each costs:

**Option 1:** Accept both as transcultural and require head coverings. This is consistent, but virtually no contemporary church takes this position. Its near-universal rejection is itself evidence that something other than the stated principle is doing the hermeneutical work.

**Option 2:** Argue that the creation-order appeals function differently in each passage in ways the text itself supports — that the appeal in 1 Timothy 2 is more foundational, or more tightly integrated with the prohibition, than the appeal in 1 Corinthians 11. This is a defensible move, but it requires a detailed argument from the texts themselves, and it must not simply reassert the conclusion it is trying to establish.

**Option 3:** Acknowledge that something other than creation-order grounding is doing the real hermeneutical work in both cases — that interpreters are bringing a prior judgment about what is culturally conditioned to both texts, and then deploying creation-order language to ratify conclusions reached by other means. This is the most honest starting point, and it requires naming what that prior judgment is and defending it.

I am not asserting that Option 3 is correct. I am asserting that every advocate in this debate — including Alderwood, including the egalitarian position, including me — is operating somewhere in this space, and that the failure to name which option one is taking is the primary source of unclarity in this debate.

---

## III. THE MAJOR HERMENEUTICAL OPTIONS AND THEIR GENUINE COSTS

I want to lay out the options honestly, because each has genuine strengths and genuine dangers that their proponents do not always acknowledge.

**Historical-grammatical interpretation** aims to recover the author's intended meaning in its original context. Its strength is that it takes the text's own categories seriously and resists importing alien frameworks. Its danger is that it can mistake the interpreter's cultural assumptions for the text's original meaning — the horizon of the interpreter is not as transparent as the method implies.

[CLAIM: E.D. Hirsch argued that meaning is determinate and lodged in the author's original intention, and that interpretation's goal is to recover that intention, distinguishing "meaning" from "significance"]
[SOURCE: E.D. Hirsch, *Validity in Interpretation*, Yale University Press, 1967]
[ARGUMENT: Hirsch's distinction between meaning and significance preserves textual determinacy while allowing for varied application, but requires that the original meaning be recoverable with sufficient confidence to ground normative claims]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The question Hirsch's framework opens is whether we can recover Paul's intended meaning with sufficient precision to determine whether he intended a universal or local application — and the honest answer, given the *authenteō* dispute and the Ephesian context debate, is that we cannot do so with certainty.

**William Webb's redemptive-movement hermeneutic** is the most directly relevant framework to this debate. Webb argues that the trajectory of Scripture — the direction of ethical movement from ancient Near Eastern context through the New Testament — rather than the static statements of any single text should govern ethical application.

[CLAIM: William Webb proposes eighteen criteria for identifying when a text's ethic is "frozen" at a culturally accommodated point versus expressing the full redemptive ideal, arguing that the NT's teaching on women moves toward greater liberation without reaching the cultural destination]
[SOURCE: William Webb, *Slaves, Women and Homosexuals*, IVP Academic, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Webb's method attempts to provide principled criteria for trajectory extrapolation, distinguishing the culturally accommodated from the transcultural by examining the direction and endpoint of biblical ethical development]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Webb's genuine strength is that it accounts for the slavery analogy honestly — the church went beyond the explicit teaching of the New Testament on slavery, and Webb provides a principled account of why that was legitimate. His genuine danger is identified precisely by his critics:

[CLAIM: Denny Burk argued that Webb's redemptive-movement hermeneutic lacks adequate controls against the interpreter importing contemporary ethical intuitions as the endpoint of the "trajectory," effectively making cultural consensus the hermeneutical authority]
[SOURCE: Denny Burk, response to Webb, *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2003]
[ARGUMENT: If the criterion for moving beyond the text is a constructed trajectory toward greater human liberation or flourishing, the method provides no principled textual stopping point — the interpreter's construction of the trajectory's destination does the real normative work]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I find this criticism partially compelling. But I want to note that it applies with equal force to the hermeneutic that freezes all creation-order texts as transcultural: that method also cannot adjudicate its own application without resources from outside itself — specifically, the prior judgment about which creation-order appeals are culturally conditioned and which are not.

**I. Howard Marshall's more cautious trajectory argument** is hermeneutically more defensible than Webb's precisely because it is less systematized:

[CLAIM: I. Howard Marshall argued that the task of Scripture is not to give final answers but to stimulate and guide moral reasoning in ways that require going "beyond the Bible," with the direction and purpose of biblical instruction as the guide rather than a multi-criteria extrapolation]
[SOURCE: I. Howard Marshall, *Beyond the Bible: Moving from Scripture to Theology*, IVP Academic, 2004]
[ARGUMENT: Marshall's approach is more modest than Webb's — it acknowledges the limits of the method rather than overclaiming its precision, and focuses on the spirit and direction of biblical instruction rather than constructing a trajectory endpoint]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Marshall's version is less vulnerable to Burk's critique because it does not claim to have an eighteen-criteria mechanism for determining the trajectory's destination. Its cost is that it provides less guidance, not more. It tells us we must go beyond the Bible without telling us precisely how far.

## IV. THE SLAVERY ANALOGY: GENUINE FORCE AND GENUINE LIMITS

The slavery analogy has genuine hermeneutical force that cannot be dismissed. The church went beyond the explicit teaching of the New Testament on slavery — which nowhere commands abolition and in several places commands obedience — by appealing to something like a redemptive trajectory. If that move was legitimate, the question of whether the same move applies to gender restrictions cannot be dismissed by simply asserting that Paul grounds his gender teaching in creation order.

But the analogy has genuine limits that its proponents do not always acknowledge:

The New Testament contains no creation-order grounding for slavery. Paul grounds the slavery instructions in household management norms and missional pragmatics. He does not say "slaves obey your masters because God created masters before slaves." The asymmetry between the slavery texts and the gender texts is not decisive — a creation-order appeal could itself be culturally conditioned — but it is real, and a hermeneutic that treats the slavery analogy as fully parallel to the gender question without accounting for this asymmetry is not being fully honest about the evidence.

[CLAIM: Richard Hays argues that the New Testament's moral logic must be read through its narrative logic, attending to the overall story of God's redemptive action rather than extracting propositions from individual texts]
[SOURCE: Richard Hays, *The Moral Vision of the New Testament*, HarperOne, 1996]
[ARGUMENT: Hays's method of moving from text to community to norm requires attending to the focal images of the New Testament — cross, community, new creation — as the organizing framework for ethical application, which may generate different conclusions than either proof-texting or trajectory extrapolation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What Hays's approach requires is asking what the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2 is doing within the larger narrative logic of Pauline theology — and that is a question the biblical scholar and systematician must help answer.

## V. WHAT THE CONSISTENCY TEST REVEALS ABOUT PRE-UNDERSTANDING

[CLAIM: Anthony Thiselton argued that the interpreter's "horizon" inevitably shapes what they find in a text, but that the text's own horizon can challenge and reshape the interpreter's, making genuine understanding possible without claiming neutral objectivity]
[SOURCE: Anthony Thiselton, *The Two Horizons*, Eerdmans, 1980]
[ARGUMENT: Thiselton's appropriation of Gadamer for evangelical hermeneutics establishes that pre-understanding is a condition of interpretation, not a disqualification from it, but that honest interpretation requires the text to be allowed to interrogate the interpreter's assumptions]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Every advocate in this debate reads these texts through a horizon shaped by gender, ecclesial tradition, and cultural location. The reception historian and the social analyst have demonstrated this with precision about the genealogy of contemporary complementarianism. But the egalitarian reading has its own genealogy, its own social location, its own cultural pressures acting on it. Acknowledging this symmetry is not relativism — it is the precondition of honest inquiry.

What the consistency test reveals is not that Alderwood's conclusion is wrong. It reveals that the hermeneutical principle Alderwood states — creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms — is not the principle Alderwood actually applies. Something else is doing the work. The task of this debate is to name that something else and evaluate it honestly.

## VI. INITIAL ORIENTATION: WHAT MY DISCIPLINE POINTS TOWARD

My discipline does not tell you whether women should be elders. It tells you what kind of argument you need to have before you can answer that question responsibly.

That argument requires:

**First**, a consistent account of what distinguishes culturally situated instructions from transcultural principles — an account that applies the same criteria to 1 Corinthians 11 and 1 Timothy 2, to the slavery texts and the gender texts, without special pleading for the conclusion already preferred.

**Second**, a defense of whichever hermeneutical framework is being employed — historical-grammatical, redemptive-movement, canonical whole-Bible, or other — that acknowledges that framework's genuine costs and does not merely invoke its strengths.

**Third**, honesty about where pre-understanding is operating. The reception historian and social analyst have shown that contemporary complementarianism has a genealogy. The hermeneutician must add: so does contemporary egalitarianism. Both readings have horizons. The question is not which reading is socially located — they both are — but which reading, when its horizon is made explicit, survives the encounter with the text most honestly.

**Fourth**, recognition that the hermeneutical question cannot be closed by hermeneutics alone. Where the biblical scholar finds genuine exegetical ambiguity, and where the consistency test reveals that the stated hermeneutical principle is not actually being applied, the systematician's doctrine of scriptural authority and the pastoral theologian's account of formative consequences become load-bearing. My discipline prepares the ground for those disciplines. It does not replace them.

The picture that begins to emerge from my lens is provisional but pointed: Alderwood's position is more methodologically vulnerable than it appears, not because its conclusion is demonstrably wrong, but because the hermeneutical principle it states does not survive the consistency test. A more honest version of Alderwood's position would need to name what is actually doing the hermeneutical work — and defend it. That defense is what the next rounds of this debate must produce.

## The Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian

# SYSTEMATIC/ECCLESIOLOGICAL THEOLOGIAN
## Round 1 — Opening Statement

---

### STATEMENT OF DISCIPLINARY LIMITS

Systematics can clarify what is at stake and expose hidden assumptions. It can ask what doctrine of the church, ministry, and authority is actually implied by a given position on women's leadership — and whether that doctrine is coherent and defensible. What it cannot do alone: determine the meaning of specific texts or the weight of historical evidence. A beautiful systematic account of new creation ecclesiology means nothing if it cannot show exegetical grounding. My conclusions must be tested against what Scripture actually says — the biblical scholar's work — and what the church has actually done — the reception historian's work. Systematic theology that ignores inconvenient textual or historical evidence is not systematic theology; it is rationalization with theological vocabulary.

---

### I. THE CENTRAL ECCLESIOLOGICAL PROBLEM ALDERWOOD HAS NOT SOLVED

The other advocates have done important work. The biblical scholar has shown that the lexical evidence is genuinely contested, that the Levitical and apostolic precedents require more hermeneutical work than Alderwood provides, and that Romans 16 attests women in substantial ministry roles. The reception historian has shown that the specific form of complementarianism Alderwood inherits has a traceable genealogy and that the early epigraphic record is more complex than either side typically acknowledges. The hermeneutician has identified the consistency problem — creation-order grounding in 1 Timothy 2 but not in 1 Corinthians 11 — as the debate's central methodological challenge. The pastoral theologian has raised the formation question: what does this practice teach, regardless of what the doctrine says? The social analyst has asked whether the position can examine its own social conditioning.

My task is different. I ask what doctrines are actually at stake beneath all of this — and whether Alderwood's position rests on a coherent theological foundation.

The answer is: not yet.

Alderwood defines the elder as the "spiritual father of the church family." This is a relational and familial description. But the document never asks the prior question its own argument requires: *what kind of authority does the elder exercise, and where does that authority come from?*

This is not a peripheral question. It is the question. Different answers generate different ecclesiologies, and those different ecclesiologies yield different conclusions about whether gender is relevant — even from identical readings of the same texts.

---

### II. THREE MODELS OF ELDERSHIP AND THEIR IMPLICATIONS

Three distinct models of eldership are available in the tradition:

**The ontological model**: Ordination confers a distinct status. The elder does not merely perform functions but *is* something different by virtue of office. This is broadly the Catholic, Orthodox, and some Anglican understanding. On this model, gender might belong to the ontological status conferred — which is why Catholic and Orthodox arguments for male-only priesthood are structurally coherent even if one disagrees with them.

**The functional model**: The elder performs certain functions — teaching, guarding doctrine, shepherding. The office is defined by what it does. On this model, the question is whether there is a *functional* reason women cannot perform these functions, and that reason must be specified and defended.

**The relational model**: The elder is the community's recognized shepherd — the person in whom the community recognizes authority. Authority is recognized from below as much as conferred from above.

[CLAIM: Miroslav Volf argues that in free-church ecclesiology, the church is constituted from below by the gathered community, and authority is recognized rather than hierarchically conferred]
[SOURCE: Miroslav Volf, *After Our Likeness: The Church as the Image of the Trinity*, Eerdmans, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Volf's trinitarian ecclesiology grounds church structure in the mutual relations of the Trinity rather than in hierarchical delegation, with implications for how authority is distributed and who can exercise it]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Alderwood's tension**: The document *operates* with a functional account — elders guard and teach doctrine, they delegate teaching to non-elders — but its creation-order argument *implies* something closer to a representational account: the elder represents God's authority to the community, and that representation requires maleness because God has consistently appointed male leaders. These are not the same argument. They are in tension, and the tension is not acknowledged.

If the account is purely functional, the restriction collapses to a narrow residual: the specific function of "guarding doctrine" as an elder. But Alderwood's own document says women can teach Scripture, lead mixed-gender groups, serve as pastors, and preach on Sunday mornings. The functional difference between an elder and a female pastor under elder authority becomes vanishingly thin. What function, precisely, can the elder perform that the female pastor cannot? The document does not say.

If the account is representational, we need a doctrine of representation: what is it about maleness that is required for representing divine authority to the community? This takes us directly to the most contested theological territory in this debate.

### III. THE ESS/EFS DEBATE AND ITS STAKES

The theologically most serious argument for male-only eldership grounds it in the eternal relation of the Son to the Father. If the Son is eternally subordinate to the Father in authority, then authority-and-submission structures are built into the fabric of reality itself, and male-female authority relations in the church reflect that eternal pattern.

[CLAIM: Wayne Grudem has argued that the eternal subordination of the Son to the Father provides a model for male-female authority relations in family and church]
[SOURCE: Wayne Grudem, *Systematic Theology*, Zondervan, 1994]
[ARGUMENT: Grudem connects the Son's eternal submission to the Father with the proper ordering of male-female relations, grounding the authority structure in the immanent Trinity rather than merely in creation or redemptive history]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Kevin Giles has argued at length that this position — Eternal Subordination of the Son (ESS) — departs from Nicene orthodoxy.

[CLAIM: Kevin Giles argues that the eternal functional subordination of the Son is inconsistent with Nicene trinitarianism, which affirms the full equality of the divine persons in power and authority]
[SOURCE: Kevin Giles, *The Trinity and Subordinationism*, IVP Academic, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Giles contends that the Son's submission in the economy of salvation is temporal and voluntary, not an eternal ontological feature of the immanent Trinity, and that using ESS to ground human authority structures imports subordinationism into trinitarian doctrine]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This matters decisively. If the strongest theological grounding for male-only eldership requires a departure from Nicene orthodoxy, that is not merely an exegetical dispute — it is a question about whether the position is theologically coherent at the level of doctrine of God. Alderwood does not invoke ESS, but any representational argument for male eldership must eventually answer this question: what is the theological ground for the representation? If it is not the Trinity, what is it?

### IV. THE IMAGO DEI AND THE REPRESENTATIONAL ARGUMENT'S INTERNAL PROBLEM

Amy Peeler's work raises a structural problem for the representational model that Alderwood cannot avoid, because Alderwood itself creates it.

[CLAIM: Amy Peeler argues that the gender of God as revealed in Scripture is not straightforwardly male, and that the masculine language for God does not straightforwardly ground male-only representation in ministry]
[SOURCE: Amy Peeler, *Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022]
[ARGUMENT: Peeler contends that the incarnation of the Son as male does not imply that maleness is necessary for representing divine authority, because the Son's maleness is not the primary ground of his representative role — his humanity is]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood's own document states: "the fullness of the way that humanity images God can only be seen in the partnership of male and female together." This is correct, and it is a significant theological affirmation. But it creates an internal problem for the representational argument. If the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires both male and female, then an all-male eldership does not fully represent the God whose image is most completely expressed in male-and-female-together. The representational argument, pressed to its logic, may actually require *including* women in eldership — not excluding them.

Alderwood cannot have it both ways: affirming that the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires both sexes while simultaneously arguing that the office representing divine authority to the community should be restricted to one sex. The tension is internal to the document itself.

### V. NEW CREATION ECCLESIOLOGY AND THE QUESTION ALDERWOOD DOES NOT ASK

The hermeneutician has correctly identified the consistency problem with creation-order appeals. Systematic theology adds a further question: even if creation order grounds certain patterns, is the church's structure primarily governed by the *old* creation order or by the *new* creation inaugurated in Christ?

[CLAIM: Jürgen Moltmann argues that the church's structure must be understood eschatologically — the church is a proleptic sign of the coming Kingdom, and its internal relations should anticipate the freedom of the new creation]
[SOURCE: Jürgen Moltmann, *The Church in the Power of the Spirit*, Fortress, 1977]
[ARGUMENT: Moltmann's pneumatological ecclesiology grounds church structure in the Spirit's gifts rather than in hierarchical office, with eschatological freedom as the normative horizon]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood's argument is oriented primarily toward the creation order and the pattern of redemptive history up to the New Testament. A new creation ecclesiology asks whether that orientation is sufficient. The church is not merely the continuation of Israel's covenant structures — it is the community of the Spirit in whom the new age has broken in. Whether that new age requires, permits, or simply allows the continuation of old-creation gender patterns in leadership is a question Alderwood does not ask. It should.

### VI. INITIAL SYSTEMATIC POSITION

Alderwood's position is more defensible than it first appears, but it rests on a theological foundation it has not examined. The document defines the elder as "spiritual father" — a relational description — but requires a representational argument to ground the male restriction. These are different accounts of eldership, generating different ecclesiologies.

The most pressing question this debate must answer is not primarily exegetical: *What kind of authority does the elder exercise, and where does that authority come from?* Until that question is answered with precision, the surface debate about specific texts cannot be resolved.

My discipline's task in this debate is to make that question unavoidable — and to insist that any answer must be internally coherent, consistent with the broader theological commitments the position claims to hold, and tested against both the biblical scholar's findings and the reception historian's evidence.

Alderwood has not yet done that work. Neither, I must acknowledge, has the other side of this debate — which tends to assume a pneumatological-functional account of authority without defending it. The ecclesiological question is genuinely open. The work of making it unavoidable begins here.

## The Pastoral Theologian

# ROUND 1 OPENING STATEMENT — PASTORAL THEOLOGIAN

---

## Statement of Limits

Pastoral theology can show that a position has harmful effects or good fruit, but fruitfulness is not the final criterion for theological truth — and harm, while morally serious, does not by itself settle exegetical questions. I resist both the move that ignores human cost entirely and the move that makes human cost the only thing that matters. What I cannot establish alone: what Scripture requires (the biblical scholar's work), what the doctrine actually is (the systematician's work), or whether the pastoral concern I am raising is actually overriding honest engagement with the text (the hermeneutician's work). I need those disciplines to keep me honest. What I bring to them is the insistence that a theological position that produces systematic harm and calls it faithfulness must answer for that harm — and that answer must be more than an appeal to transcultural principle.

---

## I. What I Affirm in the Alderwood Document — and Where the Affirmation Gets Complicated

Alderwood's document contains a sentence I want to read aloud before I say anything critical: *"The roadblocks and hindrances that have unnecessarily kept women from leading and teaching in the church have only served to hurt the body as a whole."* And then: *"Tradition and structures that restrict women from using their gifts do not glorify Jesus and should be reformed wherever they are encountered."*

This is not boilerplate. A document that maintains a restriction while naming the broader pattern as harmful — and calling that pattern *sin* — has done something pastorally significant. Alderwood deserves credit for it. I affirm it without qualification.

Then I have to push on it hard. Because if the harm has been real, and if tradition and sin are genuinely difficult to disentangle — which the document implies — then the reparative obligation is not discharged by removing future barriers. It requires asking, with evidence and accountability, which inherited structures are themselves products of the sin being repented of. Alderwood does not ask that question. It asks women to trust that this particular restriction is on the right side of that line. That is a significant pastoral ask, and it requires more than assertion to sustain.

## II. The Formation Question — What Does This Practice Actually Teach?

I want to name a question that is underused in this debate and is mine to carry: not what the doctrinal statement says, but what the practice *forms* people to believe.

[CLAIM: Embodied communal practices shape the imagination and desires of a community more powerfully than doctrinal statements, forming what people believe about God through what they habitually do and see]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Smith argues that human beings are formed primarily by liturgical practices — the repeated, embodied rituals of communal life — and that these practices communicate theology at a level deeper than conscious doctrinal instruction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

When a congregation gathers Sunday after Sunday and the person who holds final doctrinal authority is always male — when the elder board that guards the community's life is exclusively male — that congregation is being formed to associate ultimate spiritual authority with maleness. The doctrinal statement can insist on equal dignity. The practice is teaching something else. This is not a claim about intent. It is a claim about formation, and it operates below the level of what anyone consciously intends.

The question this raises for Alderwood is not rhetorical. It is: what are your children learning about who God is and who gets to speak for God? And is that what you intend to teach them?

[CLAIM: Hierarchical authority structures that concentrate power without adequate accountability create structural conditions under which spiritual abuse is more likely to occur and less likely to be named]
[SOURCE: Diane Langberg, *Redeeming Power*, Brazos Press, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Langberg argues that abuse in Christian contexts is fundamentally a misuse of power, and that any system — including gender-exclusive leadership — that concentrates authority without structural accountability creates environments where the powerful are protected and the harmed are silenced]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[SOURCE: Scot McKnight and Laura Barringer, *A Church Called Tov*, Tyndale House, 2020]

[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

I am not arguing that complementarian churches are uniformly abusive. The Systematician has asked what kind of authority the elder exercises and where it comes from. [CLAIM: The pastoral cost of restriction falls with particular severity on women who experience genuine vocational calling, and the church's typical response — affirming the gift while closing the vocational shape — is insufficient as pastoral care]

[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

## V. Initial Picture of Faithful Practice — Provisional

What does my discipline suggest, provisionally? Not a settled conclusion — the exegetical and doctrinal questions are genuinely open, as my colleagues have shown — but a set of pastoral obligations that any position must discharge.

These are not conditions that resolve the exegetical question. They are conditions for pastoral integrity while the exegetical question remains open. And they are far more demanding than most churches maintaining restriction have recognized.

## The Social and Cultural Analyst

## Social and Cultural Analyst

### STATEMENT OF LIMITS

Social analysis can show that a reading emerged in a particular social context and serves particular social interests. It cannot show that the reading is therefore wrong — texts can have genuine transcultural authority even when interpreted in socially situated ways, and even readings that serve the interests of the powerful can be correct. If all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them. My discipline's greatest risk is explaining everything and thereby explaining nothing. I need the biblical scholar to establish what the texts actually say and the hermeneutician to adjudicate how much social location counts as evidence against a reading's validity. What I bring is this: a position that cannot examine its own social conditioning, or that claims to have transcended social situatedness while everyone else is trapped by it, has not yet done its intellectual homework.

Alderwood's document contains an admission that opens more than it closes: *"Much of what has kept women from flourishing and using their gifts within the church has not been the theology of the church but has been the sin of sexism and discrimination that is the result of the Fall."*

[CLAIM: Pierre Bourdieu's concept of "symbolic violence" describes the process by which hierarchies are maintained not through overt coercion but through the internalization of the hierarchy by those subject to it, such that subordination is experienced as natural, willed, or spiritually appropriate.]

I hold these findings as evidence to be weighed, not as verdicts to be delivered. The biblical scholar and hermeneutician must carry weight my discipline cannot. But a position that has not passed the test of social self-awareness is not yet fully honest — and full honesty is what this inquiry requires.

[ARGUMENT: Walton contends that reading Genesis 2's creation sequence as establishing male authority imports a Western logical assumption (earlier = superior) that the ANE genre does not support; the woman is created last as the culmination, not as an afterthought]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The man's naming of the woman in v.23 is often cited as establishing authority — naming implies lordship in the ANE. But the man's speech is poetic recognition, not administrative designation. He names the *category* (woman/man, *'ishah*/*'ish*), not an individual. The individual naming of Eve comes only after the fall (Genesis 3:20), which is itself exegetically significant.

[CLAIM: Phyllis Trible argues that the man's speech in Genesis 2:23 is a cry of recognition and delight, not an act of naming that establishes dominion, and that the parallel naming of animals (v.20) differs because those namings assign categories while v.23 identifies kinship]
[SOURCE: Phyllis Trible, *God and the Rhetoric of Sexuality*, Fortress, 1978]
[ARGUMENT: Trible distinguishes the poetic recognition in v.23 from the functional naming of animals, arguing the former establishes mutuality rather than hierarchy]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

**What this text establishes for the church:** The *ezer* designation resists subordination. The creation sequence is genuinely ambiguous — it can bear the weight Alderwood places on it, but only with hermeneutical commitments the text itself does not require. The strongest exegetical finding here is that the text establishes differentiated partnership, not a hierarchy whose specific form is specified. What form that differentiation takes in church office requires argument beyond Genesis 2.

### TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14

This is the text where my discipline must be most precise about what is and is not settled.

**On *authenteō*:** My Round 1 and Round 2 positions stand. The verb is a hapax legomenon. Payne's negative reading and Wolters' neutral reading both have lexical warrant.

[CLAIM: Linda Belleville argues that the conjunction *oude* linking "teach" and *authenteō* in v.12 suggests the two activities form a single prohibited complex — not two separate prohibitions — and that the negative connotation of *authenteō* colors the entire construction]
[SOURCE: Linda Belleville, in Thomas Schreiner and Andreas Köstenberger, eds., *Women in the Church*, 3rd ed., Crossway, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Belleville contends that the *oude* construction means Paul is prohibiting a specific kind of teaching that involves domineering behavior, not all teaching or all exercise of authority]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

**On the creation-order appeal in v.13:** I affirm what I said in Round 1 — Paul is doing what he appears to be doing. He grounds a behavioral norm in protological order. The hermeneutician has correctly identified that this creates a consistency problem with 1 Corinthians 11, which I address below. [CLAIM: N.T. [SOURCE: N.T. Wright, "Women's Service in the Church: The Biblical Basis," NTWrightPage.com]

**What this text establishes:** A real Pauline restriction, grounded in creation-order language, whose precise scope depends on resolving *authenteō* and whose universal application requires a hermeneutical argument neither side has fully supplied.

### TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16

This text does more work than Alderwood builds on it, and my discipline must press that point.

Paul *assumes* women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. This is not incidental. Prophecy in the Pauline corpus is a significant communicative act directed to the community for its edification, exhortation, and consolation (1 Cor 14:3). Paul's only concern in 11:2-16 is the manner of the practice, not its permissibility.

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 establishes that women's public prayer and prophecy in the gathered assembly was an accepted and expected practice in Pauline communities, and that Paul's instructions about head coverings presuppose rather than restrict this practice]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee contends that 11:5 is exegetically decisive for understanding Paul's overall position on women's public speech — it establishes the baseline from which 14:34-35 must be read, not the reverse]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**On *kephalē*:** The term is genuinely contested. Grudem argues it means "authority over"; Cervin, Payne, and others argue it means "source" or carries honor-related connotations without implying hierarchical authority.

[CLAIM: Wayne Grudem argues that *kephalē* in 1 Corinthians 11:3 means "authority over" and that the head-covering instruction reflects and reinforces a creational authority structure]
[SOURCE: Wayne Grudem, "Does κεφαλή ('Head') Mean 'Source' or 'Authority Over' in Greek Literature?", *Trinity Journal*, 1985]
[ARGUMENT: Grudem surveys Greek literature and argues the "source" meaning is not attested, making "authority over" the only defensible reading]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Richard Cervin argues that Grudem's survey is methodologically flawed and that *kephalē* in the relevant period carries connotations of prominence or honor without necessarily implying hierarchical authority]
[SOURCE: Richard Cervin, "Does κεφαλή Mean 'Source' or 'Authority Over' in Greek Literature?", *Trinity Journal*, 1989]
[ARGUMENT: Cervin's response to Grudem argues the lexical data supports a range of meanings, none of which straightforwardly maps onto the modern concept of institutional authority]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**What this text establishes for the church:** Women prophesy in the gathered assembly — this is Pauline assumption, not permission granted. The *kephalē* argument is genuinely contested and cannot be used to override this baseline finding. Alderwood's failure to build more on 1 Corinthians 11:5 is an exegetical gap in its argument.

### TEXT 4: 1 Corinthians 14:33-35

The tension with 11:5 is the central exegetical problem. Three readings are live:

**Reading 1 — Restriction on disruptive questioning.** Paul prohibits women from publicly evaluating or questioning prophetic utterances, a function reserved for recognized leaders. This reading has the advantage of explaining *why* women should ask their husbands at home — the home is where evaluation of prophecy happens within the household unit.

**Reading 2 — Corinthian slogan refuted in v.36.** Paul quotes a position held by some in Corinth ("Women should keep silent...") and then refutes it with the rhetorical question in v.36 ("Did the word of God originate with you?"). This reading has been developed by Lucy Peppiatt and is supported by the observation that Paul uses similar rhetorical devices elsewhere in 1 Corinthians.

[CLAIM: Lucy Peppiatt argues that 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 is a Corinthian slogan Paul quotes and then refutes in v.36, consistent with his rhetorical practice throughout the letter]
[SOURCE: Lucy Peppiatt, *Women and Worship at Corinth*, Cascade, 2015]
[ARGUMENT: Peppiatt contends that the abruptness of v.34-35, their apparent contradiction of 11:5, and the rhetorical force of v.36 are best explained by reading v.34-35 as Paul citing and then rejecting a position, not asserting one]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Reading 3 — Textual interpolation.** A minority of scholars, including Fee, argue the verses are a non-Pauline interpolation based on their textual displacement in some manuscripts.

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues the manuscript evidence — specifically that these verses appear after v.40 in some Western manuscripts — suggests they may be a non-Pauline interpolation, though he acknowledges the evidence is not decisive]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee treats the textual displacement as a significant indicator but does not make the interpolation case his primary argument]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**My assessment:** All three readings are exegetically defensible. Reading 1 (restriction on evaluating prophecy) and Reading 2 (Corinthian slogan) are the most linguistically plausible. What cannot be sustained is a reading that makes v.34-35 a universal prohibition on women's speech — because v.36's rhetorical force and the contradiction with 11:5 are too severe. The hermeneutical principle governing the holding is: 11:5 establishes the baseline of permitted practice; 14:34-35 must be read in light of it, not the reverse.

### TEXT 5: Galatians 3:28

The exegetical question here is whether Paul's phrase "no male and female" (*ouk... arsen kai thēlu*) quotes Genesis 1:27 and what follows from that quotation.

[CLAIM: N.T. Wright argues that the phrase "male and female" in Galatians 3:28 deliberately echoes Genesis 1:27's creation language, and that Paul's point is that in Christ the original creation unity is restored — which carries ecclesiological weight, not merely soteriological]
[SOURCE: N.T. Wright, "Women's Service in the Church: The Biblical Basis," NTWrightPage.com]
[ARGUMENT: Wright contends that the Genesis 1:27 echo is intentional and that Paul's statement has implications for how the new covenant community is structured, not only for individual salvation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The Alderwood document's reading — that the emphasis is on ending division and enmity, not erasing categories — is exegetically defensible but incomplete. Paul's formulation is not merely "Jew and Greek are reconciled" but "there is *no* Jew and Greek" — the categories are relativized within the new covenant community in a way that goes beyond mere reconciliation.

However, Alderwood is right that this text does not straightforwardly establish ecclesiological egalitarianism. Paul elsewhere maintains distinctions between Jews and Gentiles in practice (Romans 14-15) and between married and unmarried (1 Corinthians 7). The soteriological reading is the text's primary register.

**What this text establishes:** The new covenant community is characterized by the relativization of the male-female distinction as a basis for differential standing before God. Whether this relativization extends to church office requires a hermeneutical argument — specifically, whether the categories relativized in Galatians 3:28 include authority structures or only access to covenant membership. That argument is not supplied by the text alone.

## Statement of Disciplinary Limits

Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time in response to forces that are not purely exegetical. It cannot show that a reading is therefore wrong — texts can be misread consistently without that making the misreading correct, and a reading that emerges in a specific cultural moment is not invalidated by that emergence alone. My discipline cannot establish what the texts mean — that is the biblical scholar's work. It cannot adjudicate whether the history of a reading counts as evidence against its validity — that is the hermeneutician's work. What I can do is put the current reading in its actual historical context and ask: do you know where this reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

---

## TEXT 1: Genesis 2:18-23 — *Ezer*, Sequence, and the History of Reading Hierarchy into Creation

The reception of Genesis 2 as a warrant for male authority is ancient but not uniform, and its uniformity has been exaggerated by the modern debate.

The sequence argument — Adam created first, therefore Adam holds authority — was deployed by Chrysostom, Ambrose, and Augustine, but with different emphases and different conclusions about its practical reach.

[CLAIM: Augustine's reading of Genesis 2 in *De Genesi ad Litteram* treats the woman's subordination as belonging to the created order, but limits its application to the domestic sphere rather than treating it as a general principle of male public authority over women]
[SOURCE: Augustine, *De Genesi ad Litteram*, Book IX, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: Augustine's exegesis does not straightforwardly support a universal male-authority principle across all social contexts; his restriction is specifically marital and domestic, not ecclesial or civic in the way modern complementarianism requires]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

What reception history observes here: the reading of Genesis 2 as establishing male authority in the church specifically — rather than in marriage — required additional argumentative steps that different interpreters supplied differently. The sequence-implies-hierarchy reading was not self-evident to the earliest interpreters; it was constructed and applied unevenly.

The modern complementarian reading of Genesis 2 — which Alderwood inherits — treats sequence as establishing a creation-order principle with direct ecclesial application. This reading crystallized in the late twentieth century in the CBMW documents, not in the patristic period. The patristic period used Genesis 2 primarily for domestic and marital ordering.

[CLAIM: Beth Allison Barr documents that the application of Genesis 2 creation-order arguments to church office specifically — rather than marriage — is a distinctively modern evangelical move, not a continuous patristic tradition]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: The specific move of grounding church office restriction in Genesis 2 sequence is a product of the twentieth-century complementarian movement, not a recovery of ancient exegesis]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

What reception history cannot say: whether this reading is wrong. It can say the reading has a recent ecclesial application history and should not be presented as simply what Genesis 2 has always been understood to teach.

## TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14 — The History of *Authenteō* and the Genealogy of a Translation

This is where reception history has the most to contribute to the current debate.

The *authenteō* question the biblical scholar has correctly identified as genuinely contested did not emerge as contested in a vacuum. The translation history is itself evidence.

[CLAIM: The Vulgate renders *authenteō* in 1 Timothy 2:12 as *dominari* — "to dominate" or "to lord it over" — a translation that carries a more negative force than the modern complementarian rendering "to exercise authority"]
[SOURCE: Jerome, *Vulgata Clementina*, 1 Timothy 2:12, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: Jerome's rendering, which shaped Western Christian reading of this text for over a millennium, supports a more negative or restricted reading of *authenteō* than the modern complementarian translations that render it as neutral authority-exercise]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This matters enormously. The church that used the Vulgate for fifteen hundred years was reading a text that said women should not *domineer* men — not that women should not *exercise authority* over men. The distinction is precisely what Payne argues in the modern lexical debate. The Vulgate's rendering was not a modern egalitarian innovation; it was the standard Western reading.

[CLAIM: The shift toward rendering *authenteō* as "to have authority over" (neutral) rather than "to domineer" or "to usurp authority" (negative) accelerated in English translations from the 1970s onward, coinciding with the institutionalization of complementarianism]
[SOURCE: Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: The translation trajectory moved away from the Vulgate's negative rendering toward a neutral-authority rendering at precisely the moment when the complementarian movement required a stronger textual basis for its position]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

What this means for the current debate: Alderwood's reading of 1 Timothy 2:12 as prohibiting women from "the specific kind of authoritative teaching that is reserved for the elders" is itself a reading with a history — and that history runs counter to fifteen hundred years of Western Christian interpretation of *authenteō* as *dominari*.

The creation-order appeal in verse 13 has a similarly complex reception. The text was used by virtually every major patristic interpreter to restrict women's public speech — but the scope of that restriction varied considerably. Tertullian applied it maximally. Chrysostom applied it to public teaching while recognizing women's prophetic and diaconal roles. The modern complementarian reading, which restricts women specifically from the elder office while permitting them to teach, preach, and lead — is in some respects *more permissive* than most patristic readings, not a recovery of them.

## TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 — Head Coverings, Prophecy, and the Selective Reception

Alderwood's document does not build on 1 Corinthians 11 as a positive warrant for women's ministry, and the reception history explains why: this text has been systematically read for its restriction (head coverings) rather than its assumption (women prophesy).

The history of reading 1 Corinthians 11 is a case study in selective reception. The assumption Paul builds on — that women *do* pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly — was largely passed over in the tradition, while the head-covering instruction became a site of extended controversy. Tertullian, Chrysostom, and Clement of Alexandria all wrote about head coverings at length. None of them built on 1 Corinthians 11:5 as a positive warrant for women's public ministry.

[CLAIM: Tertullian argued in *De Virginibus Velandis* that women should be veiled not only when praying and prophesying but at all times in the assembly, effectively negating the assumption of active female participation that Paul's instruction presupposes]
[SOURCE: Tertullian, *De Virginibus Velandis*, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: Tertullian's reception of 1 Corinthians 11 demonstrates how an instruction that presupposes women's active participation was received in a way that minimized rather than built on that presupposition]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The hermeneutician has identified the consistency problem: if creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2 transcultural, why not 1 Corinthians 11? Reception history adds a layer: virtually no tradition has applied 1 Corinthians 11 consistently. Head coverings were required, then optional, then abandoned — while the assumed female participation was neither affirmed nor extended. The text was read for its restriction while its assumption was suppressed.

This selective reception is itself data about interpretive interests. The assumption of female prophetic activity in the gathered assembly was available to every interpreter who read 1 Corinthians 11. That it was not developed as a positive warrant for women's ministry is not an exegetical finding — it is a reception-historical finding about which parts of texts interpreters choose to build on.

## TEXT 4: 1 Corinthians 14:33-35 — The Silence Passage and Its Contested History

The reception history of this passage is unusually complex and directly relevant to the current debate.

The "Corinthian slogan" reading — that verses 34-35 represent a position Paul quotes and then refutes in verse 36 — is not a modern egalitarian innovation. It has patristic antecedents, though they are minority voices.

[CLAIM: The Western manuscript tradition shows significant variation in the placement of 1 Corinthians 14:34-35, with some manuscripts placing these verses after verse 40, suggesting early scribal uncertainty about the passage's location and function]
[SOURCE: Philip Payne, *Man and Woman, One in Christ*, Zondervan, 2009]
[ARGUMENT: Payne argues the textual instability of these verses — attested in the manuscript tradition itself — supports the view that they were not original to Paul's letter in this location, which would affect how they function in the argument]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

What reception history observes: the "silence" text has been applied with extraordinary variability across the tradition. The Eastern church applied it primarily to restrict women from teaching in the liturgical assembly. The Western church applied it variously — sometimes to all speech, sometimes to questions, sometimes to judging prophecy. No tradition applied it literally to prohibit all female speech in worship, because all traditions read 1 Corinthians 11:5 and recognized the contradiction.

The variability of application is itself significant. A text whose application has been this varied across competent interpreters is not a text whose meaning is self-evident. The current debate's intensity around this passage reflects a modern sharpening of a question the tradition left deliberately imprecise.

## TEXT 5: Galatians 3:28 — The Soteriological Containment of a Radical Text

The reception history of Galatians 3:28 is a case study in the containment of a text's implications.

[CLAIM: Galatians 3:28 was cited by early Christian writers including Origen to argue for the spiritual equality of male and female — but was consistently interpreted as referring to spiritual status before God rather than social or ecclesial ordering]
[SOURCE: Origen, *Commentary on Galatians*, fragments, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: The early church's reception of Galatians 3:28 consistently limited its application to the soteriological domain, treating it as a statement about salvation rather than about community structure — a containment strategy that has persisted through the modern debate]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The modern egalitarian use of Galatians 3:28 as an ecclesiological warrant is, reception-historically, a development — not a recovery of a suppressed reading, but an extension of the text into territory the tradition had not applied it to. Alderwood's response — that the text addresses the ending of division and enmity, not the erasure of categories — is a version of the traditional containment reading.

What reception history cannot say: which reading is correct. What it can say: both readings have histories. The egalitarian reading extends the text beyond its traditional application; the complementarian reading inherits a containment strategy that itself has a history and served certain interests — specifically, the interest of maintaining existing social and ecclesial structures.

The phrase "no male and female" (οὐκ ἔνι ἄρσεν καὶ θῆλυ) echoes Genesis 1:27, and this echo was noted by some patristic interpreters. That it was not developed as a creation-order counter-argument to the creation-order arguments in 1 Timothy 2 and 1 Corinthians 11 is itself a reception-historical datum — the tradition did not use Galatians 3:28 to challenge the restriction texts, even when the linguistic connection to Genesis was visible.

## TEXT 6: Romans 16 — The History of Suppression and Recovery

I addressed the Junia evidence in Round 2. Here I extend that analysis to the full Romans 16 picture.

Romans 16 is the single most significant passage for reception history in this debate, because the history of its interpretation is a history of progressive suppression followed by modern recovery — and the mechanism of suppression is identifiable and non-exegetical.

[CLAIM: Phoebe's designation as *diakonos* and *prostatis* was recognized by Origen as referring to genuine ministerial and leadership roles, but subsequent Western interpretation increasingly rendered these terms as "servant" and "helper" — diminutives that the Greek does not require]
[SOURCE: Origen, *Commentary on Romans*, Book X, primary source; and Nijay Gupta, *Tell Her Story*, IVP Academic, 2023, for the modern recovery]
[ARGUMENT: The diminution of Phoebe's titles in Western translation tradition is a reception-historical phenomenon traceable to interpretive pressure, not to lexical evidence — Origen's reading, which recognized the ministerial weight of the terms, was available throughout the tradition and was set aside]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The Junia evidence I documented in Round 2 follows the same pattern: a reading available to the earliest interpreters (female apostle), suppressed without textual warrant (masculinized name), recovered in modern scholarship through return to primary sources.

The Priscilla precedent — named before Aquila in four of six New Testament references, described as Paul's co-worker, depicted as teaching Apollos — was consistently noted by patristic interpreters but consistently explained away. Chrysostom praised her but did not extend her example to his own ecclesial context.

[CLAIM: The pattern across Romans 16 — Phoebe, Junia, Priscilla — is one of recognition followed by containment: the patristic interpreters acknowledged these women's roles but did not allow them to function as precedents for their own ecclesial practice]
[SOURCE: Kevin Madigan and Carolyn Osiek, *Ordained Women in the Early Church*, Johns Hopkins University Press, 2005]
[ARGUMENT: Madigan and Osiek document this recognition-and-containment pattern as characteristic of patristic reception of evidence for women's ministry — the evidence was not suppressed through ignorance but through deliberate interpretive choices about what would count as normative]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This pattern — recognition without extension — is the single most important finding reception history brings to this debate. The tradition knew the evidence. It chose not to build on it. That choice was not purely exegetical.

## TEXT 7: Judges 4-5 and 2 Kings 22 — Exception, Precedent, and the Grammar of Canonical Silence

Deborah and Huldah present reception history with a revealing datum: the tradition's treatment of these texts is almost uniformly one of exception-making without textual warrant.

The texts themselves offer no exception language. Judges 4:4 introduces Deborah as "a prophet, the wife of Lappidoth, who was leading Israel at that time" — without apology, without explanation of why a woman fills this role, without any suggestion that the arrangement is anomalous. The same is true of Huldah in 2 Kings 22.

[CLAIM: John Calvin, in his commentary on Judges, described Deborah's leadership as an extraordinary divine dispensation — an exception that confirmed rather than challenged the general rule of male authority — a reading that became the standard Protestant exegetical move for handling female leadership in the Old Testament]
[SOURCE: John Calvin, *Commentaries on the Book of Judges*, primary source]
[ARGUMENT: Calvin's "extraordinary dispensation" reading is not derived from the text of Judges 4-5, which offers no such qualification, but is imposed on the text to harmonize it with a prior commitment to male authority as the norm]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a crucial reception-historical observation: the "exception" reading of Deborah is itself an interpretive imposition. The text does not call her an exception. Interpreters called her an exception because they needed her to be one.

[CLAIM: Beth Allison Barr documents that the "exceptional woman" trope — the idea that women who exercised leadership in biblical history were anomalous divine dispensations rather than normative precedents — has been a persistent strategy in complementarian exegesis for managing inconvenient female leadership texts]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: The "exceptional woman" reading is a reception strategy that protects a prior commitment to male authority from the challenge posed by texts like Judges 4-5 and 2 Kings 22]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The question the biblical scholar rightly raises — is Deborah a precedent, an exception, or something that resists the binary? — is a question reception history can illuminate but not answer. What reception history can say is this: the exception reading was not derived from the text. It was brought to the text. That is a finding about interpreters, not about Deborah.

## TEXT 1: Genesis 2:18-23 (250 words)

The hermeneutician's question here is not what the text says but what principle of reading determines what it *establishes for the church*. Every advocate has already brought a prior hermeneutical commitment to this text before reading it.

The sequence argument — Adam formed first, therefore authority — requires a general principle: *temporal priority in narrative establishes normative hierarchy*. Applied consistently, this principle yields conclusions virtually no interpreter accepts: animals were created before Adam, therefore animals have authority over humans. The animals-before-Adam reductio is not a gotcha — it is the consistency test identifying that the principle as stated is too broad. What is actually operative is something more specific: *within the creation of the human pair, sequence signals role differentiation*. That is a defensible principle, but it is narrower than the one stated, and it requires defense on its own terms.

[CLAIM: Grant Osborne argues that narrative sequence in Genesis does not automatically carry normative weight without additional contextual signals identifying the sequence as theologically significant]
[SOURCE: Grant Osborne, *The Hermeneutical Spiral*, IVP Academic, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Osborne's discussion of narrative hermeneutics holds that the theological significance of narrative sequence must be established by contextual markers, not assumed from the sequence itself]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The ANE context the social analyst and biblical scholar have introduced complicates this further: if primogeniture assumptions in the ancient world explain why sequence carried authority implications, then the principle may be culturally embedded rather than transcultural. The text does not tell us which. That is a hermeneutical gap, not an exegetical one.

*What this text establishes for the church* cannot be determined from the text alone. It requires a prior account of how narrative sequence functions normatively — an account that must survive the consistency test.

## TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14 (450 words)

This is the debate's methodological center, and I have more to say here than I have said.

The consistency test I applied in Rounds 1 and 2 stands: Paul grounds both the head-covering instruction (1 Corinthians 11:8-9) and the teaching prohibition (1 Timothy 2:13) in creation order. The principle "creation-order grounding marks transcultural norms" cannot be applied to one and not the other without a principled distinction between them. Alderwood has not supplied that distinction. Neither has any other advocate in this debate.

I want to advance the argument by naming what a principled distinction *would* require. It would need to show that the creation-order appeals function differently in each text — not merely that the *conclusions* are different, but that the *arguments* operate at different levels. One candidate: the 1 Corinthians 11 appeal is embedded in a passage that concludes with an appeal to church custom ("we have no such practice, nor do the churches of God," v.16), suggesting Paul himself signals the cultural-contextual dimension. The 1 Timothy 2 appeal is not similarly bounded. This is a textual observation, not a hermeneutical invention — and it is the kind of internal textual evidence that could, in principle, justify differential treatment.

[CLAIM: Gordon Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11:16's appeal to church custom signals that Paul recognizes the contextual dimension of the head-covering instruction, distinguishing it from the creation-order principle it invokes]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: The presence of a custom-appeal alongside the creation-order appeal in 1 Corinthians 11 suggests Paul himself distinguishes the cultural application from the theological grounding]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This is a genuine hermeneutical resource. It does not resolve the consistency problem — it narrows it. The question becomes: is the absence of a custom-appeal in 1 Timothy 2 sufficient to establish that the creation-order argument there carries universal force? Or could the absence simply reflect the letter's different rhetorical situation?

On *authenteō*: the biblical scholar has correctly established genuine lexical ambiguity. My hermeneutical observation is that the lexical ambiguity and the consistency problem are connected. If *authenteō* means "to domineer" rather than "to exercise authority," then what Paul prohibits is a specific abusive posture, not all exercise of authority — and the creation-order grounding explains why domineering by women would be particularly disordered, without requiring a universal prohibition on authority. This reading integrates the lexical and hermeneutical evidence more coherently than either the maximalist complementarian or the dismissive egalitarian reading.

[CLAIM: I. Howard Marshall argued that the combination of lexical and contextual evidence in 1 Timothy 2 supports a reading focused on the specific abuse of authority rather than authority as such]
[SOURCE: I. Howard Marshall, *A Critical and Exegetical Commentary on the Pastoral Epistles*, ICC, T&T Clark, 1999]
[ARGUMENT: Marshall's ICC commentary reads the passage in light of the Ephesian context and the lexical range of authenteō to argue for a more restricted prohibition than the complementarian reading requires]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I hold this provisionally. It is a more internally consistent reading than its competitors. Whether it is the correct reading requires the biblical scholar's judgment on the lexical evidence and the systematician's judgment on what kind of authority the text is addressing.

## TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 (350 words)

The hermeneutician's primary observation here is that this text is doing two things simultaneously, and conflating them produces the consistency problem.

First, the text *assumes* women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. This is not a concession or a permission — it is a presupposition. Paul's concern is the manner of the practice, not whether it occurs. The biblical scholar has correctly noted that Alderwood does not build enough on this. My hermeneutical observation is that the assumed legitimacy of women's prophetic speech in the assembly creates a hermeneutical constraint on how the restriction texts can be read: any reading of 1 Timothy 2:12 or 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 that prohibits all public speech by women in the assembly contradicts what 1 Corinthians 11:5 assumes. This is not a trajectory argument or a cultural argument — it is an internal canonical constraint.

Second, the text grounds the head-covering instruction in creation order (vv.8-9) and in "nature" (v.14) and in church custom (v.16). The presence of all three grounds in the same passage is hermeneutically significant: it suggests Paul can appeal to creation order while simultaneously recognizing that the specific application is shaped by nature-as-culturally-perceived and by ecclesial custom. This is the textual evidence that the creation-order appeal and the cultural conditionedness of the application are not mutually exclusive — which is the key to the consistency problem.

[CLAIM: Anthony Thiselton argues that 1 Corinthians 11's multiple grounds for the head-covering instruction — creation, nature, custom — reflect Paul's awareness that the theological principle and its cultural expression are distinguishable]
[SOURCE: Anthony Thiselton, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NIGTC, Eerdmans, 2000]
[ARGUMENT: Thiselton reads the passage as showing that creation-order grounding does not automatically determine the cultural form of the practice it grounds, because Paul himself appeals to both simultaneously]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

On *kephalē*: the debate between "source" and "authority" readings is genuine. My hermeneutical observation is that the meaning of *kephalē* cannot be determined by lexical study alone — it must be read within the rhetorical logic of the passage, where the head/body relationship is about honor and shame as much as about governance. The systematician's question about what kind of authority is at stake is the right one.

## TEXT 4: 1 Corinthians 14:33-35 (200 words)

The Peppiatt reading — that vv.34-35 are a Corinthian slogan Paul refutes in v.36 — is hermeneutically interesting but requires a specific rhetorical claim: that Paul uses the diatribal device of quoting an interlocutor without marking it explicitly. Alderwood's document notes Paul uses this device elsewhere in 1 Corinthians (6:12, 7:1, 8:1). The hermeneutical question is whether the device is marked in those cases and unmarked here, or whether it is consistently unmarked and must be inferred from context.

[CLAIM: Lucy Peppiatt argues that 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 is a Corinthian position Paul refutes, and that verse 36's rhetorical force is best explained as a rebuttal of the preceding quoted position]
[SOURCE: Lucy Peppiatt, *Women and Worship at Corinth*, Cascade, 2015]
[ARGUMENT: Peppiatt contends that the diatribal reading is contextually required to make sense of verse 36 and to avoid contradiction with 1 Corinthians 11:5]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutical principle governing the holding of this text alongside 1 Corinthians 11:5 is: *internal canonical coherence takes priority over maximalist readings of individual passages*. If 11:5 assumes women prophesy publicly, then 14:34's "be silent" cannot mean absolute silence. The exegete must explain what it does mean. That is a constraint, not a conclusion.

## TEXT 5: Galatians 3:28 (250 words)

The hermeneutical question here precedes the exegetical one: *what kind of text is this, and what kind of claim does it make?* The soteriological/ecclesiological distinction is itself a hermeneutical decision before it is an exegetical finding.

[CLAIM: Richard Hays argues that Galatians 3:28 functions as a "performative declaration" within the logic of Pauline theology, not merely a soteriological statement about individual standing before God]
[SOURCE: Richard Hays, *The Moral Vision of the New Testament*, HarperOne, 1996]
[ARGUMENT: Hays contends that the text's rhetorical force is ecclesiological — it describes the community constituted by baptism — and that limiting it to individual soteriology requires a distinction the text itself does not make]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The observation that "no male and female" echoes Genesis 1:27 is hermeneutically significant if correct: it would mean Paul is invoking the creation account to argue that the new creation in Christ transcends the distinctions of the old creation — which is precisely the opposite move from the creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13. Both texts appeal to creation; they appear to draw opposite conclusions. The hermeneutician must ask: is that a contradiction requiring resolution, or does Paul appeal to different aspects of creation for different purposes?

My answer: it is not a contradiction if Paul is arguing in 1 Timothy 2 about *order within the community* and in Galatians 3 about *membership in the community*. But that distinction is a hermeneutical imposition unless the texts themselves support it. Alderwood makes this distinction without defending it. The defense requires showing that the rhetorical contexts genuinely differ in the way the distinction claims.

## TEXT 6: Romans 16 (200 words)

The hermeneutician's contribution here is about burden of proof. The reception historian has shown that Junia was read as female and apostolic by the earliest competent interpreters. The biblical scholar has shown that *diakonos* and *prostatis* carry more weight than diminutive translations allow. The hermeneutical question is: when the natural reading of a text is clear and the alternative requires special explanation, where does the burden of proof fall?

[CLAIM: E.D. Hirsch argued that the burden of proof falls on the interpreter who proposes a meaning that departs from the most natural reading supported by the text's linguistic conventions]
[SOURCE: E.D. Hirsch, *Validity in Interpretation*, Yale University Press, 1967]
[ARGUMENT: Hirsch's principle of textual probability holds that the most probable meaning — the one requiring the least explanatory apparatus — has presumptive authority, and departures from it require positive justification]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Applied here: the reading of Junia as female and apostolic, Phoebe as a deacon with patronal authority, and Priscilla as a lead teacher requires no special apparatus — it is what the words most naturally say. The alternative readings require inventing an unattested masculine name, diminishing a term used of Paul himself, and explaining why the woman is named first. The burden of proof falls on the restrictive reading, and it has not been discharged.

## TEXT 7: Judges 4-5 and 2 Kings 22 (150 words)

The hermeneutical question is whether "exception" or "precedent" is itself a hermeneutically neutral description, or whether the choice between them is already a conclusion dressed as a category.

The text presents Deborah and Huldah without apology, explanation, or qualification. The "exception" framing is imposed by interpreters who bring a prior commitment to a male-leadership norm and must account for these figures within it. The "precedent" framing is imposed by interpreters who bring a prior commitment to gender-inclusive leadership and recruit these figures as evidence. Neither framing is derived from the text itself.

[CLAIM: Scot McKnight argues that Deborah and Huldah cannot be reduced to either exception or precedent without importing a prior hermeneutical commitment that the texts themselves do not supply]
[SOURCE: Scot McKnight, *The Blue Parakeet*, Zondervan, 2008]
[ARGUMENT: McKnight contends that the texts present these women as straightforwardly authoritative figures whose gender is noted but not apologized for, and that the interpretive categories imposed on them reveal the interpreter's prior commitments more than the text's intent]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

What the canon establishes is that God worked through these women in these ways. What follows for the church requires a hermeneutical argument the texts do not supply. Both sides must own that.

## The Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian

# ROUND 3 — SEVEN REQUIRED TEXTS
## Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian

---

### TEXT 1: Genesis 2:18-23
**What the text establishes — and what it cannot establish alone**

Alderwood reads Genesis 2 as establishing pre-fall differentiation of role, citing the ordering of creation, the naming of woman, and the *ezer* designation. I have no quarrel with the observation that differentiation precedes the fall. What I question is the ecclesiological conclusion drawn from it.

The critical systematic question is: what kind of differentiation is present here? The text presents relational differentiation — male and female as constitutively interdependent — not hierarchical differentiation in the sense of one holding authority over the other. Adam names the animals in an act of dominion; he does not "name" Eve in the same sense. His declaration in verse 23 is recognition, not designation of authority.

[CLAIM: Phyllis Trible argues that the man's speech in Genesis 2:23 is poetic recognition of kinship identity, not an act of authority over the woman, and that the naming dynamic differs structurally from the animal-naming in 2:19-20]
[SOURCE: Phyllis Trible, *God and the Rhetoric of Sexuality*, Fortress, 1978]
[ARGUMENT: The man's exclamation is ecstatic recognition, not authoritative designation; the authority-conferring naming applies to animals, not to the woman who is his equal]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

More importantly for my discipline: even if we grant pre-fall relational differentiation, the move from differentiation-in-creation to restriction-in-eldership requires a doctrine of the church that Genesis 2 does not supply. The church is not simply the household writ large. It is the community of the new creation, constituted by the Spirit. Whether creation-order differentiation governs ecclesial office is precisely the question Genesis 2 cannot answer on its own terms. It requires a doctrine of the church.

**Alderwood's tension:** The document affirms that the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires male-and-female-together, then uses creation differentiation to restrict one sex from the office that represents divine authority. These are in tension. If the fullness of God's image requires both, a representational account of eldership must grapple with what an all-male eldership represents.

### TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14
**The ecclesiological question beneath the exegetical one**

The biblical scholar and hermeneutician have handled the lexical and consistency problems with precision I will not duplicate. My contribution is doctrinal.

Paul's creation-order appeal in verse 13 has been treated as either transcultural (complementarian) or culturally embedded (egalitarian). But systematic theology asks a prior question: what *kind* of authority is Paul restricting? The answer to that question determines which doctrine of ministry is actually at stake.

If the authority Paul restricts is *ontological* — a status conferred — then the restriction is about who may *be* an elder, and gender might belong to that status. If the authority is *functional* — a role that performs certain acts — then the restriction is about who may perform those acts, and the question is whether women can perform them. If the authority is *relational* — recognized from the community — then the restriction concerns who the community may recognize.

[CLAIM: Stanley Grenz argues that Paul's restriction in 1 Timothy 2 addresses a specific functional role of authoritative doctrinal adjudication, not a comprehensive prohibition on all female teaching or leadership]
[SOURCE: Stanley Grenz, *Women in the Church*, IVP, 1995]
[ARGUMENT: Grenz contends that the restriction is narrower than typically read, targeting the specific authoritative-teaching function that belongs to recognized community leaders, not all forms of women's public speech or instruction]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood reads the text as restricting the functional role of elder. But its creation-order argument implies the restriction is grounded in something prior to function — something about what maleness *is* in relation to authority. These are different arguments, and they require different doctrines of ministry. Alderwood has not resolved which it holds.

What this text cannot settle: whether the authority Paul has in mind is the same authority the twenty-first century elder exercises. The elder in a free-church Baptist congregation holds a very different kind of authority than the *episkopos* in the Pastoral Epistles' context. The mapping requires ecclesiological work the text alone cannot do.

### TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16
**The text Alderwood underuses — and why that underuse is revealing**

This text is the most ecclesiologically significant for my discipline's question, and Alderwood does not build on it. That omission is telling.

Paul assumes women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. This is not incidental. Prophecy in the New Testament is not private speech — it is authoritative public declaration addressed to the community for its edification, exhortation, and consolation (1 Corinthians 14:3). If women exercise this function in the gathered assembly, the functional account of eldership — which Alderwood appears to hold — faces a significant challenge: what function does the elder perform that the prophesying woman does not?

[CLAIM: Wolfhart Pannenberg argues that the Spirit's charismatic gifts, including prophecy, constitute a form of authority in the community that is not reducible to office but that the church must reckon with as a genuine form of Spirit-given authority]
[SOURCE: Wolfhart Pannenberg, *Systematic Theology*, Vol. 3, Eerdmans, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Pannenberg distinguishes charismatic authority from institutional authority but insists both are real, and that the church's institutional structures must be responsive to the Spirit's charismatic distribution]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The *kephalē* question — source or authority — is genuinely contested, and I will not pretend otherwise. But the systematic point stands regardless: if *kephalē* means "source," the text grounds differentiation in origin, not in authority-relations. If it means "authority," the text grounds a relational ordering — but one that coexists with women's public prophetic speech. Either reading leaves the functional account of eldership with a narrower restriction than Alderwood's creation-order argument implies.

**Why Alderwood underuses this text:** Because it creates pressure against the restriction rather than supporting it. A document that builds its case from the restriction texts while underusing the texts that attest women's active public ministry is not reading the whole canon. The hermeneutician is right that this selective reading requires explanation.

### TEXT 4: 1 Corinthians 14:33-35
**The hermeneutical choice and its ecclesiological stakes**

The Peppiatt reading — that verses 34-35 represent a Corinthian slogan Paul refutes in verse 36 — is exegetically defensible but not the only defensible reading. My discipline's contribution is to ask what the choice between readings implies ecclesiologically.

[CLAIM: Lucy Peppiatt argues that 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 is a quotation of a Corinthian position that Paul refutes in verse 36, consistent with his use of the same rhetorical device elsewhere in the letter]
[SOURCE: Lucy Peppiatt, *Women and Worship at Corinth*, Cascade, 2015]
[ARGUMENT: Peppiatt contends that reading verses 34-35 as Paul's own instruction creates an irresolvable contradiction with 1 Corinthians 11:5, and that the quotation-refutation reading resolves this without requiring special pleading]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

If Peppiatt is right, the text is not a restriction at all — it is Paul defending women's participation against a Corinthian attempt to silence them. If the traditional reading is right, the restriction applies to some specific form of speech (disruptive questioning, doctrinal adjudication) that is distinct from prayer and prophecy.

The ecclesiological stakes: the choice between readings determines whether the New Testament contains a consistent witness to women's public speech in gathered worship, or a genuine tension between restriction and permission. My discipline's finding is that the tension, if real, requires a doctrine of the church that can hold both — and that doctrine will determine how the tension is resolved.

What neither reading establishes: a comprehensive doctrine of ecclesial office. The text is about speech in gathered worship, not about who may hold the office of elder. The move from this text to elder restriction requires additional doctrinal work.

### TEXT 5: Galatians 3:28
**The ecclesiological weight of the text — and its limits**

Alderwood reads Galatians 3:28 as soteriological only, not ecclesiological. This is a common complementarian move, and it deserves systematic scrutiny.

[CLAIM: N.T. Wright argues that Galatians 3:28 functions within Paul's argument about membership in the Abrahamic covenant community — the people of God — and that this community is the church, meaning the text has direct ecclesiological implications]
[SOURCE: N.T. Wright, *The Letter to the Galatians*, Eerdmans, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: Wright contends that the soteriology/ecclesiology distinction is artificial in Paul's thinking — membership in the covenant community is simultaneously salvific and ecclesial, and Galatians 3:28 addresses both dimensions]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The phrase "no male and female" quotes Genesis 1:27, the creation of humanity in the *imago Dei*. This is not accidental. Paul is invoking the original creation of humanity as the basis for the new-covenant community's constitution. If the *imago Dei* is the basis, and if the *imago Dei* requires male-and-female-together for its fullness (as Alderwood itself affirms), then the new-covenant community reconstitutes the fullness of the *imago Dei* in a way that has implications for its internal structure.

This does not settle the elder question. But it does place a burden of proof on any position that restricts the fullness of the *imago Dei*'s expression within the community's leadership. The burden is not insuperable — but it must be carried, not ignored.

**The honest limit:** Galatians 3:28 establishes the *character* of the new-covenant community. It does not specify its *structure*. The move from character to structure requires ecclesiological argument the text alone cannot supply.

### TEXT 6: Romans 16
**What the designations establish — and what they require**

The biblical scholar has handled Phoebe and Junia with precision. My contribution is the ecclesiological question their designations raise.

Phoebe is *diakonos* and *prostatis*. If *prostatis* carries connotations of patronal authority and leadership — as Gupta argues — then Phoebe exercises a form of authority over the community she serves. This is not the authority of elder or bishop, but it is authority. What doctrine of authority makes sense of a *prostatis* who is not an elder?

[CLAIM: Miroslav Volf argues that in free-church ecclesiology, authority is distributed across the community rather than concentrated in a single office, and that recognized roles like patronage and diaconal service constitute genuine forms of authority]
[SOURCE: Miroslav Volf, *After Our Likeness*, Eerdmans, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Volf's ecclesiology makes room for multiple forms of authority in the community, not all of which are reducible to the elder office]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Junia as "outstanding among the apostles" — if that reading is correct — represents a woman exercising apostolic function, which in the Pauline corpus includes church-founding, doctrinal authority, and community formation. This is a form of authority that exceeds what Alderwood permits the non-elder. The ecclesiological question this raises is unavoidable: if the Spirit appointed a woman to apostolic function, what doctrine of authority explains why the Spirit cannot appoint a woman to elder function?

The burden of proof here runs against restriction, not for it. A position that restricts women from eldership while acknowledging apostolic women in the New Testament owes an account of why apostolic authority exceeds elder authority in the direction of restriction.

### TEXT 7: Judges 4-5 and 2 Kings 22
**What Deborah and Huldah establish — and why the binary fails**

The "precedent or exception" binary is the wrong frame, and systematic theology can say why.

Deborah holds judicial, prophetic, and military authority simultaneously. The text presents no apology, no explanation of why a woman holds these roles, no suggestion that the situation is anomalous. Huldah is consulted for authoritative interpretation of the law by the king's male officials — including the priest Hilkiah — despite male prophets being available. Again, no apology.

[CLAIM: Amy Peeler argues that the Old Testament's presentation of women in prophetic and judicial roles without narrative qualification is evidence that the restriction of such roles to men is not a consistent biblical pattern but a tendency that admits of genuine exception at the Spirit's direction]
[SOURCE: Amy Peeler, *Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022]
[ARGUMENT: Peeler contends that the Spirit's freedom to appoint women to authoritative roles in the Old Testament creates a canonical pattern that bears on the question of who the Spirit may appoint to authoritative roles in the new-covenant community]
[CONFIDENCE: LOW]

The systematic question these texts force is: what doctrine of authority governs these appointments? If authority in Israel was purely institutional — conferred by human appointment within established structures — then Deborah and Huldah are anomalies requiring explanation. But if authority in Israel was also charismatic — conferred by the Spirit's direct appointment — then these texts establish that the Spirit is not constrained by the institutional patterns that typically govern appointment. That is a pneumatological claim with direct ecclesiological implications.

Alderwood's position requires that the Spirit's charismatic freedom in the Old Testament is constrained by an institutional pattern in the New Testament. That is a defensible claim — but it requires a doctrine of the Spirit's relation to ecclesial institution that Alderwood has not provided. The text resists the "exception" framing precisely because exceptions require a rule the text does not articulate. What the text articulates is the Spirit's freedom. The question is what constrains it — and that is a doctrinal question, not an exegetical one.

**Concluding integration:** Across all seven texts, the pattern my discipline finds is consistent: the surface exegetical debates cannot be resolved without prior doctrinal decisions about what the church is, what authority is, where it comes from, and how the Spirit's charismatic freedom relates to institutional structure. Alderwood has made those decisions implicitly. This debate requires making them explicit.

## The Pastoral Theologian

# ROUND 3 — PASTORAL THEOLOGIAN
## The Seven Required Texts: A Pastoral and Missiological Reading

### TEXT 1: Genesis 2:18-23 — *Ezer*, Formation, and the Weight of Origin Stories

**~200 words**

The pastoral question this text raises is not primarily exegetical — it is formative. How does a congregation hear this text? What does it teach, week by week, about the structure of human relationship?

Alderwood correctly refuses to read *ezer* as subordinate assistance. The biblical scholar and systematician have confirmed this. But the pastoral observation I carry is different: even when *ezer* is correctly taught as "indispensable strength," the surrounding narrative — woman created after man, from man, for man's need — is being received by congregations whose imagination has been formed by centuries of hierarchical reading. Correct doctrine does not automatically correct formative damage.

[CLAIM: Embodied practices and repeated narrative framings shape communal imagination at a level deeper than conscious doctrinal instruction, meaning that correct teaching about *ezer* does not automatically undo what the surrounding narrative structure has formed people to feel]
[SOURCE: James K.A. Smith, *You Are What You Love*, Brazos Press, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Smith argues that liturgical and narrative practices form desire and imagination prior to conscious belief, making counter-formation an active and ongoing pastoral task, not a one-time doctrinal correction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The pastoral obligation this creates: any church that teaches Genesis 2 must actively build counter-formative practices — naming women as *ezer* in the military/divine sense, not the domestic-helper sense — or it will form its children to believe something its doctrine denies. That counter-formation work is largely absent from Alderwood's document.

What this text does *not* establish for my discipline: it does not settle the hierarchy question. What it does establish is that origin stories are among the most powerful formative texts a community possesses. How they are taught matters as much as what they say.

### TEXT 2: 1 Timothy 2:11-14 — The Pastoral Cost of Contested Certainty

**~300 words**

My colleagues have established the exegetical and hermeneutical terrain with care. The *authenteō* dispute is genuine. The consistency problem with 1 Corinthians 11 is unresolved. The Ephesian context is relevant. I accept all of this and add the pastoral layer.

The pastoral problem with Alderwood's handling of this text is not its conclusion. It is its tone. The document presents this passage as yielding a relatively clear restriction — "it is most likely that Paul is prohibiting women from performing the specific kind of authoritative teaching reserved for the elders" — while the hermeneutician has demonstrated that the hermeneutical basis for that confidence has not survived the consistency test.

What does that mean for the woman sitting in the pew who is told this text settles her vocation's ceiling? She is being asked to bear the pastoral cost of a restriction whose exegetical basis is genuinely contested, presented with the confidence of settled conviction.

[CLAIM: The pastoral cost of restriction falls with particular severity on women who experience genuine vocational calling, and the church's response to that cost has often been inadequate]
[SOURCE: Ruth Tucker, *Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*, Zondervan, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Tucker argues that calling has a vocational shape that cannot be redirected without pastoral cost that must be owned — and that the church's typical response has been to acknowledge the gift while permanently closing the vocational form]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutician established in Round 2 that honest uncertainty requires naming that uncertainty to the people affected. I press this further: naming the uncertainty is not merely an epistemic obligation. It is a pastoral one. A woman told "Scripture clearly restricts you" when Scripture does not clearly restrict her — when the text is genuinely contested at the decisive lexical point — has been misled about the nature of the burden she is being asked to carry.

This does not resolve the exegetical question. It requires that however the question is held, it be held honestly — with the uncertainty disclosed to those who bear its cost.

### TEXT 3: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 — The Underused Text and What It Costs to Ignore It

**~200 words**

The pastoral observation here is simple and significant: Alderwood does not build on this text, and the silence is formative.

Paul assumes women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. He regulates *how* they do it — not *whether* they do it. The pastoral implication is that women's public, Spirit-directed speech in the gathered church is not a concession Paul makes reluctantly; it is a practice he treats as ordinary enough to regulate.

[CLAIM: Paul's instructions in 1 Corinthians 11:5 presuppose that women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly as a normal practice, not an exceptional one]
[SOURCE: Lucy Peppiatt, *Unveiling Paul's Women*, Cascade, 2018]
[ARGUMENT: Peppiatt argues that Paul's regulation of women's prophesying assumes its legitimacy and that the head-covering discussion is intelligible only if women's public speech is already an established practice in Corinth]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The formation question: if Alderwood's women can preach on Sunday mornings — which the document affirms — why is 1 Corinthians 11:5 not a central text in the document's argument? Its absence teaches something. It suggests that women's public Spirit-directed speech is still, at some level, an accommodation rather than an assumption. The text, if centered, would form the congregation differently.

## The Reception Historian

# RECEPTION HISTORIAN — ROUND 4 CLOSING ARGUMENT

## Statement of Disciplinary Limits

Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time in response to forces that are not purely exegetical. It cannot show that a reading is therefore wrong. My discipline cannot establish what the texts mean — that is the biblical scholar's work. It cannot adjudicate whether the history of a reading counts as evidence against its validity — that is the hermeneutician's work. What I can do is put the current reading in its actual historical context and ask: do you know where this reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

---

## I. What My Discipline's Findings Require of the Church

Three findings from this debate are not optional for any honest church.

**First**, the church must know the history of its own reading. Alderwood's position is presented as what the Bible teaches. This debate has demonstrated — across every discipline — that it is what the Bible has been made to teach by a specific interpretive tradition with a traceable genealogy. The Vulgate rendered *authenteō* as *dominari* for fifteen centuries. Junia was female in every patristic source and was masculinized without textual warrant beginning in the thirteenth century. The creation-order argument was applied to marriage and domestic life by Augustine, not to church office. The specific configuration Alderwood holds — elder restriction grounded in Genesis 2 sequence and 1 Timothy 2:12 read as prohibiting women from the elder-specific function of authoritative teaching — crystallized in the late twentieth century.

[CLAIM: The specific application of Genesis 2 creation-order arguments to church office restriction, as distinct from marital ordering, is a distinctively modern evangelical development]
[SOURCE: Beth Allison Barr, *The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos Press, 2021]
[ARGUMENT: The move from domestic application (patristic) to ecclesial application (modern complementarian) is a traceable historical shift, not a continuous tradition]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This does not make the reading wrong.
