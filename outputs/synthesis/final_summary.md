## Debate Summary

**How the disciplines evolved.** The Biblical Scholar entered with a stated commitment to canonical breadth and honest acknowledgment of lexical ambiguity, and held that position consistently—sharpening it in Round 3 by naming canonical adjudication as a deliberate hermeneutical method rather than a neutral exegetical finding. The Reception Historian moved from documenting genealogy to making a more precise structural argument: the pattern across patristic reception was not ignorance of women's ministry evidence but *recognition followed by deliberate non-extension*, a finding that complicated both sides. The Hermeneutician's contribution sharpened progressively into a single, unanswered demand: supply a principled distinction between the creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 and the structurally identical appeal in 1 Corinthians 11:8–9, or acknowledge that something other than the stated principle is doing the hermeneutical work. The Systematician moved from cataloguing three models of eldership to identifying a specific internal incoherence in Alderwood's position: the document affirms that the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires male-and-female-together, then restricts the representative office to one sex—a tension the document generates but does not resolve. The Pastoral Theologian moved from raising formation concerns to grounding them in the Systematician's *imago Dei* argument, gaining theological architecture for an intuition it had carried without sufficient doctrinal support. The Social Analyst made the most significant self-correction: acknowledging that Bourdieu's symbolic violence framework had been deployed asymmetrically and correcting it to apply equally to egalitarian convictions held under progressive cultural pressure.

**The two most significant tensions.** First, the consistency problem: no advocate—including those defending restriction—supplied a principled account of why creation-order grounding marks a transcultural norm in 1 Timothy 2 but not in 1 Corinthians 11. This gap was identified in Round 1, named as the debate's central methodological problem in Round 2, and remained open at the close. Second, the authority question: different disciplines were using "authority" to mean different things—lexical, ontological, functional, relational, formative, social—and the debate never achieved genuine contact on what kind of authority the elder exercises and where it comes from, which the Systematician correctly identified as the prior question on which the surface debate depends.

**What the debate clarified.** The Vulgate's *dominari* rendering of *authenteō*—the Western church's standard for fifteen centuries—supports the negative reading of the term, a finding that reframes the lexical dispute as a departure from tradition rather than a recovery of it. The "exception" framing applied to Deborah and Huldah is an interpretive imposition without textual warrant. Alderwood's position is more internally incoherent than it appears, not because its conclusion is demonstrably wrong, but because its stated hermeneutical principle does not survive the consistency test and its theological anthropology creates pressure against its ecclesiological conclusion.

**What remained unresolved.** Whether Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 establishes a transcultural norm or a culturally embedded argument cannot be settled without a prior confessional commitment about the nature of apostolic argumentation that no discipline in this debate is positioned to supply alone. Whether pastoral formation evidence can function as load-bearing in contested exegetical situations—rather than merely supplementary—depends on a doctrine of ecclesial authority and scriptural normativity that the debate surfaced but did not resolve.

**Takeaway.** This debate demonstrated that the standard complementarian and egalitarian positions are both more methodologically vulnerable than their proponents typically acknowledge, and that the questions they have not asked—about hermeneutical consistency, ecclesiological foundations, and social genealogy—are more decisive than the questions they have.
