# SYSTEMATIC/ECCLESIOLOGICAL THEOLOGIAN
## Pre-Debate Position Paper: Women, Authority, and the Church

---

### STATEMENT OF DISCIPLINARY LIMITS (read aloud — maximum 150 words)

Systematics can clarify what is at stake and expose hidden assumptions. It can ask what doctrine of the church, ministry, and authority is actually implied by a given position on women's leadership — and whether that doctrine is coherent and defensible. What it cannot do alone: determine the meaning of specific texts or the weight of historical evidence. A beautiful systematic account of new creation ecclesiology means nothing if it cannot show exegetical grounding. My conclusions must be tested against what Scripture actually says — the biblical scholar's work — and what the church has actually done — the reception historian's work. Systematic theology that ignores inconvenient textual or historical evidence is not systematic theology; it is rationalization with theological vocabulary.

---

### I. DISCIPLINARY METHOD AND CORE ASSUMPTIONS

My method is doctrinal analysis from first principles. I ask: what theological doctrines are actually operative in a given position, whether or not they are named? I then test those doctrines for internal coherence and for coherence with the broader theological commitments the position claims to hold.

Traditions — Baptist, Anglican, Reformed, Catholic, Pentecostal, Orthodox — are resources I draw on as evidence of how serious theologians have answered underlying questions. They are not constraints. Where traditions disagree, that disagreement is data about the genuine difficulty of the underlying question, not a reason to prefer any one answer.

My core assumption is that doctrinal positions are not free-floating. A claim about who may hold authority in the church implies a claim about what the church is, what authority is, and where it comes from. These prior questions must be made explicit before the surface debate can be resolved.

### II. ENGAGEMENT WITH THE ALDERWOOD DOCUMENT

**What Alderwood does well:** The document is admirable in its pastoral generosity. It correctly identifies that the debate is not between those who affirm women's dignity and those who do not. Its reframing — "What may a non-elder do?" rather than "What may a woman do?" — is ecclesiologically significant: it locates the restriction in an office rather than in a person's sex as such. This is a more defensible position than traditions that restrict women from all teaching or leadership.

Alderwood also correctly acknowledges that *ezer* carries no connotation of inferiority, and that the Spirit distributes gifts without gendered restriction.

[CLAIM: Alderwood's document states that "the Spirit distributes all gifts freely to both men and women" and that women can and should teach Scripture and lead ministries of all ages and both genders]
[SOURCE: Alderwood Community Church, "What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership," internal document, undated]
[ARGUMENT: Alderwood's position is that the restriction is located in the office of elder, not in women's giftedness or competence]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**What Alderwood leaves unresolved — the central ecclesiological problem:**

Alderwood defines the elder as the "spiritual father of the church family." This is a relational and familial description. But the document does not ask a prior question that its own argument requires: *what kind of authority does the elder exercise, and where does that authority come from?*

Three distinct models of eldership are available in the tradition, and they generate different conclusions:

1. **The ontological model**: Ordination confers a distinct status on the elder. The elder is not merely someone who performs certain functions but someone who *is* something different by virtue of the office. This is broadly the Catholic, Orthodox, and some Anglican understanding. On this model, gender might belong to the ontological status conferred.

2. **The functional model**: The elder performs certain functions — teaching, guarding doctrine, shepherding. The office is defined by what it does. On this model, the question becomes whether there is a functional reason why women cannot perform these functions — and that functional reason must be specified and defended.

3. **The relational model**: The elder is the community's recognized shepherd — the person to whom the community looks for guidance and in whom it recognizes authority. Authority is recognized from below as much as conferred from above. This is closer to some Baptist and congregationalist understandings.

[CLAIM: Miroslav Volf argues that in free-church ecclesiology, the church is constituted from below by the gathered community, and that authority is recognized rather than hierarchically conferred]
[SOURCE: Miroslav Volf, *After Our Likeness: The Church as the Image of the Trinity*, Eerdmans, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Volf's trinitarian ecclesiology grounds church structure in the mutual relations of the Trinity rather than in hierarchical delegation, with implications for how authority is distributed]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Alderwood appears to operate with a functional account** — elders guard and teach doctrine, they delegate teaching to non-elders — but its creation-order argument implies something closer to a *representational* account: the elder represents God's authority to the community, and that representation requires maleness because God has consistently appointed male leaders. These are not the same argument, and they are in tension.

If the account is purely functional, then the question is whether women can perform the functions. Alderwood's own document suggests they largely can — women can teach Scripture, lead mixed-gender groups, and serve as pastors. The functional restriction then collapses to a narrow residual: the specific function of "guarding doctrine" as an elder. This requires a much more precise argument than Alderwood provides.

If the account is representational, then we need a doctrine of representation: what is it about maleness that is required for representing divine authority to the community? This brings us directly to the most contested theological territory in this debate.

### III. THREE SIGNIFICANT FINDINGS

**Finding 1: The ESS/EFS debate and the grounding of authority structures**

The most theologically serious argument for male-only eldership is the claim that the eternal relation of the Son to the Father — a relation of authority and submission — establishes a model for human authority structures, including in the church. Wayne Grudem and Bruce Ware have argued versions of this position.

[CLAIM: Wayne Grudem has argued that the eternal subordination of the Son to the Father provides a model for male-female authority relations]
[SOURCE: Wayne Grudem, *Systematic Theology*, Zondervan, 1994]
[ARGUMENT: Grudem connects the eternal procession and submission of the Son to the Father with the proper ordering of male-female relations in family and church]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Kevin Giles has argued at length that this position — Eternal Subordination of the Son (ESS) — represents a departure from Nicene orthodoxy. The Nicene tradition insists on the full equality of the divine persons in power and authority; the Son's submission in the economy of salvation is temporal and voluntary, not an eternal ontological feature of the immanent Trinity.

[CLAIM: Kevin Giles argues that the eternal functional subordination of the Son is inconsistent with Nicene trinitarianism, which affirms the full equality of the divine persons]
[SOURCE: Kevin Giles, *The Trinity and Subordinationism*, IVP Academic, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Giles contends that using ESS to ground human authority structures imports a subordinationist heresy into trinitarian doctrine, and that Nicene orthodoxy cannot support the complementarian use of the Trinity]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This matters decisively for systematic theology. If the strongest theological grounding for male-only eldership requires a doctrine of the Trinity that departs from Nicene orthodoxy, that is a serious problem — not merely an exegetical dispute, but a question about whether the position is theologically coherent.

**Finding 2: The imago Dei and the representational model**

Amy Peeler's work raises a question that cuts across the representational argument. If the fullness of the *imago Dei* is expressed only in the partnership of male and female together — a point Alderwood itself affirms — then a representational account of eldership faces a structural problem: an all-male eldership does not fully represent the God whose image is most completely expressed in male-and-female-together.

[CLAIM: Amy Peeler argues that the gender of God as revealed in Scripture is not straightforwardly male, and that the masculine language for God in Scripture does not straightforwardly ground male-only representation in ministry]
[SOURCE: Amy Peeler, *Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022]
[ARGUMENT: Peeler contends that the incarnation of the Son as male does not imply that maleness is necessary for representing divine authority, because the Son's maleness is not the primary ground of his representative role]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This does not settle the question, but it significantly complicates the representational argument. If the argument is that elders represent God to the community, and if Alderwood itself affirms that the fullness of the *imago Dei* requires both male and female, then the representational argument may actually require *including* women in eldership to represent God more fully — not excluding them.

**Finding 3: New creation ecclesiology and the church's eschatological vocation**

If the church is the community of the new creation — the first fruits of the age to come — then its structures should embody the reality inaugurated in Christ, not merely reflect the patterns of the present age.

[CLAIM: Jürgen Moltmann argues that the church's structure must be understood eschatologically — the church is a proleptic sign of the coming Kingdom, and its internal relations should anticipate the freedom of the new creation]
[SOURCE: Jürgen Moltmann, *The Church in the Power of the Spirit*, Fortress, 1977]
[ARGUMENT: Moltmann's pneumatological ecclesiology grounds church structure in the Spirit's gifts rather than in hierarchical office, with eschatological freedom as the normative horizon]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood's argument is oriented primarily toward the creation order and the pattern of redemptive history. A new creation ecclesiology asks whether that orientation is sufficient — whether the church's structures should be governed by the patterns of the old creation or by the inaugurated reality of the new.

### IV. AN INCONVENIENT FINDING

The most inconvenient finding for an egalitarian conclusion is that the church's dominant practice across most traditions and most of history has been male-only eldership and priesthood — and this is not easily explained as mere cultural accommodation.

[CLAIM: Paul Bradshaw's historical work on ordination rites in the early church shows that the restriction of priestly and episcopal ordination to men was near-universal in the early centuries]
[SOURCE: Paul Bradshaw, *Rites of Ordination in the Early Church*, Pueblo, 1990]
[ARGUMENT: Bradshaw's liturgical history documents that ordination rites were consistently restricted to men in early Christian practice, suggesting this was not merely a later imposition]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

A systematic theology that ignores this datum is not systematic theology. The question of *why* this was the case — whether it reflects theological conviction, cultural assumption, or both — is a question for the reception historian. But the datum itself must be reckoned with.

---

### V. INITIAL POSITION ON THE GOVERNING QUESTION

Alderwood's position is more defensible than it first appears, but it rests on a theological foundation it has not examined. The document defines the elder as "spiritual father" — a relational description — but requires a representational argument to ground the male restriction. These are different accounts of eldership, and they generate different ecclesiologies.

The most pressing question this debate must answer is not exegetical but doctrinal: *what kind of authority does the elder exercise, and where does it come from?* Until that question is answered, the surface debate about specific texts cannot be resolved. My discipline's task in this debate is to make that question unavoidable.
