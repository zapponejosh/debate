# BIBLICAL SCHOLAR'S PRE-DEBATE POSITION PAPER
## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

---

### STATEMENT OF LIMITS (read aloud — maximum 150 words)

Exegesis can establish what texts mean within their canonical context. It can identify where translation choices carry theological weight and insist that those choices be defended. It can ask what the whole-Bible pattern looks like when we resist focusing only on the texts most convenient to our preferred conclusion. What it cannot do alone: determine how much weight a first-century cultural situation should carry in a twenty-first century church; build an ecclesiology from its findings without assistance from systematic theology; adjudicate between two exegetically defensible readings without a hermeneutical principle that itself requires defense. I need the hermeneutician to adjudicate method, the reception historian to test whether my readings have been consistent, and the systematician to build something from what I find.

---

### I. DISCIPLINARY METHOD AND CORE ASSUMPTIONS

My method is canonical exegesis with full attention to original languages. I operate with the following commitments:

First, texts must be read in their literary context before being read in their canonical context. A verse does not mean something in Romans 16 that it cannot mean in Romans 16.

Second, translation choices are arguments, not neutral renderings. When a Greek word is contested — *authenteō*, *kephalē*, *ezer*, *diakonos*, *prostatis* — the translation selected by a commentary or church document is already a position in the debate. I require those choices to be defended lexically, not merely asserted.

Third, the Old Testament carries full canonical weight. The gravitational pull of the "problem texts" in the Pauline corpus — 1 Timothy 2, 1 Corinthians 14 — has produced a distorted debate. Genesis 1–2, Judges 4–5, Joel 2, and the Psalms belong to this inquiry as much as the Pastoral Epistles do.

Fourth, genuine scholarly contestation must be acknowledged as such. Where two careful exegetes read the same Greek differently and both have lexical warrant, I will not pretend the matter is settled.

### II. ENGAGEMENT WITH THE ALDERWOOD DOCUMENT

**What my discipline affirms:**

Alderwood's treatment of *ezer* (Genesis 2:18) is better than most church documents.

[CLAIM: The Hebrew word *ezer* is used of God in contexts of military and saving help, not subordinate assistance]
[SOURCE: John Goldingay, *Old Testament Theology*, Vol. 2, IVP Academic, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Goldingay notes that *ezer* language applied to God carries connotations of indispensable strength, not secondary status]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood correctly refuses to load "helper" with inferiority. That is good lexical work.

Alderwood's acknowledgment that 1 Corinthians 11:5 creates genuine tension with 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 is honest and necessary. Almost no church document at this level of pastoral accessibility engages that tension directly.

Alderwood's distinction between elder and pastor in Appendix 2, and its willingness to allow women the title "pastor" while restricting eldership, represents a serious attempt to read the New Testament's office-language carefully rather than collapsing all terms into one.

**What my discipline questions:**

*The Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles as straightforward precedents.* Alderwood states: "After Genesis 2, God consistently calls men to fill the roles of the highest level of spiritual authority. The Levitical priesthood… God only appoints male kings of Israel… Jesus called 12 men to form his inner circle."

This argument requires far more exegetical work than Alderwood provides. The Levitical priesthood was a typological institution whose specific requirements — tribe, gender, physical wholeness — are not straightforwardly binding on the church, which the New Testament explicitly describes as a "royal priesthood" of all believers (1 Peter 2:9). The selection of twelve male apostles corresponding to the twelve tribes of Israel is a typological gesture toward Israel's reconstitution, not a gender policy.

[CLAIM: The twelve apostles were chosen as a symbolic reconstitution of Israel's twelve tribes, which is a typological function, not a gender paradigm]
[SOURCE: N.T. Wright, "Women's Service in the Church: The Biblical Basis," NTWrightPage.com]
[ARGUMENT: Wright argues the maleness of the Twelve is inseparable from their Jewishness and their function as the new patriarchs of Israel; applying it as a gender rule for church leadership requires a hermeneutical step the text does not authorize]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Alderwood makes this argument without that hermeneutical step. My discipline requires it to be supplied.

*The creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13.* Alderwood asserts that Paul "appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations" and that "whatever the principle is that Paul is applying, it is a principle that transcends individual cultures." This is a significant hermeneutical claim presented as an exegetical finding. But the claim that creation-order appeals in Paul are always trans-cultural is contested.

[CLAIM: Paul uses creation-order arguments in 1 Corinthians 11 to ground a requirement for head coverings, which virtually no complementarian applies as universally binding]
[SOURCE: Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, NICNT, Eerdmans, 1987]
[ARGUMENT: Fee argues that 1 Corinthians 11's appeal to creation order does not settle whether the practice grounded in that appeal is culturally conditioned, because Paul uses the same argumentative structure for practices most interpreters treat as culturally specific]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

Alderwood cannot simply assert that creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2:12 universally normative without explaining why the same logic does not bind churches to head coverings. This is a gap in the document's argument, not a refutation of its conclusion.

**Questions my discipline opens that Alderwood does not ask:**

What does Deborah's role in Judges 4–5 establish canonically? Alderwood does not mention her. The text presents her as judge, prophet, and military commander without narrative apology, and the Song of Deborah (Judges 5) is among the oldest texts in the Hebrew Bible. Is she an exception? The text does not say so. Is she a precedent? That requires argument. But she cannot simply be omitted from a canonical inquiry.

What does Romans 16 establish about women's actual ministry roles in the Pauline churches? Alderwood mentions Priscilla in passing but does not engage Phoebe's double designation or Junia's apostolic identification systematically.

### III. INITIAL POSITION ON THE GOVERNING QUESTION

The canonical evidence does not unambiguously establish either a universal male-only leadership norm or an undifferentiated egalitarianism. What it does establish, with high confidence, is more complex than either standard position acknowledges:

The Old Testament presents women in prophetic, judicial, and teaching roles without narrative condemnation. The New Testament presents women as deacons, apostles (in some sense), prophets, and teachers. The Pauline restriction texts are real, are grounded in creation-order appeals, and cannot be dismissed as purely local without a hermeneutical argument that is not itself exegetical. But those same texts are lexically contested at precisely the points that determine their scope.

My initial position is that the exegetical evidence is genuinely inconclusive at the level of church polity, that the strongest exegetical case for restriction operates at the level of a specific authoritative-teaching function rather than all leadership, and that the case for the elder restriction specifically depends heavily on how one resolves the *authenteō* question and the canonical weight of the Levitical/apostolic precedents — neither of which is as settled as Alderwood's document implies.

### IV. THREE MOST SIGNIFICANT FINDINGS

**Finding 1: The lexical evidence for *authenteō* is genuinely disputed.**

[CLAIM: The Greek verb *authenteō* in 1 Timothy 2:12 is a hapax legomenon in the New Testament and its meaning in the first century is debated among specialists]
[SOURCE: Philip Payne, *Man and Woman, One in Christ*, Zondervan, 2009]
[ARGUMENT: Payne surveys the lexical evidence and argues that *authenteō* carries a negative or domineering connotation in its pre-second-century usage, which would change the force of Paul's prohibition significantly]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Al Wolters' 2006 JETS article argues for a more neutral or positive meaning of *authenteō*, supporting a general authority prohibition]
[SOURCE: Al Wolters, "A Semantic Study of *authentes* and its Derivatives," *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Wolters surveys a broader range of cognates and argues the term does not carry an inherently negative force]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Both positions have serious lexical warrant. This is not a case where one side has the evidence and the other is special pleading.

**Finding 2: Romans 16 establishes that women held significant ministry roles in the Pauline churches, including roles described with titles Paul uses for men.**

[CLAIM: Phoebe is designated *diakonos* in Romans 16:1, the same term used of male deacons and of Paul himself]
[SOURCE: Nijay Gupta, *Tell Her Story*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Gupta argues that the masculine form *diakonos* applied to Phoebe is a substantive designation of ministry role, not a diminutive, and that translating it "servant" when applied to Phoebe while rendering it "deacon" when applied to men is a translation choice that requires defense]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Junia in Romans 16:7 is most naturally read as a woman described as "outstanding among the apostles"]
[SOURCE: Richard Bauckham, *Gospel Women*, Eerdmans, 2002]
[ARGUMENT: Bauckham argues that Junia is an unambiguously female Latin name, that the phrase *en tois apostolois* most naturally means she is numbered among the apostles, and that the attempt to read her as male is driven by theological commitment rather than linguistic evidence]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Finding 3: Joel 2 / Acts 2 establishes that the Spirit's prophetic gift is explicitly poured out on daughters and female servants without qualification.**

[CLAIM: Peter's Pentecost speech cites Joel 2 as fulfilled, including the prophecy that "your daughters will prophesy" and "my female servants" will receive the Spirit]
[SOURCE: Acts 2:17–18, citing Joel 2:28–29]
[ARGUMENT: The canonical function of this citation is to establish that the new-covenant community is characterized by the Spirit's prophetic activity distributed without gender restriction — which creates exegetical pressure on any restriction of women's public speech that must be accounted for]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

### V. A FINDING INCONVENIENT FOR THE CONCLUSION I MIGHT PREFER

The creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not easily dismissed. Paul does not appeal to Ephesian culture or to a local problem. He appeals to Adam being formed first, then Eve. This is the same kind of grounding he uses in 1 Corinthians 11 for head coverings — but the parallel cuts both ways. The honest exegetical finding is that Paul believed creation order had normative force for community practice. Whether that normative force extends to all churches in all times requires a hermeneutical argument, but the exegete must not pretend Paul is doing something other than what he is doing. He is grounding a behavioral norm in creation. That must be engaged, not dissolved.

[CLAIM: The creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 represents Paul grounding a community practice in protological order, not in local circumstances]
[SOURCE: Thomas Schreiner, in Andreas Köstenberger and Thomas Schreiner, eds., *Women in the Church*, 3rd ed., Crossway, 2016]
[ARGUMENT: Schreiner argues that creation-order grounding in Paul signals trans-cultural intent, and that egalitarian readings must account for why this grounding does not function as Paul apparently intends it]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

I find Schreiner's reading of the rhetorical function of v.13 more exegetically careful than many egalitarian dismissals of it. What I cannot determine from exegesis alone is whether that grounding settles the question of universal application. That requires the hermeneutician.
