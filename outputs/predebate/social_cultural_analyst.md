# Social and Cultural Analysis: A Pre-Debate Position Paper

## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*

**Submitted by: The Social and Cultural Analyst**

---

### Statement of Limits

Before proceeding, I must state what my discipline can and cannot do. Social analysis can show that a reading emerged in a particular social context and serves particular social interests. It cannot show that the reading is therefore wrong — texts can have genuine transcultural authority even when interpreted in socially situated ways, and even readings that serve the interests of the powerful can be correct. If all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them. My discipline's greatest risk is explaining everything and thereby explaining nothing. I need the biblical scholar to establish what the texts actually say and the hermeneutician to adjudicate how much social location counts as evidence against a reading's validity. What I bring is this: a position that cannot examine its own social conditioning, or that claims to have transcended social situatedness while everyone else is trapped by it, has not yet done its intellectual homework.

---

### I. Disciplinary Method and Core Assumptions

I bring two complementary lenses to this debate. The first is ancient: social-scientific analysis of the first-century Mediterranean world — its honor/shame dynamics, patronage networks, household structures, and the specific social locations of early Christian communities in cities like Ephesus and Corinth. The second is contemporary: the sociology of modern religious institutions, with particular attention to how gender ideology is constructed, maintained, and transmitted in American evangelical culture.

My core methodological assumption is that every text is written and read inside a social world. Ignoring that social world produces misreading in two directions simultaneously: it makes culturally specific instructions into universal principles, and it misses the ways texts themselves subverted the social worlds in which they first appeared. Social location is not a verdict on a text's authority. It is evidence that must be weighed.

---

### II. Engaging the Alderwood Document

**What my discipline affirms:**

Alderwood makes a genuinely important concession in its commitment section: *"Much of what has kept women from flourishing and using their gifts within the church has not been the theology of the church but has been the sin of sexism and discrimination that is the result of the Fall."* This is sociologically honest. It acknowledges that the social history of gender restriction in churches has been substantially driven by forces independent of — and often contrary to — the theological rationale offered for restriction. Alderwood is right to name this, and right to commit to opposing it.

The document also demonstrates unusual self-awareness in its taxonomy of positions, acknowledging that complementarianism spans a spectrum and that Alderwood's position disagrees with more restrictive complementarians. This is sociologically accurate.

**What my discipline questions:**

The same concession that I affirm opens a gap that Alderwood does not sufficiently explore. If the theology and the social forces can be separated in the *application* of the position — sexism is sin, genuine restriction is theology — can we be equally confident they can be separated in the *formation* of the position? The document does not ask this question. My discipline must.

Alderwood also states that Paul's reasoning in 1 Timothy 2 "appeals to the order of Creation and the Fall, not to any cultural considerations," and therefore "the principle transcends individual cultures." This is a hermeneutical claim that my discipline cannot adjudicate alone — but it is a claim that requires social-scientific scrutiny, because the same move (grounding a culturally specific instruction in a creation-order argument) appears throughout the household codes, and the question of whether those grounds lift the instruction out of its social world or whether the grounds themselves are socially shaped is precisely what is at issue.

**What questions my discipline opens that Alderwood does not ask:**

Three questions Alderwood does not raise:

1. When did the specific configuration of complementarianism that shaped this document emerge, and what forces produced it?
2. What is the social world of Ephesus and Corinth, and how does that world bear on the instructions addressed to those communities?
3. How does gender restriction function sociologically in religious institutions, independent of the theological rationale offered for it?

---

### III. Initial Position on the Governing Question

My discipline does not determine whether women should serve as elders. What it does determine is this: the current complementarian position, as institutionalized in American evangelicalism, emerged from a specific cultural and political moment in the late twentieth century, was shaped by forces that had nothing to do with exegesis, and cannot be cleanly separated from those forces by sincerity of belief. This does not make the position wrong. It means the position has not yet passed the test of social self-awareness, and a position that has not passed that test should be held with more epistemic humility than it typically is.

### IV. Three Most Significant Findings

**Finding One: The Genealogy of Modern Complementarianism**

[CLAIM: The modern complementarian movement, including the Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood, emerged in the late 1980s in direct response to second-wave feminism and in close entanglement with American conservative political culture, not primarily from a fresh engagement with biblical texts.]
[SOURCE: Kristin Kobes Du Mez, *Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: Du Mez documents that evangelical gender ideology in twentieth-century America was inseparable from specific cultural and political projects — the construction of militant Christian masculinity, Cold War anxieties about male leadership, and the culture wars of the 1980s and 1990s. The CBMW was founded in 1987; the *Danvers Statement* followed in 1988. These were reactive documents, not the product of disinterested exegesis.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: The term "complementarian" itself was introduced by the Piper/Grudem project as a rebranding of a position that needed to distinguish itself from both egalitarianism and what was perceived as the discredited language of male supremacy.]
[SOURCE: Kristin Kobes Du Mez, *Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020]
[ARGUMENT: The naming of a movement is itself a social act — it constructs the terms of the debate and positions its adherents as the reasonable middle.]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

The Alderwood document cites Piper, Grudem, Schreiner, and the CBMW as proponents of its own position's category. These are not simply exegetes — they are movement architects operating within a specific social moment. That does not make them wrong. It means their work cannot be read as socially neutral.

**Finding Two: The Social World of Ephesus**

[CLAIM: The city of Ephesus in the first century was home to the dominant cult of Artemis, whose temple was one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world, and whose religious economy gave women unusual cultic prominence and public religious authority.]
[SOURCE: Sandra Glahn, *Nobody's Mother: Artemis of the Ephesians in Antiquity and the New Testament*, IVP Academic, 2023]
[ARGUMENT: Glahn argues that understanding the Artemis cult and its specific social pressures on the Ephesian church is essential for reading 1 Timothy. The community Paul addresses was navigating a religious environment in which female religious authority was the dominant cultural norm, not the countercultural position — which significantly complicates any reading of 1 Timothy 2 as a transcultural principle.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: In the first-century Mediterranean world, the oikos (household) was the basic unit of social organization, and the household codes of the New Testament letters engaged directly with Greco-Roman conventions for ordering the household, which included the subordination of wives, children, and slaves to the paterfamilias.]
[SOURCE: Bruce Malina and Richard Rohrbaugh, *Social-Science Commentary on the Letters of Paul*, Fortress, 2006]
[ARGUMENT: Malina and Rohrbaugh argue that Paul's household instructions are intelligible only within the honor/shame framework of Mediterranean social life, where a household that violated conventional order brought shame on its members and on the community associated with it.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Finding Three: Symbolic Violence and Internalized Hierarchy**

[CLAIM: Pierre Bourdieu's concept of "symbolic violence" describes the process by which hierarchies are maintained not through overt coercion but through the internalization of the hierarchy by those subject to it, such that subordination is experienced as natural, willed, or spiritually appropriate.]
[SOURCE: Pierre Bourdieu, *Masculine Domination*, Stanford University Press, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Bourdieu argues that the most durable forms of domination are those in which the dominated participate in their own domination through categories of perception and appreciation that are themselves the product of the domination. The sociologically significant question for this debate is not whether women in complementarian churches believe their restriction is God's will — many sincerely do — but whether that sincere belief is itself evidence that the restriction is just.]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This framework is underused in this debate. Its application is not that sincerely held beliefs are false. It is that sincerity cannot serve as its own validation, and that the sociology of how a restriction comes to feel natural is itself evidence that must be examined.

---

### V. A Finding Inconvenient for My Preferred Conclusion

My discipline is most comfortable with the conclusion that complementarian restriction is a social construct that serves institutional power. But the cross-cultural data complicates this.

[CLAIM: Churches in the Global South — in contexts with no exposure to the American evangelical culture-war moment and no entanglement with CBMW — have independently arrived at male-leadership structures, often reading the same Pauline texts in similar ways.]
[SOURCE: Susan Hylen, *Women in the New Testament World*, Oxford University Press, 2019]
[ARGUMENT: Hylen notes that the diversity of women's roles in the New Testament world itself suggests that early Christian communities negotiated these questions differently in different contexts — which cuts both ways. It means restriction is not the only historically attested pattern, but it also means restriction cannot be explained entirely by American culture-war genealogy.]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

If complementarian restriction were purely a product of late twentieth-century American evangelical culture, we would not expect to find it independently reproduced across genuinely different social worlds. That it does appear cross-culturally does not make it theologically correct — but it does mean my genealogical argument, while significant, is not sufficient. The hermeneutician and the biblical scholar must carry weight that my discipline alone cannot bear.
