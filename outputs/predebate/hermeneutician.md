# Hermeneutical Position Paper
## Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry
### Prepared by the Hermeneutician

---

## Statement of Limits (read aloud at debate opening — 150 words maximum)

Hermeneutics can expose inconsistency and interrogate method. It can show that the same principle is applied selectively without saying whether the principle should be applied universally or abandoned. What it cannot do alone: determine which hermeneutical approach is ultimately correct — that requires theological and philosophical commitments that go beyond hermeneutics itself. I can show that the creation-order argument in 1 Timothy 2:13 is not applied consistently elsewhere. I cannot tell you from hermeneutics alone whether it should be applied universally or treated as culturally situated. For that I need the systematician's doctrine of scriptural authority and the biblical scholar's textual work. My discipline is a tool for honest inquiry, not a position in the debate.

---

## I. Disciplinary Method and Core Assumptions

Hermeneutics asks the question that every other discipline assumes it has already answered: *How do we move from what a text meant to what it means?* This is not a skeptical question. It is a foundational one. Every interpreter — Alderwood's elders, the biblical scholar presenting at this debate, the egalitarian and the complementarian — brings a set of interpretive decisions to the text before the text is read. My discipline's task is to name those decisions, test them for consistency, and evaluate them on their own terms.

[CLAIM: E.D. Hirsch argued that meaning is determinate and lodged in the author's original intention, and that interpretation's goal is to recover that intention]
[SOURCE: E.D. Hirsch, *Validity in Interpretation*, Yale University Press, 1967]
[ARGUMENT: Hirsch distinguished "meaning" (the author's intended content, stable and reproducible) from "significance" (the text's relevance for later readers, variable), arguing that only the former grounds valid interpretation]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Hans-Georg Gadamer argued that meaning arises not from authorial intention alone but from the "fusion of horizons" between text and interpreter, making the reader's situation constitutive of meaning rather than merely incidental to it]
[SOURCE: Hans-Georg Gadamer, *Truth and Method*, various editions]
[ARGUMENT: Gadamer's "wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewusstsein" (historically effected consciousness) holds that no interpreter stands outside their tradition, and that understanding is always a productive event, not mere reproduction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

These two positions — Hirsch's authorial intentionalism and Gadamer's fusion of horizons — represent the poles between which every working hermeneutic in this debate operates. Recognizing this is not relativism. It is intellectual honesty about what is actually at stake.

[CLAIM: Kevin Vanhoozer proposed a middle position in which the text's communicative act, rather than either bare authorial psychology or reader experience, anchors meaning]
[SOURCE: Kevin Vanhoozer, *Is There a Meaning in This Text?*, Zondervan, 1998]
[ARGUMENT: Drawing on speech-act theory, Vanhoozer argues that texts perform illocutionary acts that are recoverable and normative, preserving determinacy without collapsing into pure intentionalism]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

My working assumption is that meaning is constrained — texts are not infinitely malleable — but that the application of ancient texts to contemporary communities requires methodological decisions that are themselves contestable and must be defended, not assumed.

## II. Engagement with the Alderwood Document

### What Alderwood gets right, hermeneutically

Alderwood deserves credit for one hermeneutical move that many comparable documents avoid: it does not pretend that interpretation is simply "reading what the text says." It acknowledges that 1 Timothy 2:12 is difficult, that 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 cannot be applied literally without contradiction, and that the phrase "husband of one wife" in 1 Timothy 3:2 is doing something more nuanced than a simple gender restriction. This is hermeneutical honesty. The document is also right that Paul's appeal to creation order in 1 Timothy 2:13 is significant — it is a feature of the text that any hermeneutic must account for, not dismiss.

### The consistency problem: the document's sharpest vulnerability

Alderwood argues that Paul's creation-order appeal in 1 Timothy 2:13 ("Adam was formed first, then Eve") makes the prohibition on women's authoritative teaching transcultural — grounded not in Ephesian social custom but in the structure of creation itself.

[CLAIM: Alderwood's document states that Paul's appeal to creation order rather than cultural considerations means the prohibition "is a principle that transcends individual cultures"]
[SOURCE: Alderwood Community Church, *What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership*, Appendix 1, 1 Timothy 2:11-14 section]
[ARGUMENT: The document uses Paul's creation-order grounding as the criterion distinguishing transcultural principle from culturally situated instruction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

This is a specific hermeneutical principle: *appeals to creation order mark transcultural norms*. It is a defensible principle. But it must be applied consistently.

[CLAIM: Paul grounds the head-covering instruction in 1 Corinthians 11:8-9 in creation order, citing that "man was not made from woman, but woman from man" and "woman was created for man"]
[SOURCE: 1 Corinthians 11:8-9, ESV]
[ARGUMENT: The creation-order rationale appears in the head-covering passage with equal explicitness to its appearance in 1 Timothy 2:13]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

The hermeneutical question is unavoidable: if creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2:12 transcultural, why does it not make head coverings equally transcultural? Alderwood almost certainly does not require head coverings. I do not say this to score a point — I say it because this is the consistency test, and it is the most important methodological challenge this debate must answer. The differential treatment of these two texts requires a principled explanation. Three options exist: (1) accept both as transcultural and require head coverings; (2) argue that the creation-order appeals function differently in each passage in ways the text itself supports; or (3) acknowledge that something other than creation-order grounding is doing the real hermeneutical work in both cases. Option 3 is the most honest starting point, and it requires naming what that something is.

### Questions the document does not ask

Alderwood does not name its hermeneutical method. It applies one, but it does not defend it. The questions it does not ask include: What is the criterion for distinguishing culturally accommodated instructions from transcultural principles, and is that criterion applied consistently? When two exegetically defensible readings of the same text yield opposite conclusions, what adjudicates between them? Is the community's discernment — in this case, Alderwood's elder body — a legitimate hermeneutical authority, and on what grounds?

## III. The Major Hermeneutical Options

[CLAIM: William Webb's "redemptive-movement hermeneutic" argues that the trajectory of Scripture — the direction of ethical movement from ancient Near Eastern context through the New Testament — rather than the static statements of any single text should govern ethical application]
[SOURCE: William Webb, *Slaves, Women and Homosexuals*, IVP Academic, 2001]
[ARGUMENT: Webb proposes eighteen criteria for identifying when a text's ethic is "frozen" at a culturally accommodated point versus expressing the full redemptive ideal, and argues that the NT's teaching on women, like its teaching on slavery, moves toward greater liberation without reaching the cultural destination]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

[CLAIM: Denny Burk and Thomas Schreiner criticized Webb's redemptive-movement hermeneutic as insufficiently anchored to the text's propositional content, arguing that it allows the interpreter's construction of a "trajectory" to override explicit apostolic instruction]
[SOURCE: Denny Burk and Thomas Schreiner, responses to Webb in *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*]
[ARGUMENT: The critics argue that Webb's method lacks adequate controls against the interpreter importing contemporary ethical intuitions as the endpoint of the "trajectory," effectively making cultural consensus the hermeneutical authority]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

This is a genuine methodological danger in Webb's framework, and I note it as such. The trajectory argument works by extrapolating beyond what the text says — which is both its strength (accounting for the slavery analogy) and its vulnerability (the extrapolation requires criteria that are themselves contestable).

[CLAIM: I. Howard Marshall proposed a more cautious version of the trajectory argument, arguing that the task of Scripture is not to give final answers but to stimulate and guide moral reasoning in ways that require going "beyond the Bible"]
[SOURCE: I. Howard Marshall, *Beyond the Bible: Moving from Scripture to Theology*, IVP Academic, 2004]
[ARGUMENT: Marshall's approach is more modest than Webb's, focusing on the direction and purpose of biblical instruction rather than constructing a multi-criteria extrapolation, making it less vulnerable to the charge of unconstrained reader-construction]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Marshall's version of the argument is hermeneutically more defensible than Webb's precisely because it is less systematized — it acknowledges the limits of the method rather than overclaiming its precision.

## IV. Most Significant Findings

**Finding 1: The consistency test reveals that the hermeneutical principle Alderwood applies to 1 Timothy 2 (creation-order grounding = transcultural norm) is not applied to 1 Corinthians 11. This is the debate's central methodological problem, and it is not resolved by either side.**

**Finding 2: The slavery analogy has genuine hermeneutical force.** The church went beyond the explicit teaching of the New Testament on slavery — which nowhere commands abolition — by appealing to something like a redemptive trajectory. If that move was legitimate, the question of whether the same move applies to gender cannot be dismissed. But the analogy has genuine limits: the NT contains no creation-order grounding for slavery, while it does contain such grounding for gender distinctions. This asymmetry is not decisive, but it is real.

**Finding 3: Pre-understanding is inescapable but not determinative.**

[CLAIM: Anthony Thiselton argued that the interpreter's "horizon" — their set of pre-understandings — inevitably shapes what they find in a text, but that the text's own horizon can challenge and reshape the interpreter's, making genuine understanding possible without claiming neutral objectivity]
[SOURCE: Anthony Thiselton, *The Two Horizons*, Eerdmans, 1980]
[ARGUMENT: Thiselton's appropriation of Gadamer for evangelical hermeneutics establishes that pre-understanding is a condition of interpretation, not a disqualification from it, but that honest interpretation requires the text to be allowed to interrogate the interpreter's assumptions]
[CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

Every advocate in this debate — including me — reads these texts through a horizon shaped by gender, ecclesial tradition, and cultural location. Acknowledging this is not relativism. It is the precondition of honest inquiry.

---

## V. The Inconvenient Finding

The finding most inconvenient for a hermeneutic that favors expanding women's roles is this: **the redemptive-movement hermeneutic, if applied consistently, does not stop where its proponents want it to stop.** Webb himself applies it to argue that the NT's teaching on homosexuality is *not* on a liberatory trajectory — but critics have noted that the same methodological apparatus could be used to reach the opposite conclusion.

[CLAIM: Webb's critics argue that the redemptive-movement hermeneutic lacks internal controls that would prevent its application from being extended to sexual ethics beyond what Webb intends]
[SOURCE: Denny Burk, response to Webb, *Journal of the Evangelical Theological Society*, 2003]
[ARGUMENT: If the criterion for moving beyond the text is a constructed trajectory toward greater human liberation or flourishing, the method provides no principled textual stopping point]
[CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

I find this criticism partially compelling. A hermeneutic that opens questions it cannot close with its own resources is a hermeneutic that requires supplementation from elsewhere — from systematic theology's doctrine of Scripture, from the community's discernment, from the biblical scholar's textual work. That is not a refutation of the method. It is an honest account of its limits. And it applies equally to the hermeneutic that freezes all creation-order texts as transcultural: that method also cannot adjudicate its own application without resources from outside itself.

This is precisely why I arrive third in the debate order.
