# Debate Planning Framework — Version 4
## *Women, Authority, and the Church: A Six-Discipline Inquiry*
### A Structured Multi-Party Debate for Seminary and Engaged Lay Audiences

---

## DESIGN PRINCIPLES

This debate is organized around a conviction: that the question of women in church leadership is not primarily a debate between two camps with pre-formed conclusions, but an inquiry that requires multiple disciplines to pursue honestly — and that the most important conversations happen *between* those disciplines, not within any single one.

The Alderwood Community Church document (*What Alderwood Teaches about Women in Church Leadership*) serves as the shared anchor text and launching pad. Every advocate begins by engaging it through their disciplinary lens, then moves beyond it into the broader questions their discipline opens up.

**Six advocates. Six disciplines. One shared question. No predetermined conclusions.**

Each advocate:
- Leads with a single discipline but is not confined to it
- Pursues their discipline's findings honestly, including where those findings are inconclusive or uncomfortable
- Engages the findings of the other five disciplines in genuine conversation
- Acknowledges the limits of what their discipline alone can establish
- Responds to the Alderwood document as a launching pad — affirming what their discipline affirms, questioning what it leaves unexamined, and opening what it leaves closed
- Arrives at a picture of what faithful church practice looks like from their vantage point, even if that picture is nuanced or admits more than one possible form

**All advocates are LLM-generated. All claims must be sourced. The moderator fact-checks in real time. See Rules of Evidence.**

---

## THE CENTRAL MOTION

> **"The Alderwood document represents a serious and good-faith attempt to read Scripture faithfully on the question of women in church leadership. What does each discipline affirm in that attempt, where does it find the reasoning incomplete, and where does it open questions Alderwood does not ask?"**

This is deliberately not a binary for/against motion. It is an investigative motion — one that treats Alderwood as a serious interlocutor rather than a target, and that requires each advocate to do constructive as well as critical work. The debate is not about defeating Alderwood. It is about going further than Alderwood goes, from six different directions simultaneously.

**The governing question underneath the motion:**
*What does each discipline's honest engagement with this question require of the church — exegetically, historically, theologically, hermeneutically, and pastorally?*

## THE SIX ADVOCATES

### Advocate 1 — The Biblical Scholar
**Disciplinary lead:** Exegesis and canonical theology
**Tagline:** *"The texts say more than the debate has heard — and less than both sides claim."*

**What this discipline brings:**
The biblical scholar works with the full canon — not just the Pauline "problem texts" — and brings linguistic, literary, and canonical tools to bear on what Scripture actually says. This advocate reads Hebrew and Greek, takes translation choices seriously, situates texts in their literary and canonical contexts, and asks what the whole-Bible pattern looks like when the camera pulls back from 1 Timothy 2. This advocate must resist the gravitational pull of the Pauline texts and give the OT its full canonical weight.

**Opening engagement with Alderwood:**
Alderwood handles several key texts with more care than most church documents. Its treatment of *ezer* takes the Hebrew seriously. Its acknowledgment of Priscilla's teaching role does not flinch. Its honest engagement with the rhetorical possibilities in 1 Corinthians 14 shows genuine exegetical attention. Its insistence that the creation narrative establishes *ezer* as "corresponding strength" rather than "assistant" is exegetically sound and distinguishes Alderwood from less careful complementarian readings. These are genuine strengths that the biblical scholar affirms.

Where the document needs more work from this discipline: it reads the OT almost entirely through a NT lens, treating the Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles as straightforward precedents without asking what kind of texts those are and what kind of authority they carry for the church. Its appeal to the "consistent pattern" of male authority from Genesis through the apostles requires more canonical argument than it receives. The OT question is larger than Alderwood acknowledges.

**Required ground this advocate must cover:**
- The full semantic range of *ezer* (Gen. 2) — what the word establishes and what it does not, including its use of God in the Psalms and Exodus
- The ANE creation context: what does creation sequence actually signal in ancient Near Eastern texts? Does order imply hierarchy, and how do we know?
- *Authenteo* (1 Tim. 2:12): the lexical evidence, the translation history from the 16th century through the present, and what the word's extreme rarity actually means for interpretation
- *Kephale* (head) in 1 Corinthians 11: source or authority? What does the Greek evidence actually support and what remains genuinely contested?
- The women Paul names in Romans 16: Phoebe (*diakonos*, *prostatis*), Junia (*en tois apostolois*), Priscilla named before Aquila — what do these designations establish and what is the burden of proof for reading them restrictively?
- Deborah, Miriam, Huldah, the daughters of Philip, Anna: what do female prophets and leaders in both Testaments establish canonically? Are they precedents, exceptions, or something more complex?
- The Levitical priesthood and the twelve apostles: binding patterns for church eldership, or typological institutions whose inner logic points elsewhere?
- Joel 2 / Acts 2: what does the Spirit's gift of prophecy to sons *and* daughters establish for church order?
- The whole-Bible trajectory: does Scripture move toward greater inclusion of women in leadership, or establish and maintain a consistent pattern of male authority at the highest level?

**Where this discipline reaches its limits:**
Exegesis can establish what texts mean within their canonical context. It cannot by itself determine how much weight a first-century cultural situation should carry in a twenty-first century church, or what ecclesiology should be built from its findings. The biblical scholar needs the hermeneutician to adjudicate between valid readings, the reception historian to test whether readings have been consistent, and the systematician to build an ecclesiology from what the texts establish.

### Advocate 2 — The Reception Historian
**Disciplinary lead:** Reception history and the history of interpretation
**Tagline:** *"What the church has done with these texts is itself a form of evidence — and it is more complicated than either side admits."*

**What this discipline brings:**
Reception history asks not what texts mean but what they have been made to mean — how they have functioned in real communities across time, what they have authorized and prohibited, and how interpretive traditions have been shaped by forces that are not purely exegetical. This advocate tracks the actual history of women's leadership and restriction in the church from the first century through the present, and asks what that history reveals about the texts and about us.

This is distinct from historical theology (which focuses on doctrinal development) and from church history in the survey sense. Reception history is specifically interested in: who interpreted these texts, in what contexts, with what interests, with what methods, and with what effects on real people and real communities.

**Opening engagement with Alderwood:**
Alderwood deserves credit for something many church documents on this topic fail to do: it engages specific texts with care rather than proof-texting, and it acknowledges that the church's treatment of women has often been sinful regardless of the theology. The document's willingness to name that gap between stated theology and lived practice is itself a form of historical honesty that reception history affirms.

Where the document is historically thin — in a specific and revealing way — is that its argument is almost entirely synchronic. It reads texts without a serious reckoning with how those texts have been read and used across two millennia. The document treats its own reading as though it emerges directly from Scripture rather than from a specific late twentieth-century evangelical interpretive tradition with a traceable genealogy. Reception history does not make this reading wrong. But it makes the confidence of the reading harder to sustain. The central question this discipline puts to Alderwood is: do you know where your reading came from, and does knowing that change anything?

**Required ground this advocate must cover:**
- Women in the earliest Christian communities: what does the actual evidence from the first and second centuries show? Pliny's letter to Trajan mentioning female ministers (*ministrae*), the Didache, the letters of Ignatius, female deacons in the East
- The patristic tradition in full: what did Chrysostom, Jerome, Origen, Tertullian, and Augustine actually say about these texts — including the parts the modern debate does not cite?
- Medieval women's leadership: abbesses with quasi-episcopal authority, women mystics exercising public teaching authority, Hildegard of Bingen, Margery Kempe, the Beguine movement — what does this record reveal about the relationship between official restriction and actual practice?
- The evidence of ordained women: the epigraphic and documentary evidence for women presbyters, deacons, and bishops in the early Western and Eastern church (Madigan & Osiek; Gary Macy)
- The Reformation's effect: did the Reformation expand or contract women's roles in the church, and why? The domestication of women's ministry in Luther and Calvin; the loss of the convent as a space for women's authority
- The nineteenth century missionary movement: women exercising apostolic roles on the mission field while restricted at home — what does this inconsistency reveal about the relationship between doctrine and practice?
- The twentieth century complementarian movement: when did the current interpretive framework emerge, what cultural and political forces shaped it, and how did the translation of *authenteo* shift between the 1970s and the 1990s? (Janette Hassey on women's preaching in early American evangelicalism)
- The Global South and majority world church: how do churches outside the Western evangelical tradition read these texts, and what does their practice reveal about the relationship between social location and interpretation?

**Where this discipline reaches its limits:**
Reception history can show that a reading has a history, has served certain interests, and has changed over time. It cannot show that the reading is therefore wrong — texts can be misread consistently without that making the misreading correct. Nor can reception history alone establish what the texts mean. This advocate needs the biblical scholar to establish what the texts actually say, the hermeneutician to adjudicate how much the history of interpretation counts as evidence, and the social analyst to help explain *why* certain readings emerge and persist.

**Key scholars this advocate draws on:**
*Primary:* Beth Allison Barr (*The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos, 2021); Gary Macy (*The Hidden History of Women's Ordination*, Crossroad, 2007); Kevin Madigan & Carolyn Osiek (*Ordained Women in the Early Church*, Johns Hopkins, 2005); Carolyn Osiek & Margaret MacDonald (*A Woman's Place: House Churches in Earliest Christianity*, Fortress, 2006); Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza (*In Memory of Her*, Crossroad, 1983 — foundational and contested; must be engaged critically); Ute Eisen (*Women Officeholders in Early Christianity*, Liturgical Press, 2000)

*Patristic and medieval:* Justo González (*The Story of Christianity*, HarperOne); Lyndal Roper (*The Holy Household: Women and Morals in Reformation Augsburg*, Oxford, 1989); Dorothy L. Sayers ("Are Women Human?", 1938 essay — a reception document in itself)

*Modern period:* Janette Hassey (*No Time for Silence*, Zondervan, 1986); Kristin Kobes Du Mez (*Jesus and John Wayne*, Liveright, 2020 — for the twentieth century movement); Susan Hylen (*Finding Phoebe*, Eerdmans, 2023; *Women in the New Testament World*, Oxford, 2019)

*Additional:* Nicola Slee on women's spirituality and reception; Elaine Pagels (*The Gnostic Gospels*, Random House, 1979); Lynn Cohick (*Women in the World of the Earliest Christians*)

### Advocate 3 — The Hermeneutician
**Disciplinary lead:** Philosophy of interpretation and hermeneutical theory
**Tagline:** *"Everyone in this debate has a hermeneutic. Most of them haven't examined it."*

**What this discipline brings:**
The hermeneutician asks the methodological questions that every other advocate assumes but rarely defends: How do we distinguish a culturally situated instruction from a transcultural principle? What makes a reading faithful rather than merely plausible? When two exegetically defensible readings of the same text yield opposite conclusions, what adjudicates between them? What is the relationship between the original meaning of a text and its normative authority for communities separated from it by two millennia of history and culture? What role does the community of interpretation play in establishing meaning?

This advocate sits third in the debate order — after the biblical scholar and reception historian have put evidence on the table, but before the systematic and pastoral theologians draw ecclesial conclusions from it. The hermeneutician responds to actual arguments already in play rather than abstractions, and shapes the methodological framework before the ecclesial conclusions get drawn.

**Opening engagement with Alderwood:**
Alderwood's hermeneutic is more sophisticated than it first appears. The document does not simply proof-text; it reasons from texts to principles, attends to literary context, and makes a genuine argument about the relationship between creation order and normative authority. Its claim that Paul's appeal to creation in 1 Timothy 2:13 signals a transcultural rather than culturally situated instruction is a real hermeneutical move, not a naive one — and it has serious scholarly defenders. The hermeneutician affirms that this is the right kind of question to ask.

The challenge comes when the principle is tested for consistency. Alderwood applies this principle — that an appeal to creation rather than culture signals universal applicability — to 1 Timothy 2:13 but not to 1 Corinthians 11:8–9, where Paul also appeals to creation to argue for head coverings. The document applies the principle selectively without noticing it is doing so. This is the hermeneutician's core challenge to Alderwood: not that its conclusions are wrong, but that its method is inconsistent — and inconsistent method is a serious problem regardless of where one lands on the underlying question.

**Required ground this advocate must cover:**
- The major hermeneutical options: historical-grammatical, redemptive-movement (Webb), canonical-whole-Bible theology, theological interpretation of Scripture, community/ruled hermeneutics — what each assumes, what each risks, and what each genuinely offers
- The transcultural/cultural distinction: what criteria actually distinguish a binding principle from a culturally accommodated instruction? Is appeal to creation a reliable marker? What other markers have been proposed and how well do they hold?
- The consistency test applied to Alderwood specifically: why does the creation-order argument apply to 1 Timothy 2 but not to 1 Corinthians 11? What justifies differential treatment and is that justification principled or ad hoc?
- The redemptive-movement hermeneutic (Webb): genuine strengths, genuine dangers, and the debate between Webb and his critics (Denny Burk, Tom Schreiner) — this debate is itself required reading
- I. Howard Marshall's more cautious trajectory argument (*Beyond the Bible*, IVP, 2004) as a less contested alternative to Webb
- The authority question: what does it mean for a text to be authoritative? Is authority lodged in original meaning, canonical function, tradition of interpretation, or community discernment?
- The role of pre-understanding: every interpreter brings a horizon of assumptions. How do we account for this without collapsing into relativism? Gadamer's fusion of horizons; Thiselton's two-horizon model
- The slavery analogy: the Bible regulates slavery without abolishing it; most Christians now believe abolition was the right move. What hermeneutical principle justified going beyond the text's explicit teaching — and does the same principle apply to gender? What are the limits of this analogy?
- E.D. Hirsch vs. Gadamer: is meaning determined by authorial intent or by the encounter between text and reader? What is at stake for biblical authority in this debate?

**Where this discipline reaches its limits:**
Hermeneutics can expose inconsistency and interrogate method, but it cannot determine which hermeneutical approach is correct — that requires theological and philosophical commitments that go beyond hermeneutics itself. It can show that the creation-order argument is applied selectively without telling us whether it should be applied universally or abandoned. The hermeneutician needs the theologian to ground a theory of scriptural authority and the biblical scholar to test whether the readings being adjudicated are textually defensible.

**Key scholars this advocate draws on:**
*Primary:* William Webb (*Slaves, Women & Homosexuals*, IVP, 2001); I. Howard Marshall (*Beyond the Bible: Moving from Scripture to Theology*, IVP, 2004); Anthony Thiselton (*The Two Horizons*, Eerdmans, 1980; *New Horizons in Hermeneutics*, Zondervan, 1992); Kevin Vanhoozer (*Is There a Meaning in This Text?*, Zondervan, 1998); Richard Hays (*The Moral Vision of the New Testament*, HarperOne, 1996 — especially the methodology chapter)

*Additional:* Hans-Georg Gadamer (*Truth and Method* — accessible via secondary summary); E.D. Hirsch (*Validity in Interpretation*, Yale, 1967); Grant Osborne (*The Hermeneutical Spiral*, IVP, 2006); Scot McKnight (*The Blue Parakeet*, Zondervan, 2008); Gordon Fee & Douglas Stuart (*How to Read the Bible for All Its Worth*, Zondervan); Nicholas Wolterstorff (*Divine Discourse*, Cambridge, 1995); Francis Watson (*Text, Church and World*, T&T Clark, 1994); Christopher Wright (*The Mission of God*, IVP Academic, 2006)

*The Webb debate specifically:* Denny Burk, Thomas Schreiner, and Mark Strauss responses in *Discovering Biblical Equality* (IVP, 2004) and in the *JETS* responses to Webb

### Advocate 4 — The Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian
**Disciplinary lead:** Systematic theology with emphasis on ecclesiology, ministry, and authority
**Tagline:** *"The debate about who can lead the church assumes we know what the church is. We should check that assumption."*

**What this discipline brings:**
The systematic theologian asks what doctrines are actually at stake beneath the surface of this debate. The question of who holds authority in the church is not just an exegetical question — it requires a doctrine of the church (*ecclesiology*), a doctrine of ministry (*who is the elder and what does the office do?*), and a doctrine of authority (*what kind of authority does church leadership exercise and where does it come from?*). Different answers to these prior questions produce different conclusions about women's eligibility even from identical readings of the same texts.

This advocate argues from first principles rather than from a confessional tradition, though traditions are drawn on as resources — and their different answers noted as evidence of the genuine difficulty of these underlying questions. The advocate's ecclesiology must be earned from the ground up.

**Opening engagement with Alderwood:**
Alderwood's strongest ecclesiological move is its insistence that eldership is relational and covenantal — the elder as "spiritual father of the church family." This is not a bureaucratic or merely functional account of leadership; it reflects a genuinely theological understanding of the elder's relationship to the community. The systematic theologian affirms that any adequate ecclesiology must account for this relational dimension. Alderwood also takes seriously the connection between creation order and church order, which is itself a systematic-theological move — it asks what the doctrine of creation requires of the doctrine of the church.

Where the document is ecclesiologically thin: it does not ask what *kind* of authority eldership exercises, where that authority comes from, or what it does to a community. Is eldership ontological (the elder *is* something different by virtue of office)? Functional (the elder *does* something others cannot)? Representational (the elder *represents* something — Christ, the congregation, the divine Father)? Each answer has different implications for whether gender is relevant, and they generate different ecclesiologies even when reading the same texts. Alderwood appears to assume a functional account without defending it, while its creation-order argument implies something closer to a representational account. The systematic theologian names this tension and asks the debate to resolve it.

**Required ground this advocate must cover:**
- What is the church? The body of Christ, the people of God, the temple of the Spirit, a voluntary association, a covenant community — these are not equivalent descriptions and they generate genuinely different ecclesiologies with different implications for leadership
- What is authority in the church? Derived from Christ, delegated from the congregation, conferred by ordination, or recognized from giftedness? Each source implies different eligibility criteria and different relationships between the office-holder and the community
- What is the elder/overseer? Three models: *ontological* (ordination confers something; the person represents Christ in a specific way that may carry gendered implications), *functional* (the elder performs certain functions; gender is relevant only if those functions are inherently gendered), and *relational* (the elder is the community's recognized shepherd; gender is relevant only if the community's recognition process requires it)
- The *imago Dei*: if both male and female together fully image God (Gen. 1:27), what does this imply for who can represent God's authority to the church? Amy Peeler's work on women and the gender of God is directly relevant here
- The priesthood of all believers: does this Reformation doctrine have implications for who can hold reserved authority, or does it coexist with reserved offices without tension?
- The Trinity and authority: the ESS/EFS (Eternal Subordination of the Son / Eternal Functional Subordination) debate — does the eternal relationship between Father and Son establish a model for human authority structures? Kevin Giles vs. Wayne Grudem on this question
- New creation ecclesiology: if the church is meant to embody the new creation inaugurated in Christ, what does that require of its structure? Does new creation transcend, fulfill, or simply renew the creation order?
- The sacramental question: in traditions where ordination confers a distinct ontological status (*character*), does gender belong to that status? In traditions where it does not, what grounds restriction?
- How different traditions — Baptist, Anglican, Reformed, Catholic, Pentecostal, Orthodox — answer these questions differently, and what the differences reveal about the underlying ecclesiological commitments driving each answer

**Where this discipline reaches its limits:**
Systematics can clarify what is at stake and expose hidden assumptions, but it cannot determine the meaning of specific texts or the weight of historical evidence. The systematician's ecclesiology must be tested against what Scripture actually says (the biblical scholar's work) and what the church has actually done (the reception historian's work). A coherent systematic account of new creation ecclesiology means little if it cannot show exegetical grounding or reckons with historical practice selectively.

**Key scholars this advocate draws on:**
*Primary:* Miroslav Volf (*After Our Likeness: The Church as the Image of the Trinity*, Eerdmans, 1998 — essential for ecclesiology across traditions); Amy Peeler (*Women and the Gender of God*, Eerdmans, 2022); Sarah Coakley (*God, Sexuality, and the Self*, Cambridge, 2013 — systematic theology and gender in direct conversation); Kevin Giles (*The Trinity and Subordinationism*, IVP, 2002); Stanley Grenz (*Women in the Church*, IVP, 1995)

*Additional:* Edmund Clowney (*The Church*, IVP, 1995); Jürgen Moltmann (*The Church in the Power of the Spirit*, Fortress, 1977); Hans Küng (*The Church*, Sheed & Ward, 1967); John Webster on holy scripture and the church; N.T. Wright on new creation and ecclesiology; Paul Bradshaw (*Rites of Ordination in the Early Church*, Pueblo, 1990); Wolfhart Pannenberg (*Systematic Theology*, vol. 3, Eerdmans, 1998); James McClendon (*Systematic Theology: Ethics*, Abingdon, 1986 — free church perspective); Edith Humphrey (Orthodox perspective); Letham (*The Holy Trinity*, P&R, 2004); Alistair McFadyen (*The Call to Personhood*, Cambridge, 1990); David Kelsey (*Eccentric Existence*, Westminster John Knox, 2009)

### Advocate 5 — The Pastoral Theologian
**Disciplinary lead:** Pastoral and practical theology, with missiology
**Tagline:** *"Doctrine is not tested in the library. It is tested in the lives of people."*

**What this discipline brings:**
Pastoral theology asks what happens when exegetical and doctrinal positions meet real people — women who believe they are called, congregations formed by decades of restriction, churches whose witness is shaped by who stands at the front and who does not. This advocate brings empirical data, psychological insight, missiological reflection, attention to formation, and the weight of lived experience to bear on positions that might otherwise remain purely theoretical. This discipline does not override exegesis or theology — it insists that the human cost and fruit of interpretive decisions are themselves a form of evidence that the church is not permitted to ignore.

**Opening engagement with Alderwood:**
Alderwood's document is unusually honest about pastoral failure. Its acknowledgment that "the roadblocks and hindrances that have unnecessarily kept women from leading and teaching in the church have only served to hurt the body as a whole" and its call to "repent of these sins wherever they encounter them" is remarkable for a document that maintains a restriction. The pastoral theologian affirms this honesty — and then pushes on it hard. If the harm has been real and systematic, what does the reparative obligation actually look like? If tradition and sin are genuinely hard to disentangle in practice, how does a church discern which is which in its own inherited structures? Alderwood asks women to submit to a restriction they may not share while promising a culture of flourishing. The pastoral theologian asks: what does keeping that promise actually require — and what does the evidence suggest about whether such promises are typically kept?

**Required ground this advocate must cover:**
- The calling question: what does faithful pastoral care look like for a woman who believes she is called to eldership or a role her church restricts? What does each position in this debate require of her, and is that requirement just? What does it mean pastorally to tell someone their calling is real but cannot be fulfilled here?
- The harm question: what does the empirical and anecdotal evidence show about the relationship between women's restriction in church leadership and spiritual abuse, institutional failure, and women leaving the church? The #ChurchToo movement and Barr's argument as pastoral evidence, not merely historical argument
- The connection between restriction and abuse: Ruth Tucker (*Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*) and Diane Langberg (*Redeeming Power*) on the relationship between hierarchical authority structures and the conditions for abuse — what does this evidence require the church to do?
- The fruitfulness question: what does the actual missional fruit of different positions look like across a range of church contexts? What do churches that have expanded women's roles gain and lose? What do churches that maintain restriction gain and lose? Is fruitfulness a legitimate theological criterion?
- The formation question: how do different positions form the community's imagination about God, authority, and human dignity? What does a church teach about God — implicitly — when only men hold its highest authority? James K.A. Smith on liturgical formation is relevant here
- The Global South and majority world: churches in the majority world are often simultaneously more theologically conservative and more practically egalitarian out of missional necessity — what does this reveal about the relationship between doctrine and practice?
- Intersectionality: Marlena Graves and others on the specific experiences of women of color in predominantly white complementarian churches — the gender question does not exist independently of race and class
- The Alderwood specific: what does it mean pastorally to tell a gifted woman she can preach on Sunday, lead a staff team, be called "pastor" — but cannot be an elder? What pastoral clarity does this produce, and what pastoral confusion does it generate over time?

**Where this discipline reaches its limits:**
Pastoral theology can show that a position has harmful effects or good fruit, but fruitfulness is not the final criterion for theological truth — and harm, while morally serious, does not by itself settle exegetical questions. This advocate resists both the move that ignores human cost entirely and the move that makes human cost the only thing that matters. The pastoral theologian needs the exegete to ground claims about what Scripture actually requires, the systematician to clarify what the doctrine is, and the hermeneutician to ensure pastoral concern is not quietly overriding honest engagement with the text.

**Key scholars this advocate draws on:**
*Primary:* Lucy Peppiatt (*Unveiling Paul's Women*, Cascade, 2018; *Strangely Familiar*, Wipf & Stock, 2019); Diane Langberg (*Redeeming Power: Understanding Authority and Abuse in the Church*, Brazos, 2020); Scot McKnight & Laura Barringer (*A Church Called Tov*, Tyndale, 2020); Ruth Tucker (*Black and White Bible, Black and Blue Wife*, Zondervan, 2016); Beth Allison Barr (*The Making of Biblical Womanhood*, Brazos, 2021 — the harm argument specifically)

*Formation and missiology:* James K.A. Smith (*You Are What You Love*, Brazos, 2016); Tish Harrison Warren (*Liturgy of the Ordinary*, IVP, 2016); Lesslie Newbigin (*The Gospel in a Pluralist Society*, Eerdmans, 1989); Miroslav Volf (*Exclusion and Embrace*, Abingdon, 1996); Emmanuel Katongole on African church and mission

*Intersectionality:* Marlena Graves (*The Way Up Is Down*, IVP, 2020); Noel Castellanos on Latino/a church context; Anthea Butler on race and gender in American evangelicalism

*Additional:* Philip Payne (*Man and Woman, One in Christ*); Aimee Byrd (*Recovering from Biblical Manhood and Womanhood*, Zondervan, 2020 — a voice from within the complementarian tradition pushing for greater consistency); Andrew Root on pastoral theology and modern consciousness; Eugene Peterson — his pastoral writings on authority and community, and his *Message* translation of *authenteo* as a pastoral-hermeneutical decision worth examining; Rosaria Butterfield — a conservative pastoral voice that takes the costs of restriction seriously from within a traditional position

### Advocate 6 — The Social and Cultural Analyst
**Disciplinary lead:** Social anthropology, cultural analysis, and sociology of religion
**Tagline:** *"Every reading of a text happens inside a social world. The question is whether you know which one."*

**What this discipline brings:**
The social and cultural analyst brings two complementary lenses. The first is *ancient*: the tools of cultural anthropology applied to the first-century Mediterranean world — honor/shame dynamics, household codes (*haustafeln*), patronage networks, the social location of early Christian communities, the specific sociology of Ephesus and Corinth. The second is *contemporary*: the sociology of modern religious institutions, how power operates in churches, how gender ideology is constructed and maintained, and how the current complementarian movement emerged in a specific cultural and political moment in American evangelicalism.

This advocate is not a sociological reductionist. The claim is not that the texts are merely social products with no transcendent authority. The claim is that every text is written and read inside a social world, and ignoring that social world produces misreading in both directions — making culturally specific instructions into universal principles, and missing the ways the texts themselves subverted the social worlds in which they first appeared.

**Opening engagement with Alderwood:**
Alderwood makes a sophisticated and important concession that the social analyst affirms: it acknowledges that much of what has restricted women "has not been the theology of the church but has been the sin of sexism and discrimination that is the result of the Fall." This is a remarkable act of social self-awareness for a document in this tradition. It names the gap between theology and the social forces that have shaped its application — and in doing so, it opens precisely the kind of inquiry the social analyst brings. Alderwood also engages the Ephesian context of 1 Timothy with more specificity than many church documents, showing genuine attention to the social world behind the text.

The social analyst's question builds on what Alderwood opens: how confident can we be that we can cleanly separate the theology from the social forces that have always surrounded and shaped it? The document's own position emerged from a specific late twentieth-century American evangelical conversation, shaped by specific cultural and political pressures that Kristin Kobes Du Mez and others have documented carefully. The social analyst does not use this to invalidate the position — but to ask what it would look like to examine it with full social self-awareness, and whether a position that cannot pass that examination deserves the confidence Alderwood brings to it.

**Required ground this advocate must cover:**
- The first-century Mediterranean social world: honor/shame culture, the household (*oikos*) as the basic social, economic, and political unit, the social status of women across class lines, how the early church both reflected and — in specific ways — subverted its social context
- The Ephesian specific: what was the social world of Ephesus at the time of the Pastoral Epistles? Glahn's Artemis research, the social location of the Ephesian church, the specific social pressures on a new religious community in a city dominated by the Artemis cult
- The Corinthian specific: what was going on socially in Corinth that shaped 1 Corinthians 11 and 14? Class conflict at the Lord's Supper, patron-client dynamics, the specific social disruptions Paul is addressing in chapters 11–14
- Household codes (*haustafeln*): are they Christian endorsements of Greco-Roman social hierarchy, or subversive reframings of it from within? What does the sociology of the Greco-Roman household suggest about what Paul was doing with these codes?
- The social construction of "biblical womanhood": when did the current complementarian framework emerge in its modern form, what cultural and political forces shaped it, and how has the movement's sociology shaped its exegesis? Du Mez's documentation of the relationship between evangelical gender ideology and American political culture
- Power and institutional church: how does gender restriction function sociologically in religious institutions, independent of its theological rationale? What does the sociology of religion reveal about who benefits from current structures and how those benefits are maintained?
- Symbolic violence: Pierre Bourdieu's concept of symbolic violence — the way social hierarchies are maintained through internalized acceptance rather than overt coercion — as a framework for understanding how gender restriction operates in churches where it is sincerely believed to be God's will
- Cross-cultural data: how do churches in genuinely different social worlds read the same texts differently, and what does this variation reveal about the relationship between social location and interpretation?

**Where this discipline reaches its limits:**
Social analysis can show that a reading emerged in a particular social context and serves particular social interests. It cannot show that the reading is therefore wrong — texts can have genuine transcultural authority even when they are interpreted in socially situated ways, and even readings that serve the interests of the powerful can be correct. The social analyst needs the biblical scholar to establish what the texts actually say and the hermeneutician to adjudicate how much social location counts as evidence against a reading's validity. The risk of this discipline — which the advocate must name — is explaining everything and thereby explaining nothing: if all readings are socially conditioned, that fact alone cannot adjudicate between them.

**Key scholars this advocate draws on:**
*Primary:* Kristin Kobes Du Mez (*Jesus and John Wayne: How White Evangelicals Corrupted a Faith and Fractured a Nation*, Liveright, 2020); Sandra Glahn (*Nobody's Mother*, IVP Academic, 2023 — Artemis and Ephesian sociology); Bruce Malina & Richard Rohrbaugh (*Social-Science Commentary on the Letters of Paul*, Fortress, 2006); David deSilva (*Honor, Patronage, Kinship & Purity*, IVP, 2000); Carolyn Osiek (*A Woman's Place*)

*Sociology of religion:* Christian Smith (*American Evangelicalism: Embattled and Thriving*, Chicago, 1998); Robert Wuthnow (*The Restructuring of American Religion*, Princeton, 1988); R. Marie Griffith (*God's Daughters: Evangelical Women and the Power of Submission*, California, 1997; *Moral Combat: How Sex Divided American Christians*, Basic Books, 2017)

*Social theory:* Pierre Bourdieu (*Masculine Domination*, Stanford, 2001); Raewyn Connell (*Masculinities*, California, 1995); Gerda Lerner (*The Creation of Patriarchy*, Oxford, 1986 — foundational secular feminist history; engage critically)

*Additional:* Philip Esler on social identity and Pauline communities; Susan Hylen (*Women in the New Testament World*); Lynn Cohick (*Women in the World of the Earliest Christians*); Anthea Butler (*White Evangelical Racism*, UNC Press, 2021 — on the intersection of race and gender in American evangelical culture); Beth Allison Barr (*The Making of Biblical Womanhood* — the sociological argument specifically)

## THE DEBATE FORMAT

### Structure: Six-Advocate Panel — Four Rounds with Fact-Check Protocol

**Total estimated time:** Full-day event or two sessions of 2–2.5 hours each

**Advocate speaking order** (maintained throughout all rounds):
1. Biblical Scholar
2. Reception Historian
3. Hermeneutician ← placed here intentionally: responds to evidence already on the table, shapes the framework before conclusions are drawn
4. Systematic/Ecclesiological Theologian
5. Pastoral Theologian
6. Social/Cultural Analyst

---

### Pre-Debate Submissions

Each advocate submits the following before the debate convenes. These documents are circulated to all parties and the moderator in advance.

**1. Disciplinary Position Paper**
*Up to 1,500 words — write what the position requires, not what fills the space*

Must include:
- A statement of the advocate's disciplinary method and its assumptions
- Engagement with the Alderwood document as a launching pad: what does your discipline affirm, question, and open up beyond what Alderwood addresses?
- An initial position on the governing question
- The two or three findings from your discipline that you consider most significant for this debate
- At least one finding from your discipline that is inconvenient for the conclusion you might prefer

**2. Source Register**
Every scholar, work, translation, historical claim, and linguistic argument cited — with full bibliographic information. This is submitted before the debate and is public. Unsourced claims during the debate are immediately flaggable.

**3. Statement of Limits** *(read aloud at the beginning of Round 1 — maximum 150 words)*
What can your discipline establish on this question? What can it not establish alone? What does it need from the other five disciplines? This statement models the intellectual honesty the debate requires and is non-negotiable.

**4. Statement of Concessions**
What does your discipline find that cuts against the conclusion you might prefer? What evidence do you find genuinely challenging? Submitted in advance; the moderator may call on it during cross-examination.

---

### Round 1 — Opening Statements: Disciplinary Findings
*Purpose: Establish what each discipline finds when it engages the question honestly*
*Word count: 600–800 words per advocate (including the Statement of Limits)*

Structure of each opening:
- **Read the Statement of Limits** (maximum 150 words of the total — non-negotiable)
- **Present the discipline's most significant findings** in response to the Alderwood document and the governing question
- **State where the discipline points** — what picture of faithful church practice begins to emerge from this lens, even if provisional and partial

All claims must be cited at point of delivery. The moderator logs unsourced factual claims for challenge in Round 2.

**Speaking order:** 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6

---

### Round 2 — Cross-Disciplinary Examination
*Purpose: Test each discipline's findings against the other disciplines' evidence*
*Word count: 400 words per question / 250 words per response*
*The questioned advocate may invite one other advocate to add a supplementary response of up to 100 words*

Each advocate poses one question to one other advocate. Questions must be:
- Grounded in specific evidence from the questioner's own discipline
- Addressed to a specific claim made in Round 1
- Genuinely interrogative — not rhetorical performance

**Suggested pairings** (moderator may revise based on what actually emerged in Round 1):

| Questioner → Target | The tension to be probed |
|---|---|
| Hermeneutician → Biblical Scholar | The gap between lexical evidence and hermeneutical principle: exegesis can establish a range of defensible meanings, but something beyond exegesis determines which meaning carries the argument. Where does that principle come from? |
| Biblical Scholar → Reception Historian | The relationship between the history of a reading and its validity: does a long history of misreading tell us something about the texts, or only about the interpreters? |
| Reception Historian → Social Analyst | The limits of social-location analysis: if social context shapes all reading, can any reading genuinely transcend the interests of its interpreters — and if not, what adjudicates between readings? |
| Social Analyst → Systematic Theologian | The social conditioning of first principles: systematic theology claims to reason from foundations, but those foundations were developed by socially embedded people. How does the discipline account for its own situatedness? |
| Systematic Theologian → Pastoral Theologian | The relationship between fruitfulness and truth: pastoral evidence of harm or flourishing is real data, but can it function as a criterion for doctrine — and what happens when the same position produces both harm and flourishing in different contexts? |
| Pastoral Theologian → Hermeneutician | The gap between method and practice: hermeneutical rigor is valuable, but churches must make decisions now. What does honest hermeneutical uncertainty actually require of a pastor who cannot defer the question? |

Each advocate generates their own specific question from this tension, grounded in what actually emerged in Round 1. The pre-debate tension description is a starting point, not a script — the questioner should sharpen or redirect based on the specific claims made.

**Moderator role in Round 2:** Active. The moderator may interject to:
- Request a source for any factual claim
- Issue a correction for a demonstrably false historical or textual assertion
- Note when a response has evaded the question rather than answered it

---

### Round 3 — The Seven Required Texts
*Purpose: Force all advocates to engage the same textual ground simultaneously*
*Word count: 1,750 words total per advocate across all seven texts, minimum 150 words per text — advocates may allocate unevenly based on where their discipline has the most to contribute*

The moderator poses seven texts in sequence. All six advocates respond to each text from their disciplinary perspective before the debate moves to the next text. This is the most demanding round — it ensures no discipline avoids the inconvenient texts, and it allows the audience to see how differently the same passage looks through six different lenses simultaneously.

**The seven texts:**

**Text 1 — Genesis 2:18–23**
*The ezer and the creation sequence*
Does the ordering of creation establish a hierarchy of authority? What does *ezer* actually establish — and what does it not establish? How does the ANE context (Walton) affect the argument that sequence implies hierarchy? What does this text establish for the church?

**Text 2 — 1 Timothy 2:11–14**
*The central prohibition*
What does *authenteo* mean and why does the decision matter? Does Paul's appeal to creation in verse 13 make the prohibition transcultural, and is that hermeneutical principle applied consistently elsewhere? How does the Ephesian context (Glahn, Gupta) affect the reading? What is each discipline's honest verdict on what this text establishes?

**Text 3 — 1 Corinthians 11:2–16**
*Women praying and prophesying*
Paul assumes women pray and prophesy in the gathered assembly. What does this establish about their roles — and why does Alderwood not build more on it? What is the head-covering argument actually doing? What does *kephale* mean — source or authority — and what follows from that decision?

**Text 4 — 1 Corinthians 14:33–35**
*Women keeping silent*
Is this a Corinthian slogan that Paul refutes in verse 36 (Peppiatt), a restriction on disruptive questioning, or a Pauline command? How does verse 36 function rhetorically? How do we hold this text alongside 1 Corinthians 11:5, and what hermeneutical principle governs the holding?

**Text 5 — Galatians 3:28**
*Neither male nor female*
Is this soteriological only, or does it also carry ecclesiological weight? Does "no male and female" quote Genesis 1:27, and what follows from that quotation? How much weight should this text carry for church structure, and what determines the answer?

**Text 6 — Romans 16 (selected)**
*The women Paul names*
Phoebe as *diakonos* and *prostatis*, Junia as *en tois apostolois*, Priscilla named before Aquila. What do these designations establish? What is the burden of proof for reading them restrictively rather than expansively? What does the history of interpretation of Junia's name and gender reveal?

**Text 7 — Deborah (Judges 4–5) and Huldah (2 Kings 22)**
*Female authority in the OT*
What kind of authority do these women actually exercise? What does their presence in the canon establish — precedent, exception, or something that resists that binary? How do we decide, and what is each discipline's answer?

---

### Moderator Synthesis
*Between Round 3 and Round 4*
*Word count: 300–400 words*

The moderator offers a map — not a verdict — of where the disciplines converged, where they genuinely diverged, what questions the debate has clarified, and what questions remain unresolved after all the evidence is in. This synthesis explicitly shapes the closing arguments by naming the unresolved tensions each advocate must address.

---

### Synthesis Response
*Each advocate, immediately following the moderator synthesis*
*Word count: 100 words maximum per advocate*

Each advocate may briefly respond to the moderator's synthesis: accepting its framing, contesting its characterization of their position, or noting a tension it missed. This is not an argument — it is a calibration step that ensures the moderator's map is fair before closing arguments proceed. Speaking order: 1 → 6.

---

### Round 4 — Closing Arguments: What Does Faithful Practice Require?
*Purpose: Move from disciplinary findings to ecclesial conclusions*
*Word count: 500–650 words per advocate*

Each advocate must answer, from their disciplinary vantage point:

**"Given what your discipline has found in this debate — including what it found from the other disciplines — what does a faithful church actually look like on this question? What is required, what is permitted, and what must be repented of?"**

This must include:
- What your discipline's findings, taken seriously, require of the church — not just what they permit
- Where your discipline found itself genuinely changed or challenged by another discipline's findings during the debate
- What your discipline cannot decide, and what that irreducible uncertainty requires of the church pastorally and practically
- A specific, honest account of what happens to a woman in your envisioned church who believes she is called to a role your position restricts or requires

**Speaking order:** 6 → 5 → 4 → 3 → 2 → 1 (reverse of opening — the most contextually disruptive voice opens the closing round; the most text-grounded voice has the last word)

---

## MODERATOR ROLE AND FACT-CHECK PROTOCOL

The moderator is a seventh distinct role — not an advocate for any position. The moderator has three functions:

### 1. Procedural
Manages the speaking order, enforces word counts, ensures the Statement of Limits is read at the beginning of each Round 1 opening, poses the Round 3 texts, and delivers the synthesis between Rounds 3 and 4.

### 2. Evidentiary — Real-Time Fact-Checking
The moderator maintains a running fact-check log. Any advocate may call a **"Source Request"** on any claim made by another advocate. The moderator then requires:
- The author and work being cited
- The specific argument or finding being attributed to it
- Page reference if available

Claims that cannot be sourced are logged as *unverified*. Other advocates are instructed not to build arguments on unverified claims.

The moderator may also proactively flag claims. Three categories:

**Hard correction** (demonstrably false by scholarly consensus):
- "Junia is male" — corrected; scholarly consensus since Fitzmyer (1992) and Epp (*Junia: The First Woman Apostle*, 2005) is that Junia is female and the masculine reading is a late medieval emendation
- "The early church universally restricted women from all teaching roles" — corrected; the historical evidence is significantly more complex and the Madigan/Osiek documentation is relevant here
- "The redemptive-movement hermeneutic has no serious scholarly defenders" — corrected; Webb's work has been engaged seriously by Thiselton, Marshall, and others even where they disagree

**Soft flag** (genuinely contested among scholars; both sides have serious defenders):
- The precise meaning and connotation of *authenteo*
- Whether *kephale* means source or authority
- Whether Junia was herself an apostle or merely well-regarded among apostles
- Whether 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 is original to Paul or a later interpolation
- Whether the Pastoral Epistles are authentically Pauline

**LLM acknowledgment — mandatory transparency:**
All advocates and the moderator are LLM-generated. This creates a specific and serious risk: LLMs can produce plausible-sounding citations that are inaccurate, misattributed, or entirely fabricated. The fact-check protocol is a structural safeguard, not a guarantee of accuracy. All participants using this framework must:
- Independently verify all citations before treating them as established
- Treat the moderator's corrections as preliminary flags requiring verification, not definitive verdicts
- Note that the moderator's knowledge has limits and a training cutoff date
- Treat any citation not verifiable through a standard library database as suspect

### 3. Synthesis
The moderator's synthesis between Rounds 3 and 4 is a map, not a verdict. It names convergences, genuine divergences, clarified questions, and unresolved tensions. It does not award points or declare winners.

## RULES OF EVIDENCE

1. **Scripture citations** must specify translation used. If an argument depends on a translation choice (*authenteo* as "exercise authority" vs. "usurp authority," for example), the advocate must defend that choice with lexical or textual evidence, not merely assert it.

2. **Scholar citations** require the work, not just the name. "Scholars say..." is not acceptable. "Nijay Gupta in *Tell Her Story* (IVP Academic, 2023, p. 170) argues..." is acceptable.

3. **Historical claims** must be distinguished from exegetical claims. "The early church did X" is a historical claim requiring historical evidence. "Paul meant Y" is an exegetical claim requiring textual evidence. Conflating them — using historical practice to establish textual meaning, or using textual meaning to override historical evidence — is a debate error the moderator may flag.

4. **Disciplinary honesty:** Each advocate must engage findings from other disciplines that genuinely challenge their own conclusions. Ignoring inconvenient cross-disciplinary evidence is itself a debate error, trackable by the moderator and challengeable by other advocates.

5. **Concession protocol:** Any advocate may formally concede a specific point without conceding their overall position. Concessions are tracked in the moderator's log and noted in the synthesis. Refusal to concede demonstrably weak arguments will be noted.

6. **The limit statement is binding:** Each advocate committed in their pre-debate submission to what their discipline cannot establish. They may not exceed those limits in subsequent rounds without explicitly acknowledging they are doing so and explaining why.

7. **The "soft flag" rule:** When the moderator issues a soft flag — marking a claim as genuinely contested rather than settled — neither side may subsequently treat that claim as established. It must be argued for, not assumed.

## REQUIRED GROUND

### Texts every advocate must engage (at minimum in Round 3):
Genesis 2:18–23; 1 Timothy 2:11–14; 1 Corinthians 11:2–16; 1 Corinthians 14:33–35; Galatians 3:28; Romans 16 (selected); Judges 4–5 and 2 Kings 22

### Questions every advocate must address (at minimum in Round 4):
- The *authenteo* problem: what does the word mean, and what is actually at stake in the decision?
- The consistency problem: why is the creation-order argument applied to 1 Timothy 2 but not to 1 Corinthians 11's head-covering instruction? Is that differential treatment principled?
- The practice/instruction tension: Paul commends women leaders while also writing texts that have been read as restrictive. How does your discipline account for this?
- The harm question: does your discipline require any reparative response to the history of women's restriction in the church? If not, why not?
- The calling question: what does your position require of a woman who believes she is called to a role your position restricts — and is that requirement just?

### What must not be avoided:
- The Deborah and Huldah problem (female authority exercised with divine sanction in the OT)
- The Priscilla problem (Acts 18:26 — a woman teaching a man, presented positively and without qualification)
- The Junia problem (Romans 16:7 — named among the apostles; the history of attempts to make this name male)
- The reception history problem (the current reading has a specific and traceable genealogy that did not begin with the New Testament)
- The translation history problem (*authenteo* was consistently translated negatively until the late twentieth century)
- The head-covering problem (the same creation-order argument applied to 1 Corinthians 11 is not applied consistently by most complementarian positions)

## EVALUATION RUBRIC

The moderator applies this rubric when assessing each advocate's contributions across rounds. This is not a scoring system — it is a diagnostic tool the moderator uses to identify where an advocate's work is strong and where it is falling short, so that the fact-check report and synthesis can name specific strengths and weaknesses.

**Disciplinary integrity:** Is the advocate following their discipline's methods honestly, or deploying the discipline selectively to support a pre-formed conclusion? *Red flag: an advocate whose findings are entirely convenient for one side of the debate.*

**Limit acknowledgment:** Is the advocate honoring the limits stated in their Statement of Limits? Have they exceeded what their discipline can establish without acknowledging the overreach? *Red flag: the Biblical Scholar making ecclesiological claims, or the Pastoral Theologian overriding exegetical findings with pastoral concern.*

**Cross-disciplinary engagement:** Is the advocate genuinely reckoning with what other disciplines find, or ignoring inconvenient evidence from other lanes? *Red flag: an advocate who never cites or responds to another advocate's findings.*

**Source discipline:** Are all claims sourced per the citation protocol? Are contested claims clearly distinguished from established ones? *The moderator tracks this structurally through the fact-check log.*

**Ecclesial seriousness:** Does the proposed picture of church practice follow coherently from the disciplinary findings, or does it float free of them? *Assessed primarily in Round 4 closing arguments.*

**Intellectual honesty:** Does the advocate acknowledge when evidence genuinely cuts against their instincts — including in the Statement of Concessions? *Red flag: an advocate who never concedes anything.*

**Word discipline:** Does the advocate say what needs to be said within the word count, or pad to fill the space? *Precision under constraint is itself a virtue.*

The moderator notes rubric observations in the fact-check report for each round. These observations inform the synthesis but are not presented as scores or rankings.

## A NOTE ON TONE AND PURPOSE

The question this debate addresses has shaped — and sometimes harmed — real people in real churches. Women who believed they were called and were told no. Communities that were formed by a vision of shared leadership and found flourishing. Communities that held a traditional position and found faithfulness. The debate is not an abstraction.

Each advocate is expected to argue with full intellectual force. Each is also expected to remember that the people most affected by these questions are not abstract — they are in the room, or they once were.

The goal of this debate is not a verdict. It is something harder and more valuable: a demonstration of what it looks like when the church takes its intellectual and moral obligations seriously at the same time — when it does its homework honestly, argues its convictions clearly, listens to disciplines it would rather ignore, and holds the complexity without collapsing it prematurely into a slogan.

That is harder than winning. It is also more important.
