# Persona Impact Assessment

**Date:** 2026-03-31
**Auditor:** Claude Opus 4.6
**Scope:** Dimension 9 of the Post-Debate Audit Plan

---

## Persona 1: Woman at Alderwood Who Believes She May Be Called

**Profile:** A member of Alderwood Community Church. She takes the church's authority seriously, loves her community, and hasn't left -- but she has a persistent sense that she is called to teach or preach in ways the church's position forecloses. She is not an academic. She has read the Alderwood document and accepts its sincerity but not its conclusion.

### What the Debate Gave Her

- **Language for her experience.** The Biblical Scholar's R4 closing addresses her directly: "She is not told 'the Bible clearly forbids this.' She is told 'we are reading the Bible as carefully as we can, and honest scholars disagree.'" The Pastoral Theologian's treatment of calling as having "a shape, a direction, a sense of fit between person and role that cannot be redirected without pastoral cost" names something she has likely felt but not articulated.
- **Validation that the restriction's basis is genuinely contested**, not settled. Every advocate converged on this point.
- **Exact language a church could use to address her honestly.** The Hermeneutician's R4: "We cannot tell you with certainty that Scripture prohibits what you believe God is calling you to."
- **A theological tool, not just an emotional appeal.** The Systematician's argument that her church's own theology (imago Dei requiring male-and-female-together) creates tension with the restriction.

### What the Debate Failed to Give Her

- **A clear answer.** The debate explicitly refuses to resolve the question, which may feel like another form of being told to wait.
- **A complementarian voice that takes her calling seriously from within the complementarian framework.** No advocate modeled what a complementarian pastor would say to her that is both honest and pastorally warm.
- **Practical next steps.** She learns the basis is contested, but what does she do with that information inside a church that still restricts? The debate does not address whether she should stay, leave, or advocate for change.
- **Full emotional and spiritual address.** The Pastoral Theologian speaks to her situation more directly than any other voice, but even that voice does not fully address the cost of remaining in an honest-but-still-restricting community.

### Rating: Somewhat Informed (Medium-High)

She gains language, theological grounding for her intuition, and a framework for demanding honesty from her church. She does not gain resolution or practical guidance. The needle moves because the debate equips her to name what she needs from her community, even if it does not give her what she ultimately wants.

### One Specific Improvement

Add a structured section -- perhaps from the Pastoral Theologian -- that directly addresses the practical decision: "If you are in a church that restricts honestly, with disclosed uncertainty and genuine counter-formative practices, here is what faithful discernment looks like. If you are in a church that restricts with false certainty, here is what you owe yourself."

### Reliability Flag

This simulation is shaped by the fact that the model is representing a person with strong experiential knowledge it does not possess. The emotional and spiritual weight of vocational calling is something the model can describe but not calibrate. The assessment may overweight intellectual satisfaction and underweight the pastoral inadequacy of "the basis is contested" as an answer to a woman who believes God is calling her.

## Persona 2: Male Alderwood Congregant, Young Daughters, Conservative Upbringing

**Profile:** Mid-30s, married, two or three young daughters. Grew up in a conservative evangelical context where complementarianism was assumed, not argued. Not trained in theology or biblical studies. He is not looking to confirm a conclusion; he is trying to figure out where the weight of the evidence actually falls.

### What the Debate Gave Him

- **The Hermeneutician's three-option framework** (accept both creation-order texts as transcultural, argue textual differentiation, or name the real operative principle) is accessible without theological training and is the most clarifying single move in the debate for a non-expert.
- **The Pastoral Theologian's formation question** -- "What are your children learning about who God is and who gets to speak for God?" -- lands directly on his situation and does not require specialist vocabulary.
- **Genuine clarity on WHY the question is hard:** the same argumentative structure (creation-order grounding) appears in two texts, and virtually everyone treats them differently. A non-expert can grasp this.
- **A question he can carry into a conversation with his pastor:** "If creation-order grounding makes 1 Timothy 2 transcultural, why doesn't it make 1 Corinthians 11 (head coverings) transcultural?"

### What the Debate Failed to Give Him

- **Consistent accessibility.** The Systematician's imago Dei argument, the ESS/EFS discussion, and the Reception Historian's patristic material require theological literacy he likely does not have. He will lose the thread in significant portions of the debate.
- **A voice that sounds like someone from his tradition.** The debate lacks a warm, pastoral complementarian voice that models how his own upbringing's convictions can be held honestly.
- **A clear framework for what to tell his daughters.** He gets diagnostic tools, not parenting language.

### Rating: Somewhat Informed

The consistency challenge and the formation question are genuinely clarifying and will change how he thinks about the issue. But the debate's register is academic enough in places to lose him, and it does not provide the reassurance he needs that engaging this question is safe within his tradition.

### One Specific Improvement

Include a plain-language summary section at the end of each round -- three to five sentences, no technical vocabulary -- that states what was established, what remains open, and what a non-specialist should take away. The Round Digests approach this but are still written at a specialist level.

### Reliability Flag

This simulation assumes a level of intellectual curiosity that may overestimate a typical congregant's willingness to engage a multi-round academic debate. The more likely scenario is that he reads the final summary and the Pastoral Theologian's closing argument, if anything.

## Persona 3: Theologically Serious Christian Egalitarian

**Profile:** Familiar with the standard egalitarian arguments (Galatians 3:28, Junia, Phoebe, redemptive-movement hermeneutic, *authenteo* debate) and has read some scholarship. Believes the complementarian position is exegetically indefensible. Comes in with a thesis and will test whether the debate engages the strongest version of her arguments.

### What the Debate Gave Her

- **The imago Dei internal coherence argument** is genuinely novel in its application to Alderwood's specific text. Most egalitarian scholarship argues from external premises; the Systematician argues from Alderwood's own stated theology.
- **The recognition-and-containment pattern** (Reception Historian) is more precise than most egalitarian treatments of patristic evidence and gives her a more accurate historical narrative.
- **The Vulgate *dominari* finding** reframes the tradition question in a way she may not have encountered.
- **The Social Analyst's Bourdieu self-correction** (symmetrical application) models intellectual honesty she can use when engaging complementarian interlocutors.

### What the Debate Failed to Give Her

- **Additive biblical scholarship.** The Biblical Scholar's treatment of Junia and Phoebe does not advance beyond what Bauckham, Gupta, and Epp have already established. For a reader of those scholars, the coverage is competent but not new.
- **A genuine interlocutor for her position.** There is no complementarian voice that seriously challenges the egalitarian reading. The debate's "inconvenient findings" are honest but not sufficient to constitute a serious complementarian challenge.
- **Sustained treatment of Galatians 3:28** as a programmatic theological statement -- a significant gap for egalitarian scholarship.

### Rating: Somewhat Informed

The imago Dei argument and the recognition-and-containment finding are genuine contributions. But the debate largely confirms what she already knows rather than challenging or extending it. The absence of a strong complementarian interlocutor limits value.

### One Specific Improvement

Add a seventh advocate -- a theologically serious complementarian voice -- who engages the egalitarian position at its strongest and attempts to supply the principled distinction the Hermeneutician demands.

### Reliability Flag

This simulation may underestimate how much the cross-disciplinary integration itself is valued by a theologically serious reader, even when individual disciplinary findings are not novel. The architecture of the argument may be more valuable than the model credits.

## Persona 4: Intellectually Honest Complementarian Pastor

**Profile:** Confident in the complementarian position but not defensive. Has read Grudem and Schreiner. Genuinely interested in engaging the strongest objections to understand them, not to defeat them rhetorically.

### What the Debate Gave Him

- **The Hermeneutician's consistency challenge** is the single most important finding. If he is honest, he has likely felt the 1 Tim 2 / 1 Cor 11 tension but has not had it stated this precisely with the three-option framework. The debate forces him to name which option he holds.
- **The Systematician's imago Dei argument**, drawn from Alderwood's own text, is a challenge he must answer -- an internal tension, not an external attack.
- **The Reception Historian's Chrysostom finding** gives him a more honest historical narrative than either "the church always agreed with me" or "the church was always wrong."
- **The debate's explicit acknowledgment that the egalitarian position is also methodologically vulnerable** (e.g., canonical tiebreaker without disclosed controls) treats his position with seriousness.

### What the Debate Failed to Give Him

- **A complementarian position he would fully recognize.** No advocate articulates the complementarian case at its best. He would feel his position was analyzed but not represented.
- **Serious engagement with the consistency challenge from the complementarian side.** Schreiner's argument that the scope of instruction in 1 Tim 2 differs from 1 Cor 11 (teaching office vs. propriety) is absent from the debate.
- **Non-ESS complementarian theology.** A pastor who does not hold ESS (and many do not) would want the debate to engage non-ESS complementarian theology more fully.

### Rating: Somewhat Informed (Low-Medium)

The consistency challenge and the imago Dei tension are genuine provocations an honest complementarian must engage. But the absence of a strong complementarian voice means he is more likely to feel the debate was conducted about his position rather than with it.

### One Specific Improvement

The Hermeneutician's Option 2 -- that the creation-order appeals function differently in each passage -- should be developed seriously, not merely listed as a possibility. This is where the best complementarian scholarship operates.

### Reliability Flag

This simulation carries significant risk of model bias. The model is more likely to generate egalitarian-leaning analysis than to accurately represent the internal logic of a complementarian conviction held with intellectual seriousness. The finding that his position is "analyzed but not represented" should be weighted heavily.

## Persona 5: Undecided Church Leadership Team

**Profile:** A pastoral staff or elder team that has avoided the question or has a temporary informal position but knows they need to decide formally. They are practitioners, not academics. They need to land somewhere institutional.

### What the Debate Gave Them

- **A clear map of settled vs. contested.** The Moderator Synthesis between R3 and R4 is the single most useful document: six convergences and two irreconcilable tensions. This is decision-relevant architecture.
- **A concrete first step regardless of conclusion:** tell the congregation the basis is contested. The R4 convergence on disclosure obligation gives them an immediate action.
- **The Hermeneutician's three-option framework** as a diagnostic for their own position: which option are we holding, and can we defend it?
- **The Pastoral Theologian's counter-formative practice demand** as a concrete implementation requirement if they maintain restriction.

### What the Debate Failed to Give Them

- **A recommendation framework.** The debate produces diagnostic tools but not a decision matrix. They need: "If you conclude X, here are the minimum requirements. If you conclude Y, here are the minimum requirements."
- **Cost-benefit analysis of all positions.** The debate addresses the costs of restriction extensively but barely addresses the costs (congregational division, denominational consequences, theological risk) of opening.
- **Implementation guidance.** What does the process look like for either direction?

### Rating: Clarifying

The debate moves them from "we don't know what the arguments are" to "we understand the terrain and the minimum honesty requirements." It does not move them to decision-ready because it lacks a recommendation framework, cost analysis of all options, and implementation guidance.

### One Specific Improvement

Add a structured "Decision Framework" as a synthesis output: what is settled, what must be decided by conviction, minimum requirements for holding either position with integrity, and implementation considerations for each path.

### Reliability Flag

This simulation may underestimate the political dynamics within a leadership team. The debate assumes good-faith engagement; a team may include members not open to revisiting the position, and the debate provides no tools for navigating that internal dynamic.

## Cross-Persona Pattern Observations

Five patterns emerge across all five personas:

**1. The missing complementarian voice is the single most consequential structural weakness.** Every persona -- including the egalitarian -- would be better served by a seventh advocate who articulates the best complementarian case. The debate analyzes complementarianism but does not represent it.

**2. The debate is strongest at diagnosis and weakest at prescription.** Every persona receives a clearer picture of the problem. No persona receives practical guidance for what to do with that clarity. The gap between "now you understand the terrain" and "now you can act" is not closed for anyone.

**3. The debate's register is calibrated for Persona 3** (theologically serious egalitarian) **and partially accessible to Persona 4** (complementarian pastor). It is intermittently accessible to Persona 2 (non-expert congregant) and Persona 5 (leadership team). It is emotionally resonant for Persona 1 in the R4 "woman in the room" sections but not consistently accessible across the full debate.

**4. The R4 "woman in the room" component is the debate's highest-value pastoral innovation.** It forces every discipline to move from abstraction to consequence. It is the section most valuable to Persona 1 and most convicting for Persona 4. But all six advocates' answers converge so tightly that they read as variations of the same answer rather than genuinely different disciplinary perspectives.

**5. The debate produces a unanimous practical demand** -- disclose the contested basis to those affected -- **that functions as a minimum-viable-honesty threshold** for any church holding restriction. This is the most actionable output and is valuable to all five personas, though insufficient as a complete response for any of them.

### Who Is This Debate Actually For?

The pattern of impact reveals the debate is most valuable for **theologically literate readers who have already encountered the basic arguments and want to see them tested across disciplines** -- Personas 3 and 4. It is secondarily valuable for **institutional decision-makers** (Persona 5) who need a map of the terrain. It is least equipped to serve **non-specialist congregants** (Personas 1 and 2), who need more accessible language and more practical guidance.

This partially matches the intended audience (the debate system was designed for rigorous scholarly inquiry), but the inclusion of the persona assessment in the audit plan suggests the designers hoped for broader impact. Achieving that impact requires either additional output formats (plain-language summaries, decision frameworks) or additional advocates (a complementarian voice, a pastoral counselor focused on practical guidance).
