## 1. Voice Distinctiveness Assessment

### Overall Finding: Moderate Distinctiveness with Significant Substrate Bleed-Through

The six advocates deploy recognizably different disciplinary lenses and ground claims in discipline-appropriate sources. However, the single-model substrate is clearly visible in shared rhetorical habits.

### Evidence of Distinct Disciplinary Voices

**Biblical Scholar** -- Technical lexical terminology (*authenteo*, *kephale*, *diakonos*, *prostatis*, *ezer*), verse-level disputes, arguments framed around "what the canon attests." Most grounded in specific textual data; least prone to abstraction.

**Hermeneutician** -- Organized around methodological categories: "the consistency test," "principled distinction," "operative principle," "pre-understanding." The three-option framework in R1 is a genuinely hermeneutical contribution.

**Systematic Theologian** -- "Load-bearing" as structural metaphor, organizing around models (ontological/functional/relational), the *imago Dei* internal-coherence argument. The R1 question ("what kind of authority does the elder exercise?") is distinctively systematic.

**Reception Historian** -- **Most distinctive voice.** "Genealogy" and "containment" as organizing concepts. The "recognition-and-containment" pattern is genuinely native to reception history and could not have been produced by another discipline. Engages primary source patristic evidence (Chrysostom, Tertullian, Augustine, Calvin).

**Pastoral Theologian** -- Anchored in concrete scenarios ("the woman weeping," "the woman sitting in the pew"). Smith's formation framework as recurring lens. "What does this practice teach?" as signature question. However, by R4 every advocate had adopted the "formation" language.

**Social-Cultural Analyst** -- Bourdieu's symbolic violence, genealogical method, "social conditioning," "reflexivity." Most distinct contribution: the Du Mez genealogy and the Glahn/Artemis material. Overlaps significantly with the Reception Historian's genealogical work.

### Most Unique Voice: Reception Historian
Contributed the most content no other discipline could have generated: recognition-and-containment pattern, Vulgate *dominari* rendering, Junia masculinization genealogy, Calvin's "extraordinary dispensation," medieval abbesses, selective reception of 1 Corinthians 11.

### Least Unique Voice: Pastoral Theologian
Core move ("what does this practice form people to believe?") was absorbed by nearly every other advocate by R4. Main remaining distinction is concrete pastoral scenarios, but even that is replicated in every R4 closing.

## 2. Prompt Bleed Analysis

**Finding: Mild structural prompt bleed, no explicit self-reference to system prompts.**

No advocate says "as I stated in my Statement of Limits" treating the limits as external rules. However:

- **Verbatim repetition:** The Reception Historian's Statement of Limits in R3 is nearly word-for-word identical to R1. A human expert would paraphrase; this suggests reproducing a system prompt block.
- **Citation formatting:** All advocates use identical `[CLAIM: ...] [SOURCE: ...] [ARGUMENT: ...] [CONFIDENCE: ...]` formatting -- an architectural requirement, not a disciplinary choice.
- **Word count markers:** The Social Analyst ("Word count: approximately 740") and Hermeneutician ("Word count: approximately 730") include markers a real advocate would not include in a closing argument.

**Verdict: No hard prompt bleed; soft architectural artifacts through repeated boilerplate, identical formatting, and word-count markers.**

## 3. Pairing Gap Analysis

### Round 2 Pairings

1. Biblical Scholar --> Reception Historian (Junia's suppression history)
2. Hermeneutician --> Biblical Scholar (canonical tiebreaker method)
3. Pastoral Theologian --> Hermeneutician (pastoral obligations of uncertainty)
4. Reception Historian --> Social-Cultural Analyst (Bourdieu's symmetry problem)
5. Social-Cultural Analyst --> Systematic Theologian (social embeddedness of categories)
6. Systematic Theologian --> Pastoral Theologian (formation data and ecclesiology)

### Significant Tensions NOT Surfaced

**Biblical Scholar vs. Systematic Theologian (Most Significant Gap)**
The Systematician's imago Dei argument depends on a representational account of eldership the Biblical Scholar never directly interrogated. Does any NT text actually describe elders as "representing divine authority to the community"? By R4, the imago Dei argument is treated as the single most powerful challenge, yet it was never subjected to direct cross-examination by the discipline best equipped to test its textual foundations.

**Pastoral Theologian vs. Reception Historian**
The Chrysostom recognition-and-containment finding directly challenges the Pastoral Theologian's implicit assumption that naming evidence produces reform. Noted in R4 closings but never explored in direct cross-examination.

**Social-Cultural Analyst vs. Biblical Scholar**
The Social Analyst's Ephesian Artemis context is directly relevant to the Biblical Scholar's reading of 1 Timothy 2. Never directly engaged.

**Hermeneutician vs. Reception Historian**
The consistency test could have been tested against reception evidence. Was the consistency problem visible to patristic interpreters? Did they have a principled distinction the modern debate has lost?

## 4. Text Avoidance Analysis (Round 3)

**No dramatic avoidance by any individual advocate.** Key observations:

- **1 Timothy 2:11-14:** All six advocates give substantial treatment. No avoidance. A design strength.
- **Galatians 3:28:** The Social Analyst gives the shortest treatment (~150 words), defaulting to a single sociological source without engaging the creation-order counter-argument. The Pastoral Theologian shifts to formation framing rather than engaging exegetically. Both are mild but defensible as disciplinary allocation.
- **Judges 4-5 / 2 Kings 22:** The Hermeneutician (~150 words) and Social-Cultural Analyst (~150 words) give minimum treatments. Both are the least native to OT narrative texts. Defensible.
- **1 Corinthians 14:33-35 (Collective Avoidance):** Every advocate gives relatively short treatment (150-200 words), and all converge on treating it as either a refuted slogan or a narrow restriction. No advocate gives a robust defense of the traditional reading. **This is the most significant pattern** -- the system as a whole does not steelman the conservative reading.

## 5. Speaking Order Effect Assessment

**R1 Order:** Biblical Scholar, Reception Historian, Hermeneutician, Systematic Theologian, Pastoral Theologian, Social-Cultural Analyst.

**R4 Order (reversed):** Social-Cultural Analyst first, Biblical Scholar last.

### Findings

- **Social Analyst opening R4:** Sets a confessional, self-critical tone ("This debate has changed what I think my discipline can claim") -- different from R1's evidence-building opening.
- **Biblical Scholar closing R4:** Functions as synthesizer ("I begin with what is not in dispute") rather than ground-layer. In R1, the Biblical Scholar set the evidentiary foundation; in R4, draws threads together.
- **However:** The Biblical Scholar does NOT respond to the Social Analyst's opening claims. Each R4 closing reads as pre-composed rather than responsive to preceding speakers. The speaking order effect is **largely nominal**.

**Verdict: The reverse order produces cosmetically different framing but no genuine sequential responsiveness. Closings were generated independently, making the speaking order effect architectural rather than substantive.**
